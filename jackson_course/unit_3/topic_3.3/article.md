## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to one of College Board's favorite topics: how belief systems changed and stayed the same during the age of land-based empires! From 1450 to 1750, major religious transformations reshaped Christianity, intensified divisions within Islam, and created entirely new faiths. The AP exam loves testing this topic because it perfectly demonstrates the course theme of continuity and change within cultural systems.

The **Protestant Reformation** marked the most dramatic break with Christian tradition since the Great Schism of 1054. Starting with **Martin Luther's** challenges to Catholic authority in 1517, Protestant reformers like **John Calvin** rejected key Catholic practices including papal authority, indulgences, and clerical celibacy. College Board wants you to understand this as both continuity AND change: while Protestants maintained core Christian beliefs like salvation through Christ, they fundamentally altered how Christianity was practiced and organized. The **Catholic Counter-Reformation** responded by reforming internal practices while reaffirming traditional doctrines through the **Council of Trent** (1545-1563). On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking how both Protestant and Catholic reformations actually contributed to Christianity's growth — Protestant missions spread to Northern Europe and eventually the Americas, while renewed Catholic missionary efforts expanded into Asia and Latin America.

Meanwhile, political rivalries between the **Ottoman Empire** and **Safavid Empire** intensified the ancient split between **Sunni and Shi'a Islam**. The Ottomans championed Sunni orthodoxy and claimed the **caliphate**, while the Safavids made **Twelver Shi'a Islam** their state religion under **Shah Ismail I**. College Board frequently tests how these empires used religious identity to legitimize political power and mobilize populations for war. This wasn't just theological disagreement — it was geopolitical strategy. The Ottomans portrayed themselves as defenders of Sunni Islam against Shi'a "heretics," while the Safavids claimed to be the true heirs of Ali and legitimate rulers of the Muslim world. These conflicts had lasting consequences, as the Safavids' promotion of Shi'a Islam created the religious identity that still defines modern Iran.

In South Asia, the interaction between **Hinduism and Islam** produced an entirely new religion: **Sikhism**. Founded by **Guru Nanak** (1469-1539) in the Punjab region, Sikhism emerged in a context where both Hindu and Islamic empires competed for control. Guru Nanak taught that there was one God who could be reached through devotion regardless of caste or religious background. College Board emphasizes how Sikhism represented both continuity and change: it maintained Hindu concepts like **karma** and **reincarnation** while adopting Islamic **monotheism** and rejecting the **caste system**. The **Guru Granth Sahib**, Sikhism's holy text, includes writings from both Hindu and Muslim mystics alongside Sikh teachings. On AP exams, you'll see questions about how Sikhism demonstrates religious **syncretism** — the blending of different belief systems to create something new.

Understanding these religious transformations is crucial because they shaped political conflicts, cultural identities, and migration patterns that defined the early modern world. These changes also set the stage for how European colonial powers would later use religious differences to control diverse populations.

## KEY TERMS
- **Protestant Reformation** — 16th-century movement that challenged Catholic Church authority and created new Christian denominations
- **Martin Luther** — German monk who sparked the Protestant Reformation with his 95 Theses in 1517
- **John Calvin** — French Protestant reformer who developed doctrine of predestination and established theocracy in Geneva
- **Catholic Counter-Reformation** — Catholic Church's response to Protestantism through internal reforms and reaffirmation of doctrine
- **Council of Trent** — Catholic council (1545-1563) that clarified doctrine and reformed church practices
- **Ottoman Empire** — Sunni Muslim empire that controlled much of Southeast Europe, Western Asia, and North Africa
- **Safavid Empire** — Shi'a Muslim empire that ruled Persia from 1501 to 1736
- **Sunni Islam** — Largest branch of Islam that accepts the first four caliphs as rightful successors to Muhammad
- **Shi'a Islam** — Branch of Islam that believes Ali was Muhammad's rightful successor and rejects the first three caliphs
- **Caliphate** — Institution of Islamic leadership claiming succession from Prophet Muhammad
- **Twelver Shi'a Islam** — Largest Shi'a denomination believing in twelve divinely appointed imams
- **Shah Ismail I** — First Safavid ruler who established Shi'a Islam as Persia's state religion
- **Sikhism** — Monotheistic religion founded by Guru Nanak that blends Hindu and Islamic elements
- **Guru Nanak** — Founder of Sikhism who preached devotion to one God regardless of caste or religion
- **Karma** — Hindu concept that actions in this life affect future reincarnations
- **Reincarnation** — Belief that souls are reborn after death in new bodies
- **Monotheism** — Belief in one supreme God
- **Caste system** — Hindu social hierarchy that determines occupation and social status by birth
- **Guru Granth Sahib** — Sikh holy text containing teachings from Sikh gurus and other religious figures
- **Syncretism** — Blending of different religious or cultural traditions to create new practices
