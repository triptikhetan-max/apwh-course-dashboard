## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to one of College Board's favorite topics: how empires actually stay in power! Between 1450-1750, massive land-based empires like the Ottomans, Mughals, Ming/Qing China, and European monarchies faced the same challenge — how do you control millions of people across vast territories? The answer lies in understanding the specific administrative methods these empires used to legitimize their authority and maintain centralized control.

The recruitment and use of **bureaucratic elites** became essential for imperial administration during this period. College Board wants you to understand that empires couldn't rely on the ruler's family alone — they needed professional administrators. The **Ottoman devshirme** system exemplifies this perfectly: the Ottomans recruited Christian boys, converted them to Islam, and trained them as elite administrators and soldiers (Janissaries) who were loyal only to the sultan. Similarly, empires developed **military professionals** rather than relying on feudal armies. Japan's **salaried samurai** transformed from independent warriors into paid government officials, demonstrating how empires professionalized their military forces to ensure loyalty and effectiveness.

Religious legitimacy provided another crucial pillar of imperial power, and College Board frequently tests these diverse approaches. **European notions of divine right** claimed monarchs ruled by God's will, making rebellion against the king equivalent to defying God himself. The **Mexica practice of human sacrifice** served multiple purposes: it demonstrated the emperor's connection to the gods while intimidating both subjects and enemies through displays of absolute power. The **Songhai promotion of Islam** helped rulers legitimize their authority through religious unity while connecting their empire to the broader Islamic world's trade networks and scholarly traditions.

Art and monumental architecture served as powerful tools of imperial propaganda — on the AP exam, you'll see questions about how these visual displays reinforced political power. **European palaces like Versailles** weren't just homes for kings; Louis XIV's massive palace forced French nobles to compete for his attention at court rather than plotting rebellion in their own territories. **Qing imperial portraits** depicted emperors with symbols of Mandate of Heaven authority, while **Mughal mausolea and mosques** like the Taj Mahal demonstrated imperial wealth and Islamic piety simultaneously. The **Incan sun temple of Cuzco** placed the emperor at the center of religious worship, literally making him the son of the sun god.

Taxation systems provided the economic foundation for all other administrative methods, and College Board loves testing the variety of approaches empires used. **Ming practice of collecting taxes in hard currency** rather than goods created a more efficient system and encouraged commercial development. **Mughal zamindar tax collection** used local elites as intermediaries, allowing the empire to collect revenue without direct administration of every village. **Ottoman tax farming** sold collection rights to private contractors, ensuring steady imperial revenue while transferring collection risks to entrepreneurs. **Mexica tribute lists** carefully recorded what each subject territory owed, from precious metals to exotic feathers, demonstrating the empire's sophisticated administrative record-keeping.

Understanding these administrative methods is crucial because they show how empires adapted traditional legitimacy concepts to manage increasingly complex states. These same challenges — balancing central control with local administration, legitimizing authority, and extracting resources — will reappear as we examine how these empires interacted with each other and with European maritime expansion.

## KEY TERMS
- **Bureaucratic elites** — Professional administrators recruited and trained to carry out imperial policies
- **Ottoman devshirme** — Ottoman system of recruiting Christian boys for conversion and training as administrators and soldiers
- **Military professionals** — Salaried, trained soldiers loyal to the state rather than feudal lords
- **Salaried samurai** — Japanese warriors transformed into paid government officials under centralized rule
- **European notions of divine right** — Belief that monarchs received their authority directly from God
- **Mexica practice of human sacrifice** — Aztec religious ritual used to demonstrate imperial power and intimidate subjects
- **Songhai promotion of Islam** — West African empire's use of Islamic identity to legitimize rule and connect to trade networks
- **European palaces, such as Versailles** — Monumental architecture designed to display royal power and control nobility
- **Qing imperial portraits** — Chinese artwork depicting emperors with symbols of Mandate of Heaven authority
- **Mughal mausolea and mosques** — Islamic architectural monuments demonstrating imperial wealth and religious devotion
- **Incan sun temple of Cuzco** — Religious center that positioned the emperor as divine son of the sun god
- **Ming practice of collecting taxes in hard currency** — Chinese system requiring monetary rather than in-kind tax payments
- **Mughal zamindar tax collection** — System using local elites as intermediaries for imperial tax collection
- **Ottoman tax farming** — Practice of selling tax collection rights to private contractors
- **Mexica tribute lists** — Detailed records of goods and services owed by subject territories to the Aztec Empire
