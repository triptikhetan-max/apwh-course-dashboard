## ARTICLE CONTENT

As industrial powers expanded their reach across the globe between 1750 and 1900, indigenous peoples didn't simply submit to imperial control. Instead, they fought back through **direct military resistance**, created **new independent states**, and launched **religious and cultural revival movements**. College Board loves testing this topic because it demonstrates how local peoples maintained agency even when facing technologically superior empires — a crucial theme that challenges the narrative of passive colonization.

**Direct military resistance** took many forms across different continents. In South America, **Túpac Amaru II's rebellion** (1780-1782) mobilized indigenous Peruvians against Spanish colonial rule, drawing on both Inca heritage and contemporary grievances about forced labor and taxation. In Africa, military leaders like **Samory Touré** fought French expansion in West Africa for nearly two decades (1882-1898), using guerrilla tactics and strategic retreats while building a powerful state that controlled trade routes. The **Yaa Asantewaa War** (1900-1901) saw an Asante queen mother lead resistance against British attempts to capture the Golden Stool, the sacred symbol of Asante power. Perhaps most famously, the **1857 rebellion in India** (also called the Sepoy Mutiny) united Hindu and Muslim soldiers against British East India Company rule, spreading across northern India before being brutally suppressed. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking you to compare these resistance movements — remember that they all combined traditional authority with responses to specific imperial policies.

Some indigenous groups responded to imperial pressure by forming **new independent states** that could better defend their interests. In West Africa, **Usman dan Fodio** established the **Sokoto Caliphate** (1804) in modern-day Nigeria, creating a powerful Islamic state that controlled trade and resisted both European encroachment and local non-Muslim rulers. The **Zulu Kingdom** under Shaka Zulu militarized Zulu society and expanded dramatically, creating a centralized state that could challenge British expansion in southern Africa. In North America, the **Cherokee Nation** adopted a written constitution, developed their own alphabet, and even took legal cases to the U.S. Supreme Court to defend their sovereignty — though they were ultimately forced west on the Trail of Tears. The **Balkans** saw multiple new states emerge as the Ottoman Empire weakened, with Serbia, Greece, Bulgaria, and others gaining independence through combinations of rebellion and great power intervention. College Board frequently tests how these new states used both traditional and modern methods to legitimize their authority.

When military resistance failed, many indigenous peoples turned to **religious and spiritual movements** that promised supernatural solutions to imperial domination. The **Ghost Dance** movement among Plains Indians in the 1890s promised that proper ritual performance would restore the buffalo, resurrect dead warriors, and drive away white settlers. The **Xhosa Cattle-Killing Movement** (1856-1857) in southern Africa saw thousands of Xhosa people destroy their cattle and crops based on prophecies that this sacrifice would resurrect ancestors and expel British colonizers — resulting instead in devastating famine. In Sudan, the **Mahdist wars** (1881-1899) combined Islamic messianic beliefs with resistance to Anglo-Egyptian rule, creating a theocratic state that controlled much of Sudan until British reconquest. These movements demonstrate how colonized peoples used spiritual resources to maintain hope and resist cultural destruction, even when facing overwhelming odds.

Understanding indigenous responses to state expansion is crucial because it reveals how colonialism was never a one-sided process — colonized peoples constantly adapted, resisted, and shaped the very empires that sought to control them.

## KEY TERMS
- **Túpac Amaru II's rebellion** — Indigenous uprising (1780-1782) against Spanish colonial rule in Peru led by a descendant of Inca rulers
- **Samory Touré** — West African leader who resisted French expansion through guerrilla warfare and state-building (1870s-1890s)
- **Yaa Asantewaa War** — Resistance movement (1900-1901) led by Asante queen mother against British attempts to seize the Golden Stool
- **1857 rebellion in India** — Major uprising by Indian soldiers and civilians against British East India Company rule
- **Sokoto Caliphate** — Islamic state established in northern Nigeria (1804) that resisted European colonization
- **Zulu Kingdom** — Militarized southern African state that expanded under Shaka Zulu and resisted British expansion
- **Cherokee Nation** — Native American state that adopted written constitution and legal strategies to defend sovereignty
- **Ghost Dance** — Plains Indian religious movement promising supernatural restoration of traditional life
- **Xhosa Cattle-Killing Movement** — South African spiritual movement involving cattle destruction based on prophecies of liberation
- **Mahdist wars** — Islamic resistance movement in Sudan combining messianic beliefs with anti-imperial warfare
- **Balkans independence movements** — Series of national liberation movements against Ottoman rule in southeastern Europe
