## ARTICLE CONTENT

The period from 1750 to 1900 marked the height of European imperialism, fundamentally reshaping global relationships through the power of industrial capitalism. College Board emphasizes this topic because it demonstrates how technological and economic changes create profound political and social consequences — a pattern that continues to influence our modern world.

**Industrial capitalism** drove imperial expansion as European nations sought new markets for their manufactured goods and sources of raw materials for their factories. The development of steamships, railways, and advanced weaponry gave industrialized nations unprecedented advantages over non-industrialized societies. Britain's control of India exemplifies this pattern: British factories needed cotton from Indian fields, but they also needed Indian consumers to buy British textiles, creating a **dependent colonial economy**. France similarly exploited West Africa for raw materials while using Algeria as a market for French goods. College Board frequently tests how this economic relationship benefited imperial powers while devastating local economies — remember that colonized regions often became **monoculture economies** focused on single cash crops rather than diverse, self-sufficient production.

The establishment of **transoceanic empires** created new forms of global migration that College Board considers essential knowledge. Unlike earlier voluntary trade migrations, imperial-era movements were often forced or coercive. The **indentured labor system** replaced slavery in many regions, bringing millions of Indians to work on British plantations in the Caribbean, Fiji, and South Africa, while Chinese workers migrated to build railways across the American West and work in Southeast Asian mines. These **contract laborers** faced harsh conditions but weren't technically enslaved, representing a new form of economic exploitation. Simultaneously, imperial administrators, military personnel, and settlers moved from Europe to colonies, creating **settler colonies** like Australia and South Africa where European populations established permanent communities.

Imperialism's social and cultural effects varied dramatically between colonizers and colonized peoples, creating what scholars call the **"dual mandate"** of imperial rule. For European societies, imperial wealth funded improved living standards, better infrastructure, and expanded educational opportunities — the profits from Indian cotton helped build British railways and schools. However, imperialism also created new social tensions as working-class Europeans questioned why colonial wealth didn't reach them. For colonized peoples, the effects were overwhelmingly negative: traditional political structures were dismantled, local industries were destroyed to eliminate competition with European manufacturers, and **cultural imperialism** imposed European languages, religions, and values while suppressing indigenous traditions. The **Berlin Conference** (1884-1885) perfectly illustrates imperial arrogance — European powers divided Africa among themselves without consulting a single African leader.

The political consequences of imperialism created the foundation for future global conflicts that you'll study in Unit 7. Imperial competition intensified European rivalries, as nations raced to claim territories before their competitors could. The **"Scramble for Africa"** transformed European diplomacy, while imperial tensions in Asia set the stage for conflicts like the **Opium Wars** in China and growing resentment that would fuel 20th-century independence movements. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking you to evaluate which effects of imperialism were most significant — remember that while imperial powers gained economically and politically in the short term, imperialism created long-term instabilities that would eventually contribute to two world wars and decolonization movements.

Understanding these causal relationships is crucial because imperial-era patterns of economic exploitation, forced migration, and cultural suppression established global inequalities that persist today, while the political boundaries and ethnic tensions created by imperial rule continue to influence international relations.

## KEY TERMS
- **Industrial capitalism** — Economic system combining factory production with private ownership that drove imperial expansion
- **Dependent colonial economy** — Economic system where colonies provided raw materials and bought manufactured goods from imperial powers
- **Monoculture economies** — Colonial economic systems focused on single cash crops for export rather than diverse production
- **Transoceanic empires** — Imperial systems spanning multiple continents connected by sea routes and steamship technology
- **Indentured labor system** — Contract labor system where workers agreed to fixed-term service in exchange for transportation and wages
- **Contract laborers** — Workers bound by specific agreements to work for set periods, often under harsh conditions
- **Settler colonies** — Colonial territories where large European populations established permanent communities
- **Dual mandate** — Imperial policy claiming to benefit both colonizing and colonized peoples (though rarely achieving this)
- **Cultural imperialism** — Imposition of European languages, religions, and values while suppressing indigenous traditions
- **Berlin Conference** — 1884-1885 meeting where European powers divided Africa without African input
- **Scramble for Africa** — Period of rapid European colonization of Africa between 1880-1914
- **Opium Wars** — Conflicts between Britain and China over trade rights and imperial influence in Asia
