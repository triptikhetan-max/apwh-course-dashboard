## ARTICLE CONTENT

The period from 1750-1900 witnessed unprecedented global migration as industrialization, imperialism, and economic opportunities drove millions of people across continents. College Board frequently tests this topic because these migration patterns fundamentally transformed both the societies people left and the places they moved to — creating ripple effects that shaped the modern world.

One of the most significant social changes occurred when male migrants dominated migration flows, leaving women behind to fill roles traditionally occupied by men. In rural Ireland during the **Potato Famine** (1845-1852), men migrated to America for construction work, leaving Irish women to manage farms, make financial decisions, and head households. Similarly, in China, male laborers migrated to build railroads in the American West and work plantations in Southeast Asia, while Chinese women took over family businesses, agricultural management, and became primary breadwinners. This pattern repeated globally — from Italian men working in Argentine cities while women managed Mediterranean farming, to Indian men laboring in British colonies while women assumed leadership in village communities. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking you to analyze how these **gendered migration patterns** challenged traditional patriarchal structures and expanded women's economic and social authority.

**Labor migration systems** created by industrial and imperial powers further transformed societies through both voluntary and forced movement. The **indentured servitude** system brought millions of Indians to British colonies in the Caribbean, Fiji, and South Africa after slavery's abolition, creating new multicultural societies with complex racial hierarchies. Chinese **coolies** built railroads across the American West and worked plantations in Peru and Cuba, establishing Chinatowns that preserved cultural traditions while adapting to local conditions. College Board loves testing how these migrations created **diaspora communities** that maintained connections to their homelands while developing new hybrid identities.

Internal migrations within industrializing nations also produced dramatic social changes. **Rural-to-urban migration** in Britain, Germany, and the United States created massive industrial cities where traditional family structures broke down and new social classes emerged. Young women migrated from farms to work in textile factories, gaining economic independence but facing dangerous working conditions. These **factory girls** in places like Lowell, Massachusetts, lived in company boarding houses and developed new forms of female solidarity through labor organizing. Meanwhile, agricultural regions lost young people, forcing older residents and women to adopt new farming techniques and social arrangements.

The receiving societies also transformed as migration created cultural diversity, labor competition, and new social tensions. In the United States, **nativism** emerged as established populations feared job competition from Irish, Chinese, and later Southern and Eastern European migrants. Australia's **White Australia Policy** and American **Chinese Exclusion Act** (1882) demonstrated how migration could provoke racist legislation designed to limit certain ethnic groups. Yet these same migrations also enriched receiving societies through new foods, religious practices, artistic traditions, and business innovations that migrants brought with them.

Understanding migration's effects is crucial because it reveals how industrialization didn't just change production — it fundamentally reorganized human societies, family structures, and gender roles in ways that continue to influence our modern world.

## KEY TERMS
- **Gendered migration patterns** — Tendency for migration flows to be predominantly male, leaving women to assume new roles
- **Potato Famine** — Irish crop failure (1845-1852) that forced mass male migration to America
- **Labor migration systems** — Organized movement of workers to meet industrial and agricultural labor demands
- **Indentured servitude** — Contract labor system where workers agreed to years of service in exchange for transportation
- **Coolies** — Term for Chinese and Indian contract laborers who migrated globally for construction and plantation work
- **Diaspora communities** — Populations living outside their ancestral homeland while maintaining cultural connections
- **Rural-to-urban migration** — Movement from agricultural areas to industrial cities seeking factory employment
- **Factory girls** — Young women who migrated from rural areas to work in textile mills
- **Nativism** — Political movement favoring native-born citizens over immigrants
- **White Australia Policy** — Discriminatory immigration policy designed to maintain European racial dominance
- **Chinese Exclusion Act** — 1882 U.S. law prohibiting Chinese immigration and naturalization
- **Chain migration** — Process where initial migrants help family and community members follow the same migration path
- **Remittances** — Money sent by migrants back to their home communities
