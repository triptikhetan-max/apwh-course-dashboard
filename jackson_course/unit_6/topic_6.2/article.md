## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to one of College Board's favorite topics: how states dramatically expanded their power between 1750 and 1900! This period saw unprecedented territorial expansion and colonial consolidation as industrialized nations used their technological advantages to dominate weaker regions. Understanding these patterns is crucial because the AP exam frequently asks you to compare different methods of state expansion across various continents.

The most significant trend was European governments taking **direct control** over colonies previously managed by private companies. The most dramatic example occurred in the **Congo**, where **King Leopold II of Belgium** had personally owned the entire territory since 1885, running it as his private business through brutal forced labor that killed millions of Africans. International outrage over these atrocities forced the Belgian government to assume control in 1908, creating the **Belgian Congo**. Similarly, the **Dutch East India Company** had controlled Indonesia and much of Southeast Asia for centuries, but financial troubles led the Dutch government to take direct control in 1800. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking why governments replaced private companies — remember that states wanted more reliable control over profitable colonies and needed to manage international criticism of company abuses.

European states also launched the **"Scramble for Africa"** after 1870, carving up the entire continent except for Ethiopia and Liberia. **Britain expanded in West Africa**, taking control of modern-day Nigeria, Ghana, and Sierra Leone to protect existing coastal trading posts and access palm oil and gold. **France conquered vast territories in West Africa**, creating French West Africa that stretched from Senegal to Chad, motivated by national prestige and the desire to compete with Britain. These African conquests differed from earlier colonialism because Europeans used **superior military technology** — particularly repeating rifles and machine guns — to overwhelm African resistance. College Board loves testing the **Berlin Conference of 1884-1885**, where European powers literally drew lines on maps to divide Africa without consulting any African leaders.

Beyond Europe, three major powers expanded through **territorial conquest and settlement**. The **United States** pursued **Manifest Destiny**, conquering Mexican territories in the Southwest and purchasing Alaska from Russia in 1867, while also expanding overseas to Hawaii, Puerto Rico, and the Philippines after 1898. **Russia** continued its centuries-long expansion, conquering Central Asian territories like Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan while pushing into the Caucasus region. **Japan**, after its 1868 Meiji Restoration, rapidly industrialized and then conquered Taiwan (1895) and Korea (1910) while defeating both China and Russia in wars. These three powers succeeded because they combined industrial technology with **settler colonialism** — actually moving their populations into conquered territories rather than just extracting resources.

European powers also established **settler colonies** where large numbers of Europeans permanently relocated, fundamentally transforming local societies. **New Zealand** became a British settler colony after 1840, where European diseases devastated the indigenous Māori population, making room for British farmers and ranchers. These settler colonies differed from other imperial territories because colonizers intended to recreate European society rather than simply exploit local resources, leading to more complete cultural destruction of indigenous peoples.

## KEY TERMS
- **Direct control** — Government management of colonies, replacing private company administration
- **King Leopold II** — Belgian monarch who personally owned the Congo (1885-1908) through brutal exploitation
- **Belgian Congo** — Territory taken over by Belgian government in 1908 after international outrage over Leopold's abuses
- **Dutch East India Company** — Private trading company that controlled Indonesia until Dutch government takeover in 1800
- **Scramble for Africa** — Rapid European colonization of Africa between 1870-1914
- **Berlin Conference (1884-1885)** — Meeting where European powers divided Africa without consulting African leaders
- **Manifest Destiny** — American belief that westward expansion across North America was justified and inevitable
- **Meiji Restoration (1868)** — Japanese political revolution that ended feudalism and began rapid modernization
- **Settler colonialism** — Form of colonialism where colonizers permanently relocate to establish new societies
- **New Zealand** — British settler colony established in 1840 that displaced indigenous Māori populations
- **French West Africa** — Large colonial federation stretching from Senegal to Chad, created by French expansion
- **Superior military technology** — Industrial weapons like repeating rifles and machine guns that enabled European conquest
