## ARTICLE CONTENT

Economic imperialism represents one of the most important yet often overlooked forms of imperial control. Unlike traditional political colonialism where European powers directly governed territories, **economic imperialism** allowed industrialized nations to dominate other regions through financial control, trade monopolies, and resource extraction — often without the expense of formal political administration. College Board frequently tests this concept because it explains how global economic inequality developed and persisted long after political independence.

The foundation of economic imperialism lay in the **industrial revolution's asymmetrical development**. As Britain, France, Germany, and the United States industrialized rapidly after 1750, they created massive demand for raw materials and new markets for manufactured goods. This led to what historians call **unequal exchange** — industrialized nations exported expensive manufactured products while importing cheap raw materials from non-industrialized regions. In Asia, the **British East India Company** pioneered this model in India, controlling textile production and forcing Indian artisans to produce raw cotton for British factories rather than finished cloth. Similarly, British merchants used the **Opium Wars** (1839-1842, 1856-1860) to force China to accept unfavorable trade terms, including opening **treaty ports** where European merchants enjoyed extraterritorial rights and controlled local commerce.

Latin America became another prime target for economic imperialism, particularly after independence movements in the 1810s-1820s left new nations financially vulnerable. **British merchants and banks** quickly filled the void left by Spanish colonial administration, providing loans to new governments while gaining control over key industries. In Argentina, British companies dominated railroad construction and beef exports. In Chile, European firms controlled nitrate mining. In Brazil, British and American companies controlled coffee exports. This created a pattern of **export dependency** where Latin American nations became specialized producers of single commodities for European markets. When global commodity prices fluctuated, entire national economies suffered — exactly what European financiers intended, since economic instability made these nations more dependent on European investment and expertise.

The **commodity trade organization** that emerged during this period systematically advantaged European and American merchants. **Vertical integration** allowed Western companies to control every stage of production, from raw material extraction to final sale in European markets. For example, British firms in India controlled cotton growing, processing, transportation, and marketing, ensuring maximum profits stayed with British shareholders. The **steamship revolution** and expanded **telegraph networks** gave Western merchants significant advantages in market information and transportation speed. Meanwhile, **lending practices** and **currency manipulation** kept non-European economies dependent on Western financial systems. College Board often asks about how these economic structures persisted even after political independence — understanding economic imperialism explains why formal decolonization didn't immediately end global inequality.

The effects of economic imperialism created lasting global patterns that shaped the modern world economy. Raw material producers remained economically subordinate to industrial manufacturers, a relationship that continued well into the twentieth century and helps explain persistent global development gaps.

## KEY TERMS
- **Economic imperialism** — Control of foreign territories through financial dominance rather than direct political rule
- **Unequal exchange** — Trading system where industrialized nations export expensive manufactured goods for cheap raw materials
- **British East India Company** — British trading company that controlled Indian commerce and textile production
- **Opium Wars** — British military conflicts forcing China to accept unfavorable trade terms (1839-1842, 1856-1860)
- **Treaty ports** — Chinese coastal cities where European merchants enjoyed special trading privileges and extraterritorial rights
- **Export dependency** — Economic condition where nations rely heavily on exporting single commodities to foreign markets
- **Vertical integration** — Business practice of controlling all stages of production from raw materials to final sale
- **Commodity trade organization** — Global system organizing raw material trade to benefit European and American merchants
- **Steamship revolution** — Transportation innovation that gave Western merchants speed advantages in global trade
- **Telegraph networks** — Communication systems providing Western merchants faster access to market information
- **Currency manipulation** — Financial practices that kept non-European economies dependent on Western banking systems
- **Extraterritorial rights** — Legal privileges allowing foreign merchants to operate under their home country's laws
