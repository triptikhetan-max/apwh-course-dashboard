## ARTICLE CONTENT

The period from 1750 to 1900 witnessed unprecedented human migration as millions of people moved across continents and oceans. College Board emphasizes this topic because it perfectly demonstrates how industrialization created interconnected global networks that fundamentally changed human movement patterns. Understanding these migration causes is essential because they shaped the demographic makeup of entire continents and created the foundation for our modern globalized world.

**Environmental factors** drove massive population movements throughout this period. The **Irish Potato Famine** (1845-1852) exemplifies how environmental disasters could trigger mass migration — when a potato blight destroyed Ireland's primary food source, over one million people died and another million emigrated, primarily to the United States. Similarly, **droughts, floods, and crop failures** across Europe and Asia forced rural populations to seek new opportunities elsewhere. In China, the **Yellow River floods** of the 1850s displaced millions, contributing to Chinese migration to Southeast Asia and the Americas. College Board often tests how these environmental push factors created specific migration patterns — remember that environmental disasters typically created sudden, large-scale movements of desperate populations seeking basic survival.

**Economic transformations** created both push and pull factors that reshaped global demographics. **Industrialization** in Britain, the United States, and Germany created enormous labor demands in factories, mines, and railroad construction, pulling millions from rural areas to industrial centers. The **abolition of slavery** created labor shortages in plantation economies, leading to new forms of migration like **indentured servitude** from India and China. Meanwhile, **agricultural changes** — including the enclosure movement in Britain and similar land consolidations elsewhere — pushed small farmers off their land. On the AP exam, you'll frequently see questions about how these economic factors created both internal migration (rural to urban within countries) and international migration (between countries and continents).

The **demographic revolution** fundamentally altered migration patterns by creating population pressures in some areas while opening opportunities in others. **Population growth** in Europe, driven by improved nutrition and medical advances, created competition for limited resources and jobs, encouraging emigration to the Americas, Australia, and other colonies. Simultaneously, **industrializing regions** experienced rapid urbanization as people sought factory work — Manchester, England's population exploded from 75,000 in 1801 to 645,000 by 1901. College Board frequently contrasts voluntary economic migration with forced movements, so remember that while many migrants chose to relocate for better opportunities, others had little choice due to environmental disasters or economic displacement.

These migration patterns created lasting global connections that would define the modern world. **Voluntary migration** for economic opportunities built diverse societies in receiving areas, while environmental and demographic pressures continued to drive human movement. Understanding these causes helps explain not only where people moved, but why certain migration streams developed between specific regions — patterns that would continue influencing global demographics well beyond 1900.

## KEY TERMS
- **Irish Potato Famine** — Environmental disaster (1845-1852) that killed one million Irish and forced another million to emigrate
- **Environmental push factors** — Natural disasters, crop failures, and climate changes that force people to leave their homes
- **Yellow River floods** — Major flooding in 1850s China that displaced millions and contributed to Chinese emigration
- **Industrialization pull factors** — Factory jobs, mining opportunities, and railroad construction that attracted migrants to industrial centers
- **Indentured servitude** — Labor system where workers contracted for fixed terms, often replacing enslaved labor
- **Enclosure movement** — Process of consolidating small farms into larger holdings, displacing rural populations
- **Internal migration** — Movement of people within the same country, typically from rural to urban areas
- **International migration** — Movement of people across national boundaries seeking new opportunities
- **Demographic revolution** — Rapid population growth due to improved nutrition and medical advances
- **Voluntary economic migration** — People choosing to relocate in search of better economic opportunities
- **Rural-to-urban migration** — Movement from agricultural areas to industrial cities during industrialization
- **Labor shortages** — Insufficient workers in specific industries or regions, creating migration pull factors
- **Population pressure** — Competition for resources and opportunities due to rapid population growth
