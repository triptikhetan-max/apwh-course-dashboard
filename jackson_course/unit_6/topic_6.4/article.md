## ARTICLE CONTENT

The Industrial Revolution didn't happen in a vacuum — it created a completely new global economy based on environmental exploitation. As factories multiplied across Europe and North America from 1750 to 1900, they demanded massive quantities of **raw materials** that simply weren't available locally. This industrial hunger transformed entire continents into specialized **export economies** focused on extracting natural resources and growing crops for distant markets. College Board loves testing this cause-and-effect relationship because it shows how industrialization reshaped the entire world, not just the countries with factories.

The environmental foundation of this new economy was simple but revolutionary: different regions had different natural advantages that industrial powers desperately needed. Egypt's **Nile River valley** provided perfect conditions for growing **cotton**, which British textile factories consumed in enormous quantities. The **rubber trees** of Brazil and Southeast Asia became essential as demand exploded for bicycle tires, electrical insulation, and waterproof goods. **Guano deposits** on Pacific islands and **nitrates** from Chile's Atacama Desert provided fertilizers that fed Europe's growing urban populations. Palm oil from West Africa lubricated industrial machinery, while **copper** from Chile and Montana supplied the electrical revolution. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how these environmental advantages determined which regions specialized in which exports — remember that geography literally shaped the global economy.

This specialization created a clear global division of labor that College Board frequently tests. Industrial centers like Britain, Germany, and the northeastern United States used their technological advantages to manufacture **finished goods** — textiles, steel products, machinery, and chemicals. Meanwhile, regions with abundant natural resources but less industrial development became **commodity exporters**. **Argentina's pampas** specialized in beef and wheat production, shipping food to European cities. **Indian cotton** flowed to British mills, then returned as finished cloth. **Australian wool** and **New Zealand mutton** fed Britain's industrial workers. The profits from these raw material exports were then used to purchase manufactured goods from industrial centers, creating a cycle of economic dependence that would shape international relations for decades.

The environmental impact of this global economy was staggering and often devastating. **Monoculture agriculture** — growing single crops for export — depleted soil nutrients and made entire regions vulnerable to crop failures. The California Gold Rush and similar **mining booms** in South Africa and Australia stripped landscapes bare and polluted waterways. **Deforestation** accelerated as demand grew for ship timber, railroad ties, and charcoal for smelting. College Board emphasizes that these environmental changes weren't just side effects — they were fundamental to how the global economy operated. The same environmental exploitation that powered industrial growth also created long-term ecological problems that affected millions of people.

Understanding this environmental foundation of global economic development is crucial because it explains patterns of wealth and poverty that persisted well into the twentieth century. Regions that became raw material exporters often remained economically dependent, while industrial centers accumulated capital and technological expertise that reinforced their advantages.

## KEY TERMS
- **Raw materials** — Unprocessed natural resources like cotton, rubber, and metals needed for industrial production
- **Export economies** — National or regional economies focused on selling natural resources and agricultural products to foreign markets
- **Cotton** — Fiber crop that became essential to textile industry, especially from Egypt, India, and the American South
- **Rubber** — Latex extracted from trees, crucial for tires, gaskets, and electrical insulation
- **Guano deposits** — Bird droppings accumulated on islands, providing nitrogen-rich fertilizer for European agriculture
- **Nitrates** — Chemical compounds from Chile used in fertilizers and explosives
- **Finished goods** — Manufactured products made from raw materials, such as textiles, machinery, and steel products
- **Commodity exporters** — Regions specializing in producing and selling raw materials rather than manufactured goods
- **Argentina's pampas** — Fertile grasslands ideal for cattle ranching and wheat farming for European markets
- **Indian cotton** — Raw cotton exported to British textile mills, then reimported as finished cloth
- **Monoculture agriculture** — Growing single crops for export, which depleted soil and created economic vulnerability
- **Mining booms** — Rapid extraction of gold, silver, and other minerals that transformed local landscapes and economies
- **Deforestation** — Large-scale clearing of forests to supply timber for ships, railroads, and fuel
