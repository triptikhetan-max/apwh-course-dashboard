## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to one of College Board's favorite topics: the **Enlightenment**! This 18th-century intellectual revolution didn't just change how people thought — it created the ideological foundations for every major revolution from 1750 to 1900. College Board loves testing the Enlightenment because it perfectly demonstrates how new ideas can transform entire societies and political systems.

The Enlightenment fundamentally changed how Europeans understood both the natural world and human society through **empiricism** — the idea that knowledge comes from observation and experience rather than tradition or religious authority. Philosophers called **philosophes** developed new ways of thinking that emphasized **reason** over faith and challenged the role of religion in public life. Key thinkers like John Locke argued for **natural rights** (life, liberty, property), while Jean-Jacques Rousseau developed the **social contract** theory — the idea that governments derive their authority from the consent of the governed, not from divine right. On the AP exam, you'll frequently see questions about how these concepts directly challenged absolute monarchy and traditional religious authority.

These Enlightenment ideas about individual rights and government legitimacy created the intellectual foundation for revolutionary movements across the Atlantic world. The concept of **popular sovereignty** — that political power ultimately belongs to the people — directly inspired the American Revolution's Declaration of Independence and the French Revolution's Declaration of the Rights of Man. College Board wants you to understand that these weren't just abstract philosophical debates; they became the blueprints for overthrowing existing governments and creating new political systems based on constitutional limitations and individual rights.

The Enlightenment also sparked early challenges to gender hierarchies through emerging **feminism**. **Mary Wollstonecraft's "A Vindication of the Rights of Woman"** (1792) applied Enlightenment reasoning to argue that women deserved equal education and political rights. **Olympe de Gouges** wrote the **"Declaration of the Rights of Woman and of the Female Citizen"** (1791) during the French Revolution, explicitly demanding that women receive the same rights as men. Later, the **Seneca Falls Conference** (1848), organized by **Elizabeth Cady Stanton** and **Lucretia Mott**, launched the American women's suffrage movement. College Board frequently tests how these early feminist thinkers used Enlightenment principles to challenge traditional gender roles.

The rise of **nationalism** during this period created another powerful force that would reshape empires and states. Enlightenment ideas about popular sovereignty combined with shared language, culture, and history to create new concepts of national identity that challenged multi-ethnic empires and absolute monarchies.

Understanding the Enlightenment is crucial because it provides the intellectual context for virtually every revolution and reform movement you'll study in Unit 5. These weren't just political upheavals — they were the practical application of Enlightenment philosophy to real-world governance and society.

## KEY TERMS
- **Enlightenment** — 18th-century intellectual movement emphasizing reason, empiricism, and individual rights
- **Empiricism** — Philosophical approach that knowledge comes from observation and experience rather than tradition
- **Philosophes** — Enlightenment thinkers who applied reason to understand natural world and human society
- **Reason** — Use of logical thinking and evidence rather than tradition or religious authority
- **Natural rights** — John Locke's concept that humans possess inherent rights like life, liberty, and property
- **Social contract** — Theory that governments derive authority from consent of the governed
- **Popular sovereignty** — Principle that political power ultimately belongs to the people
- **Feminism** — Movement advocating for women's political, social, and economic equality
- **Mary Wollstonecraft** — Author of "A Vindication of the Rights of Woman" (1792)
- **Olympe de Gouges** — French writer who authored "Declaration of the Rights of Woman and of the Female Citizen" (1791)
- **Seneca Falls Conference** — 1848 women's rights convention organized by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott
- **Nationalism** — Political ideology emphasizing shared identity based on common language, culture, and history
