## ARTICLE CONTENT

By the mid-1800s, the Industrial Revolution had created a clear divide between the "haves" and "have-nots" of global power. College Board wants you to understand that while Britain initially led industrialization through private enterprise, other nations realized they needed **state-sponsored industrialization** to compete. This wasn't just about economic growth — it was about national survival in an increasingly competitive world.

As the influence of industrialization spread, a small number of governments took direct control of their economic transformation. **Germany** under Otto von Bismarck used state intervention to rapidly build railroads, establish technical schools, and coordinate industrial development after unification in 1871. **Russia** under Tsar Alexander II and later Sergei Witte implemented massive state-directed programs, including the construction of the **Trans-Siberian Railway** and heavy investment in steel production. The **Ottoman Empire** attempted similar reforms during the **Tanzimat period** (1839-1876), though with less success due to financial constraints and internal resistance. College Board frequently tests why some state-sponsored programs succeeded while others failed — the key factors include financial resources, administrative capacity, and social acceptance of change.

The most dramatic example of successful government-led industrialization occurred in **Japan during the Meiji Era** (1868-1912). Facing pressure from American **Commodore Matthew Perry's** "Black Ships" in 1853 and subsequent unequal treaties, Japan recognized that industrialization was essential for maintaining independence. The **Meiji Restoration** of 1868 marked the end of the **Tokugawa Shogunate** and the beginning of systematic westernization under the slogan **"fukoku kyōhei"** (rich country, strong military). On the AP exam, you'll often see questions comparing Japan's response to Western pressure with China's more resistant approach during the same period.

The Meiji government implemented comprehensive reforms that transformed Japan from a feudal society into an industrial power within a single generation. They abolished the **samurai class** and **han system** (feudal domains), replacing them with a centralized government modeled on Western bureaucracies. The state invested heavily in infrastructure, building railroads, telegraph lines, and modern ports. They established the **zaibatsu system**, where the government created industries and then sold them to private families, creating powerful industrial conglomerates. Education became compulsory, with thousands of Japanese students sent abroad to study Western technology and methods. By 1905, Japan's victory in the **Russo-Japanese War** demonstrated that an Asian nation could defeat a European power, marking Japan's emergence as a regional power.

Understanding government-sponsored industrialization is crucial because it shows how states adapted to the challenges of the Industrial Revolution. Unlike the laissez-faire approach of early British industrialization, these later efforts required massive state intervention and planning — a pattern that would continue into the 20th century with Soviet five-year plans and other centrally planned economies.

## KEY TERMS
- **State-sponsored industrialization** — Government-directed economic development programs to promote industrial growth
- **Trans-Siberian Railway** — Russian state-built railroad connecting Moscow to the Pacific, completed in 1916
- **Tanzimat period** — Ottoman reform era (1839-1876) attempting to modernize the empire through Western-style changes
- **Meiji Era** — Japanese period (1868-1912) of rapid westernization and industrialization under restored imperial rule
- **Commodore Matthew Perry** — American naval officer who forced Japan to open trade relations in 1853-1854
- **Meiji Restoration** — 1868 political revolution that ended the Tokugawa Shogunate and restored imperial rule
- **Tokugawa Shogunate** — Japanese military government (1603-1868) that maintained isolation until forced opening
- **"Fukoku kyōhei"** — Japanese slogan meaning "rich country, strong military" that guided Meiji-era reforms
- **Samurai class** — Traditional Japanese warrior class abolished during Meiji reforms
- **Han system** — Feudal domain system in Japan replaced by centralized prefectures during Meiji era
- **Zaibatsu system** — Japanese industrial conglomerates initially created by government investment, then privatized
- **Russo-Japanese War** — 1904-1905 conflict where Japan's victory demonstrated its emergence as a modern power
