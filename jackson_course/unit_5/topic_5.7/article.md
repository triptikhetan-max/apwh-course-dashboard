## ARTICLE CONTENT

The Industrial Revolution didn't just transform how goods were made — it completely revolutionized how the global economy worked. College Board wants you to understand that between 1750 and 1900, Western European nations abandoned centuries-old economic practices and created entirely new systems that still influence our world today.

The biggest shift was Western Europe's abandonment of **mercantilism** in favor of **free trade policies**. For centuries, mercantilist thinking had dominated European economics — the idea that wealth was finite and nations needed to hoard gold, maintain favorable trade balances, and heavily regulate commerce. But **Adam Smith's** revolutionary ideas about **laissez-faire capitalism** and **free markets** changed everything. Smith argued in "The Wealth of Nations" that if governments stepped back and let markets operate freely, competition would naturally lead to better products, lower prices, and greater prosperity for everyone. College Board loves testing this transition because it shows how new ideas can completely transform economic systems — on the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking you to compare mercantilist policies with free trade approaches or explain why European nations adopted Smith's theories.

This shift to **industrial capitalism** created unprecedented opportunities but also new challenges. Living standards improved dramatically for some people — the expanding middle class enjoyed access to mass-produced consumer goods that were previously luxury items. Manufacturing methods continuously improved, making products more available, affordable, and varied than ever before. However, College Board emphasizes that these benefits weren't equally distributed. Factory workers often faced dangerous conditions and long hours, while factory owners accumulated enormous wealth. This growing inequality would later fuel social reform movements and alternative economic ideologies like socialism.

The new capitalist system required innovative financial institutions and business structures that College Board considers essential knowledge. **Stock markets** allowed people to invest in companies by buying shares, spreading both risk and potential profits across many investors. **Limited-liability corporations** protected investors by limiting their financial responsibility if a company failed — you could only lose what you invested, not your entire personal wealth. These innovations enabled massive **transnational businesses** like the **Hong Kong and Shanghai Banking Corporation (HSBC)**, which facilitated trade across the British Empire, and **Unilever**, which operated soap and margarine factories from England and the Netherlands while sourcing palm oil from British West Africa and the Belgian Congo. On the AP exam, you'll need to explain how these financial instruments and business structures enabled the global expansion of industrial capitalism.

Understanding these economic developments is crucial because they created the foundation for modern global capitalism and set up the social tensions that would drive reform movements, labor organizing, and revolutionary ideologies in the late 1800s and early 1900s.

## KEY TERMS
- **Mercantilism** — Economic theory emphasizing government regulation, favorable trade balances, and accumulation of gold
- **Free trade policies** — Economic approach removing government barriers to international commerce
- **Adam Smith** — Scottish economist who advocated for laissez-faire capitalism and free markets
- **Laissez-faire capitalism** — Economic system with minimal government interference in business operations
- **Free markets** — Economic system where prices and production are determined by supply and demand
- **Industrial capitalism** — Economic system combining factory production with private ownership and market competition
- **Stock markets** — Financial institutions where company shares are bought and sold publicly
- **Limited-liability corporations** — Business structure protecting investors from losing more than their initial investment
- **Transnational businesses** — Companies operating across multiple countries and continents
- **Hong Kong and Shanghai Banking Corporation (HSBC)** — British bank facilitating trade throughout the British Empire
- **Unilever** — Anglo-Dutch company operating consumer goods factories globally
- **Financial instruments** — Tools like stocks and bonds that facilitate investment and capital formation
