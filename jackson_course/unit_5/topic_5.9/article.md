## ARTICLE CONTENT

The Industrial Revolution didn't just transform how people worked — it completely reshuffled society itself. College Board loves testing how industrialization created entirely new social classes while transforming existing ones, because this demonstrates one of history's most dramatic social transformations in just a few generations.

The most significant change was the emergence of the **industrial working class** or **proletariat**. These factory workers faced harsh conditions that College Board wants you to understand: 12-16 hour workdays, dangerous machinery, polluted air, and wages barely sufficient for survival. Women and children worked alongside men, often for even lower pay. Workers lived in overcrowded **tenements** in industrial cities where disease spread rapidly and life expectancy plummeted. This wasn't just unfortunate — it was the direct result of factory owners prioritizing profits over worker welfare. On the AP exam, you'll see questions asking you to analyze how industrialization worsened living conditions for this new working class, at least initially.

Simultaneously, industrialization created and expanded the **middle class** or **bourgeoisie**. This group included factory owners, managers, professionals like doctors and lawyers, and skilled craftsmen who could afford comfortable homes and education for their children. The middle class developed distinct cultural values emphasizing hard work, moral respectability, and **domesticity** — the idea that women should focus on home and family while men worked in the public sphere. College Board frequently tests how the middle class used these values to distinguish themselves from both the aristocracy above and the working class below. Middle-class families could afford consumer goods, domestic servants, and leisure activities that were impossible for industrial workers.

The traditional aristocracy found their power challenged but not eliminated. While they retained land ownership and political influence, the new industrial capitalists began competing for social status and political power. Some aristocrats adapted by investing in factories and railroads, while others resisted these "new money" industrialists. This created tension between old wealth based on land and new wealth based on industry — a dynamic College Board often includes in exam questions about changing social hierarchies.

These class divisions led to new forms of social conflict and organization. Workers formed **labor unions** and engaged in strikes to demand better conditions, while middle-class reformers pushed for regulations to address the worst industrial abuses. The stark contrast between industrial wealth and working-class poverty would soon inspire new political ideologies like socialism and communism, setting up the social tensions that would define the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

## KEY TERMS
- **Industrial working class/Proletariat** — New social class of factory workers who sold their labor for wages
- **Middle class/Bourgeoisie** — Social class of factory owners, professionals, and skilled workers with comfortable incomes
- **Tenements** — Overcrowded, poorly constructed apartment buildings housing industrial workers
- **Domesticity** — Middle-class ideology emphasizing women's roles as wives and mothers in the home
- **Labor unions** — Organizations formed by workers to collectively bargain for better wages and conditions
- **Class consciousness** — Awareness of belonging to a particular social class with shared interests
- **Social mobility** — The ability to move between social classes, limited but possible in industrial society
- **Working conditions** — Often dangerous, unhealthy factory environments with long hours and low pay
- **Consumer culture** — Middle-class lifestyle based on purchasing manufactured goods for comfort and status
- **Industrial capitalism** — Economic system where factory owners controlled production and workers sold their labor
- **Social hierarchy** — Ranking system placing aristocrats at top, middle class in middle, workers at bottom
- **Urban poverty** — Concentration of poor living conditions in rapidly growing industrial cities
