## ARTICLE CONTENT

The period from 1750 to 1900 represents one of history's most revolutionary eras, and College Board loves testing how **nationalism** — the idea that people sharing language, religion, social customs, and territory should form their own nation-states — transformed the political map of the world. This new sense of commonality wasn't just a natural development; it was often deliberately fostered by governments and revolutionary leaders to create unity and challenge existing imperial rule. Understanding this period is crucial because it establishes the foundation for modern nation-states and democratic ideologies.

The intellectual foundation for these revolutions came from **Enlightenment philosophers** whose ideas about natural rights, popular sovereignty, and democratic government directly challenged traditional monarchical and imperial authority. College Board specifically wants you to know how these ideas appeared in key revolutionary documents: the **American Declaration of Independence** (1776) proclaimed that governments derive their power from the consent of the governed, the French **"Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen"** (1789) established universal human rights, and Bolívar's **"Letter from Jamaica"** (1815) applied these democratic ideals to Latin American independence movements. On the AP exam, you'll frequently see questions asking you to compare how these documents reflected Enlightenment principles while adapting them to local circumstances.

The success of the **American Revolution** created a powerful model for colonial subjects worldwide, demonstrating that European empires could be defeated and republican governments established. This inspiration directly influenced the **Haitian Revolution** (1791-1804), where enslaved Africans led by Toussaint Louverture successfully overthrew French colonial rule and created the first independent nation founded by formerly enslaved people. The **Latin American independence movements** led by figures like Simón Bolívar and José de San Martín further proved that European colonial control could be broken, leading to the emergence of independent states throughout the Americas. College Board emphasizes that these weren't isolated events but part of a connected pattern of democratic revolutions that challenged imperial authority.

Beyond the Americas, **nationalism** drove movements for both independence and unification across Europe and other regions. The **German and Italian unifications** showed how fragmented regions could be unified under new national identities — Otto von Bismarck's strategy of "blood and iron" created modern Germany, while Giuseppe Garibaldi's campaigns unified the Italian peninsula. **Balkan nationalisms** challenged Ottoman control as ethnic groups like Serbs, Greeks, and Bulgarians sought independence based on shared language and religion. Even within empires, movements like **Ottomanism** attempted to create inclusive national identities, while colonial subjects developed their own nationalist movements: the **Propaganda Movement in the Philippines** pushed for reforms and eventual independence from Spain, **Maori nationalism** led to the New Zealand Wars as indigenous people resisted British colonization, and writers like **Lola Rodríguez de Tió** in Puerto Rico used literature to foster national consciousness.

These revolutionary and nationalist movements fundamentally transformed global politics by establishing the principle that political boundaries should align with national communities defined by shared culture, language, and territory. This period's emphasis on popular sovereignty and democratic ideals sets the stage for understanding the global conflicts and decolonization movements of the next century.

## KEY TERMS
- **Nationalism** — Political ideology advocating that people sharing language, religion, customs, and territory should form independent nation-states
- **Enlightenment philosophers** — 17th-18th century thinkers who promoted ideas about natural rights, popular sovereignty, and democratic government
- **American Declaration of Independence** — 1776 document proclaiming natural rights and government by consent of the governed
- **"Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen"** — 1789 French revolutionary document establishing universal human rights
- **"Letter from Jamaica"** — 1815 document by Simón Bolívar applying Enlightenment ideals to Latin American independence
- **American Revolution** — 1775-1783 colonial rebellion that established the United States as a model for republican government
- **Haitian Revolution** — 1791-1804 successful slave rebellion led by Toussaint Louverture that created an independent Haiti
- **Latin American independence movements** — Early 19th century rebellions led by Bolívar and others that freed most of South America from Spanish rule
- **German and Italian unifications** — 19th century nationalist movements that created modern Germany and Italy from fragmented regions
- **Balkan nationalisms** — Ethnic independence movements that challenged Ottoman control in southeastern Europe
- **Ottomanism** — Imperial ideology attempting to create inclusive Ottoman national identity across diverse ethnic groups
- **Propaganda Movement** — Philippine intellectual movement promoting reforms and eventual independence from Spanish rule
- **Maori nationalism** — Indigenous resistance to British colonization that led to the New Zealand Wars
