## ARTICLE CONTENT

The spread of communism after 1900 represents one of the most significant political transformations in world history, and College Board frequently tests this topic because it demonstrates how economic ideologies can reshape entire societies. Communist movements promised to redistribute wealth and eliminate class differences, appealing to populations suffering under economic inequality and foreign domination.

China's path to communism perfectly illustrates how internal weaknesses and external pressures created revolutionary conditions. The **Chinese Communist Party (CCP)**, founded in 1921, initially struggled against the **Nationalist Party (Kuomintang)** in a brutal civil war. However, **Japanese aggression** during the Second Sino-Japanese War (1937-1945) devastated China and exposed the Nationalist government's inability to defend the country. As the CED emphasizes, these internal tensions and Japanese invasion allowed **Chinese communists to seize power** under **Mao Zedong** in 1949. The Communists gained popular support by promising land redistribution to peasants and presenting themselves as effective fighters against Japanese occupation. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how external threats can strengthen revolutionary movements — the Japanese invasion essentially handed the Communists a nationalist cause that resonated with ordinary Chinese.

Once in power, **communist China** implemented radical economic policies with devastating consequences for its population. The **Great Leap Forward** (1958-1962) represented Mao's attempt to rapidly industrialize China through state control of the economy. The government forced peasants into **People's Communes**, massive collective farms that were supposed to produce both agricultural and industrial goods. College Board loves testing the contrast between communist intentions and outcomes — while the Great Leap Forward promised prosperity, it actually caused the **Great Chinese Famine**, killing an estimated 15-45 million people. The government's **repressive policies** included forcing unrealistic production quotas, punishing anyone who reported failures, and prioritizing steel production over food. This demonstrates how centralized economic planning, a key feature of communist systems, often led to catastrophic results when ideology trumped practical reality.

Beyond China, **movements to redistribute land and resources** emerged across the developing world, often advocating **communism or socialism**. In Latin America, **Cuba's revolution** (1959) under **Fidel Castro** redistributed sugar plantations and established a communist state. African nations like **Angola** and **Mozambique** adopted Marxist-Leninist ideologies during their independence struggles, viewing capitalism as connected to colonial exploitation. In Asia, **North Vietnam** under **Ho Chi Minh** combined communist ideology with anti-colonial nationalism. These movements shared common causes: extreme economic inequality, foreign domination, and the appeal of communist promises to redistribute wealth. However, as you'll see throughout this unit, the gap between communist theory and practice often resulted in authoritarian governments that failed to deliver promised prosperity.

The global spread of communism after 1900 fundamentally reshaped the twentieth century, creating ideological divisions that would define the Cold War era and influence decolonization movements worldwide.

## KEY TERMS
- **Chinese Communist Party (CCP)** — Political party founded in 1921 that seized control of China in 1949
- **Nationalist Party (Kuomintang)** — Chinese political party that fought against the Communists in civil war
- **Mao Zedong** — Leader of Chinese Communist Party who established People's Republic of China
- **Great Leap Forward** — Mao's failed economic campaign (1958-1962) to rapidly industrialize China
- **People's Communes** — Large collective farms created during the Great Leap Forward
- **Great Chinese Famine** — Massive famine (1959-1961) caused by Great Leap Forward policies
- **Repressive policies** — Harsh government measures that restricted freedoms and punished dissent
- **Land redistribution** — Communist policy of taking land from wealthy owners and giving it to peasants
- **Fidel Castro** — Cuban revolutionary who established communist government in 1959
- **Ho Chi Minh** — Vietnamese communist leader who fought against French and American forces
- **Marxist-Leninist** — Communist ideology combining Marx's economic theories with Lenin's political strategies
- **Collective farms** — Agricultural system where land is owned and worked communally rather than individually
- **Central planning** — Economic system where government controls production and distribution decisions

## Bridge to Next Topic
The global spread of communism after 1900 set the stage for the ideological confrontation that would dominate international relations for the next half-century. As communist movements gained power in China, Cuba, Vietnam, and other nations, Western capitalist countries — led by the United States — viewed this expansion as a direct threat to their own political and economic systems. This ideological divide would transform into the Cold War, where the competition between communist and capitalist blocs would reshape global politics, fuel proxy wars, and influence decolonization movements worldwide.
