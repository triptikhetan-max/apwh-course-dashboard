## ARTICLE CONTENT

As we near the end of our AP World History journey, Topic 8.7 examines a crucial pattern: how people resist oppression. While the 20th century was marked by unprecedented violence and authoritarian control, it was equally defined by remarkable acts of resistance. College Board tests this topic because it demonstrates how ordinary people and organized groups challenge power structures — a theme that connects everything from decolonization movements to modern terrorism.

The rise of brutal **authoritarian regimes** after 1900 sparked various forms of resistance. In Chile, General **Augusto Pinochet's military dictatorship** (1973-1990) used torture, disappearances, and economic shock therapy to maintain control, but faced constant opposition from human rights groups, labor unions, and the **Mothers of the Plaza de Mayo**-style protests. Similarly, **Francisco Franco's fascist regime** in Spain (1939-1975) brutally suppressed regional identities, languages, and political opposition, yet underground resistance movements, including Basque separatists and communist cells, persisted throughout his rule. In Uganda, **Idi Amin's reign of terror** (1971-1979) led to the deaths of an estimated 300,000 people, but even under such extreme conditions, exile groups and internal opposition continued organizing. College Board frequently tests how these dictatorships used similar methods — secret police, propaganda, and state violence — while resistance movements adapted different strategies based on their contexts.

Some resistance movements chose violent methods to challenge established orders, often with devastating consequences. Peru's **Shining Path** (Sendero Luminoso) emerged in the 1980s as a Maoist guerrilla movement that killed approximately 69,000 people through bombings, assassinations, and rural terror campaigns, claiming to liberate peasants while actually terrorizing them. The organization's leader, Abimael Guzmán, believed that violence was necessary to destroy Peru's existing order and create a peasant revolution. More globally, **Al-Qaeda** developed after 1988 as a transnational terrorist network opposing Western influence in Muslim lands, orchestrating attacks from the 1993 World Trade Center bombing to 9/11. Both movements justified extreme violence as necessary to overthrow what they saw as corrupt, oppressive systems — but their methods often harmed the very people they claimed to represent.

The growth of the **military-industrial complex** and **weapons trading** created new dynamics of conflict and resistance. As Heimler explains in the video, the Cold War arms race meant that both superpowers and smaller nations invested enormous resources in military capabilities, often at the expense of social programs. This militarization provided tools for both oppression and resistance — authoritarian regimes could purchase sophisticated surveillance and weapons technology, but resistance movements could also access increasingly deadly weapons through international networks. The global arms trade meant that conflicts in places like Afghanistan, Central America, and Africa became proxy battles between superpowers, with local resistance movements receiving external support that prolonged and intensified conflicts.

Understanding these patterns of resistance and conflict is crucial for recognizing how the 20th century's political upheavals continue shaping our contemporary world. The methods, justifications, and international dimensions of these resistance movements established precedents that influence modern conflicts and social movements.

## KEY TERMS
- **Augusto Pinochet** — Chilean military dictator (1973-1990) who used brutal repression against political opposition
- **Francisco Franco** — Spanish fascist dictator (1939-1975) who suppressed regional cultures and democratic opposition
- **Idi Amin** — Ugandan dictator (1971-1979) responsible for approximately 300,000 deaths through systematic violence
- **Shining Path (Sendero Luminoso)** — Peruvian Maoist guerrilla movement that used extreme violence in failed revolution attempt
- **Al-Qaeda** — Transnational terrorist network founded to oppose Western influence in Muslim territories
- **Military-industrial complex** — Relationship between government, military, and defense contractors that promotes military spending
- **Weapons trading** — Global arms trade that supplied conflicts and enabled both state repression and resistance movements
- **Authoritarian regimes** — Non-democratic governments that concentrate power and suppress opposition through violence
- **State terrorism** — Government use of violence and intimidation against its own population
- **Guerrilla warfare** — Irregular military tactics used by smaller forces against conventional armies
- **Proxy conflicts** — Wars where superpowers supported opposing sides without direct confrontation
- **Human rights movements** — Organizations that documented abuses and advocated for political prisoners and victims
