## ARTICLE CONTENT

The period from 1945 to 1991 witnessed unprecedented global transformation as two major forces — the **Cold War** and **decolonization** — reshaped the world order. College Board emphasizes this topic because it demonstrates how ideological conflicts can have remarkably similar effects across different continents, even when the specific contexts vary dramatically. Understanding these parallels is crucial for success on the AP exam, which frequently asks students to compare Cold War impacts in different regions.

The **proxy wars** that defined the Cold War era reveal striking similarities between the Eastern and Western Hemispheres. In Asia, the **Korean War** (1950-1953) and **Vietnam War** (1955-1975) saw the United States and Soviet Union fighting through local allies, devastating entire nations while the superpowers avoided direct confrontation. Similarly, in Latin America, conflicts like the **Nicaraguan Civil War** and **Salvadoran Civil War** during the 1970s-1980s followed identical patterns — the USSR supported leftist rebels while the US backed right-wing governments, regardless of their human rights records. In Africa, the **Angolan Civil War** and conflicts in Somalia and Ethiopia demonstrated the same superpower rivalry playing out through local proxies. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking you to identify these parallel patterns of **superpower intervention** across different continents.

The process of **decolonization** accelerated dramatically after World War II, but the Cold War context shaped how new nations developed. In both hemispheres, newly independent states found themselves forced to choose sides in the ideological struggle, even when their primary concerns were economic development and nation-building. Countries like **India** under Nehru and **Egypt** under Nasser attempted to navigate this through the **Non-Aligned Movement**, seeking aid from both superpowers without fully committing to either. However, many nations found this neutrality impossible to maintain. In Africa, countries like **Ghana** under Nkrumah initially embraced socialist policies with Soviet support, while others like **Kenya** aligned with Western capitalist models. College Board loves testing how these choices affected economic development — socialist-aligned nations often struggled with inefficient state-controlled economies, while Western-aligned nations frequently dealt with **structural adjustment programs** that prioritized debt repayment over social welfare.

The economic dimensions of Cold War competition created remarkably similar patterns worldwide. Both superpowers used **foreign aid** as a weapon, offering development assistance to win allies. The **Marshall Plan** rebuilt Western Europe along capitalist lines, while the Soviet Union provided similar assistance to Eastern European satellite states and developing nations that embraced socialism. This competition actually benefited some developing nations who could play the superpowers against each other — South Korea and Taiwan received massive US investment to showcase capitalism's success, while Cuba and North Korea became Soviet showcases. However, when the Cold War ended in 1991, many of these artificially supported economies collapsed, leading to the **structural adjustment programs** of the 1990s that imposed free-market reforms regardless of social costs.

The cultural and social impacts of the Cold War also transcended hemispheric boundaries. **McCarthyism** in the United States paralleled political purges in Latin American military dictatorships, both justified as necessary measures against communist infiltration. The **Space Race** inspired scientific education worldwide, while **propaganda campaigns** shaped popular culture from Hollywood movies to Soviet ballet. Perhaps most significantly, the Cold War created a global language of human rights and democracy that both superpowers claimed to represent, even as they frequently supported authoritarian allies when strategically convenient.

Understanding these causation patterns is essential because they help explain why the post-Cold War world developed as it did, setting up the globalization trends you'll study in the final unit.

## KEY TERMS
- **Cold War** — Ideological conflict (1945-1991) between capitalist and communist blocs that shaped global politics
- **Decolonization** — Process of former colonies gaining independence, accelerated after World War II
- **Proxy wars** — Conflicts where superpowers supported opposing sides without direct military confrontation
- **Korean War** — First major proxy war (1950-1953) establishing Cold War conflict patterns
- **Vietnam War** — Extended proxy conflict (1955-1975) that demonstrated limits of superpower intervention
- **Non-Aligned Movement** — Group of nations attempting neutrality in Cold War, led by India, Egypt, and Yugoslavia
- **Superpower intervention** — Pattern of US and Soviet involvement in regional conflicts to expand influence
- **Marshall Plan** — US economic aid program (1947) that rebuilt Western Europe along capitalist lines
- **Foreign aid** — Economic assistance used by both superpowers as diplomatic weapons to win allies
- **Structural adjustment programs** — Free-market economic reforms imposed by international lenders in 1990s
- **McCarthyism** — US anti-communist political persecution that paralleled similar campaigns globally
- **Space Race** — Superpower competition in space exploration that inspired global scientific advancement
- **Propaganda campaigns** — Information warfare used by both sides to promote their ideological systems
