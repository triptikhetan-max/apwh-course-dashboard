## ARTICLE CONTENT

The **Cold War** (1945-1991) emerged as the defining global conflict of the post-World War II era, but unlike previous wars, this was primarily an **ideological struggle** between two fundamentally different ways of organizing society. College Board frequently tests your understanding of how this wasn't just a political conflict — it was a battle between **capitalism** (represented by the United States) and **communism** (represented by the Soviet Union) that reshaped every aspect of global politics, economics, and culture.

The roots of the Cold War lay in the power vacuum left after World War II. As the video explains, both the United States and Soviet Union emerged as **superpowers** with massive military capabilities, including nuclear weapons, but their visions for the post-war world were completely incompatible. The US promoted **democratic governance** with free market capitalism, emphasizing individual rights and private property. Meanwhile, the Soviet Union championed **authoritarian communist** systems with state-controlled economies and collective ownership. This ideological divide quickly evolved from wartime cooperation into global competition, as each superpower sought to expand its sphere of influence and prevent the other's ideology from spreading.

The Cold War's effects extended far beyond the superpowers themselves, creating what historians call a **bipolar world order**. The **Marshall Plan** (1947) provided American economic aid to rebuild Western Europe along capitalist lines, while the Soviet Union established **satellite states** in Eastern Europe under communist control. The formation of military alliances like **NATO** (1949) and the **Warsaw Pact** (1955) formalized this division. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how this superpower rivalry played out in **proxy wars** across Asia, Africa, and Latin America — from Korea and Vietnam to Angola and Nicaragua — where local conflicts became battlegrounds for competing ideologies without direct US-Soviet confrontation.

However, not all nations wanted to choose sides in this ideological struggle. The **Non-Aligned Movement**, founded at the **Bandung Conference** (1955) and formalized by leaders like India's Nehru, Egypt's Nasser, and Yugoslavia's Tito, represented a "third way" between capitalism and communism. These nations, many of them newly independent from colonial rule, sought to maintain sovereignty while accepting aid from both superpowers. College Board loves testing how the Non-Aligned Movement challenged the bipolar world order by promoting **neutrality** and **South-South cooperation**, offering developing nations an alternative to choosing between the superpowers.

The Cold War's cultural dimensions were equally important, as both sides used **propaganda**, **education**, and **cultural exchanges** to promote their ideologies. From the **Space Race** to **Hollywood films** versus **Soviet cinema**, every aspect of culture became a battlefield for hearts and minds. Understanding the Cold War is essential because it explains the foundations of our modern world — from international organizations to regional conflicts to economic systems that persist today.

## KEY TERMS
- **Cold War** — Ideological and political conflict (1945-1991) between the United States and Soviet Union without direct military confrontation
- **Ideological struggle** — Competition between fundamentally different belief systems about how society should be organized
- **Capitalism** — Economic system based on private ownership, free markets, and individual profit
- **Communism** — Economic and political system advocating collective ownership and state control of production
- **Superpowers** — Nations with global influence through military, economic, and political power
- **Democratic governance** — Political system emphasizing individual rights, elections, and representative government
- **Authoritarian communist** — Political system with single-party control and state-directed economy
- **Bipolar world order** — International system dominated by two major powers with opposing ideologies
- **Marshall Plan** — US program (1947) providing economic aid to rebuild Western Europe
- **Satellite states** — Eastern European nations under Soviet political and economic control
- **NATO** — Western military alliance (1949) led by the United States
- **Warsaw Pact** — Eastern military alliance (1955) led by the Soviet Union
- **Proxy wars** — Conflicts where superpowers supported opposing sides without direct confrontation
- **Non-Aligned Movement** — Coalition of nations refusing to align with either superpower bloc
- **Bandung Conference** — 1955 meeting of Asian and African nations promoting neutrality and cooperation
