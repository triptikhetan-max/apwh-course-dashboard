## ARTICLE CONTENT

The wave of **decolonization** after World War II created dozens of new nations, but independence brought unexpected challenges that College Board frequently tests. When European powers withdrew from Africa, Asia, and the Middle East, they left behind **artificial boundaries** drawn for colonial administrative convenience, not ethnic, religious, or cultural reality. These boundaries became the borders of new nations, creating immediate problems that persist today.

The most dramatic example occurred during the **Partition of India** in 1947, when British India split into India and Pakistan based on religious lines. This **demographic reorganization** displaced over 14 million people and killed hundreds of thousands in communal violence. Similarly, when Belgium abruptly abandoned the Congo in 1960, they left behind a territory larger than Western Europe with over 200 ethnic groups forced into a single state. College Board loves testing how these **arbitrary colonial boundaries** created ongoing conflicts — you'll see questions about how ethnic groups were divided across new borders or forced together into unstable nations.

In Africa, the redrawing of boundaries after colonial withdrawal created particularly complex situations. The **Organization of African Unity** (OAU), founded in 1963, made the crucial decision to respect colonial boundaries to prevent endless border wars, even though these boundaries often ignored traditional ethnic territories. This meant that groups like the Yoruba were split between Nigeria and Benin, while Nigeria itself contained over 250 ethnic groups with different languages, religions, and customs. On the AP exam, you'll often encounter questions about how this **territorial inheritance** from colonialism contributed to civil wars like the **Nigerian Civil War** (1967-1970) or ongoing conflicts in Sudan and Somalia.

Economically, newly independent states faced the challenge of transforming **colonial economies** designed to extract raw materials for European factories. Most inherited economies focused on exporting one or two primary products — Ghana depended on cocoa, Zambia on copper, and many Middle Eastern states on oil. To address this vulnerability, governments adopted **state-directed development** strategies, taking strong roles in planning economic growth. India's **Five-Year Plans** modeled on Soviet planning emphasized heavy industry and self-sufficiency through **import substitution industrialization**. Tanzania's Julius Nyerere promoted **African socialism** (Ujamaa) emphasizing collective agriculture and reduced dependence on former colonial masters.

These **mixed economies** combined private enterprise with extensive government control, reflecting both practical needs and ideological preferences during the Cold War era. Egypt's Gamal Abdel Nasser nationalized the Suez Canal and promoted pan-Arab socialism, while Indonesia's Sukarno developed **guided democracy** with strong state economic intervention. College Board frequently tests how these newly independent states navigated between capitalist and communist models, often creating their own hybrid systems like India's **non-aligned movement** that sought aid from both superpowers while maintaining political independence.

Understanding newly independent states is crucial because their struggles with **nation-building**, economic development, and managing ethnic diversity became defining features of the post-colonial world and continue to influence global politics today.

## KEY TERMS
- **Decolonization** — Process by which colonies gained independence from European powers after WWII
- **Artificial boundaries** — Colonial borders drawn for administrative convenience, ignoring ethnic and cultural divisions
- **Partition of India** — 1947 division of British India into India and Pakistan based on religious lines
- **Demographic reorganization** — Large-scale population movements following political boundary changes
- **Arbitrary colonial boundaries** — Borders established by colonial powers without regard for local populations
- **Organization of African Unity (OAU)** — 1963 alliance that agreed to respect colonial boundaries despite their problems
- **Territorial inheritance** — Political boundaries and administrative structures left behind by colonial powers
- **Nigerian Civil War** — 1967-1970 conflict arising from ethnic tensions within artificially created borders
- **Colonial economies** — Economic systems designed to extract raw materials for European industrial use
- **State-directed development** — Government-led economic planning and industrialization strategies
- **Five-Year Plans** — India's Soviet-inspired economic development programs emphasizing heavy industry
- **Import substitution industrialization** — Strategy of producing goods domestically instead of importing them
- **African socialism (Ujamaa)** — Tanzania's blend of traditional communal values with socialist economic principles
- **Mixed economies** — Economic systems combining private enterprise with significant government control
- **Non-aligned movement** — Policy of maintaining independence from both US and Soviet blocs during Cold War
