## ARTICLE CONTENT

The collapse of European empires after 1900 represents one of the most dramatic transformations in world history, and College Board loves testing this because it shows how the same goal — independence — was achieved through completely different methods. As you'll see, some colonies negotiated their way to freedom while others fought bloody wars, and understanding why these differences occurred is crucial for the AP exam.

**Nationalist movements** emerged across Asia and Africa as educated elites, often trained in European universities, returned home with ideas about self-determination and democratic governance. In India, the **Indian National Congress** (founded 1885) initially sought greater autonomy within the British Empire, while leaders like **Mahatma Gandhi** developed **non-violent resistance** strategies including boycotts, strikes, and civil disobedience. Gandhi's **Salt March** (1930) perfectly exemplified how colonized peoples could challenge imperial authority without violence. Similarly, in Africa, leaders like **Kwame Nkrumah** in Ghana and **Leopold Senghor** in Senegal initially worked within colonial systems, forming political parties and demanding representation. College Board frequently asks you to compare these peaceful approaches with more militant strategies, so remember that the choice of tactics often depended on how willing the colonial power was to negotiate.

The aftermath of **World War II** created a perfect storm for decolonization that dramatically accelerated independence movements. European powers emerged economically devastated and morally weakened after fighting a war supposedly about freedom and democracy while denying those same rights to their colonies. The **United Nations Charter** (1945) explicitly supported self-determination, giving colonized peoples international legal backing for their independence claims. In this context, some colonies successfully negotiated independence relatively peacefully. **India and Pakistan** gained independence in 1947 through British parliamentary action (though partition led to massive violence), while many **British African colonies** like Ghana (1957) and Nigeria (1960) followed similar negotiated paths. **French colonies** in West Africa mostly achieved independence through the **French Community** system around 1960, maintaining close economic ties with France.

However, where colonial powers refused to negotiate or where settler populations resisted majority rule, **armed liberation movements** emerged. In Algeria, the **National Liberation Front (FLN)** fought a brutal eight-year war (1954-1962) against France, which considered Algeria an integral part of French territory. The **Mau Mau Uprising** in Kenya (1952-1960) saw the Kikuyu people fight British rule and white settler dominance through guerrilla warfare. In Southeast Asia, **Ho Chi Minh** led Vietnamese forces against first the French and then the Americans in conflicts lasting decades. College Board often tests your ability to explain why these particular regions experienced violent decolonization — typically because the colonial power had significant economic investments (like French settlers in Algeria) or strategic interests (like British tea plantations in Kenya) that they were unwilling to abandon.

The success of these independence movements created a domino effect that spread across the globe, with newly independent nations supporting liberation movements elsewhere and providing evidence that European colonial rule could be successfully challenged. This pattern of decolonization fundamentally reshaped the international system and set the stage for Cold War competition in the developing world.

## KEY TERMS
- **Nationalist movements** — Political organizations seeking self-rule or independence from colonial powers
- **Indian National Congress** — Political party founded in 1885 that led India's independence movement
- **Mahatma Gandhi** — Indian leader who developed non-violent resistance strategies against British rule
- **Non-violent resistance** — Strategy of opposing authority through peaceful methods like boycotts and civil disobedience
- **Salt March** — Gandhi's 1930 protest against British salt monopoly that exemplified civil disobedience
- **Kwame Nkrumah** — Ghanaian leader who led his country to independence and promoted Pan-Africanism
- **Leopold Senghor** — Senegalese poet and politician who became first president of independent Senegal
- **United Nations Charter** — 1945 document that supported principle of national self-determination
- **French Community** — System allowing French colonies to gain independence while maintaining economic ties
- **National Liberation Front (FLN)** — Algerian organization that fought an eight-year war for independence from France
- **Mau Mau Uprising** — Kenyan rebellion (1952-1960) against British colonial rule and white settlers
- **Ho Chi Minh** — Vietnamese communist leader who fought for independence from France and later the United States
- **Armed liberation movements** — Organizations that used military force to achieve independence from colonial rule
