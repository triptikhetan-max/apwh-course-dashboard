## ARTICLE CONTENT

World War II didn't just end in 1945 — it completely reshaped the global balance of power and set the stage for the two defining movements of the next half-century: the **Cold War** and **decolonization**. College Board loves testing this topic because it shows how one massive conflict can create the conditions for entirely new forms of global tension and liberation movements.

The war's end left only two true **superpowers** standing: the United States and Soviet Union. Both emerged with dramatically enhanced technological capabilities and economic strength, but their wartime alliance quickly crumbled over fundamental disagreements about how to rebuild the world. The United States, with its economy booming from wartime production and possessing nuclear weapons, promoted **capitalism** and **democratic governance** globally. Meanwhile, the Soviet Union, despite massive wartime losses, had expanded its influence across Eastern Europe and promoted **communist ideology** as an alternative to Western capitalism. As the video explains, these competing visions for post-war reconstruction created the ideological foundation for Cold War tensions. College Board frequently tests how these **technological and economic gains** shifted power away from traditional European empires toward these two superpowers, fundamentally altering international relations.

Simultaneously, World War II accelerated the collapse of European colonial empires by exposing their weaknesses and strengthening **anti-imperialist sentiment** worldwide. The **Atlantic Charter** (1941), signed by Britain and the United States, promised "self-determination" for all peoples — a principle that colonial subjects took seriously even when the colonial powers didn't intend it for them. Additionally, many colonized peoples had fought for their European rulers during the war, expecting political rights in return. When these expectations went largely unfulfilled after 1945 (just as they had after World War I), it fueled massive independence movements across Asia and Africa. The war had also demonstrated that European powers weren't invincible — Japan's rapid conquest of European colonies in Southeast Asia shattered the myth of white racial superiority that had helped justify imperialism.

The **economic disruption** caused by World War II further weakened European colonial powers. Britain, France, and other colonial empires emerged from the war financially exhausted and unable to maintain expensive overseas territories. Simultaneously, both superpowers — despite their other disagreements — generally opposed traditional European colonialism, though for different reasons. The United States wanted access to global markets previously restricted by colonial trade barriers, while the Soviet Union supported anti-colonial movements as part of its broader opposition to Western capitalism. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how this convergence of factors — weakened colonial powers, strengthened independence movements, and superpower opposition to traditional empires — created the perfect storm for decolonization.

Understanding these post-1945 developments requires recognizing the crucial continuity with unfulfilled promises after World War I. The **mandate system** established by the League of Nations had promised eventual self-government for former Ottoman and German territories, but these promises remained largely unfulfilled through the 1920s and 1930s. This pattern of broken promises created deep skepticism among colonized peoples about European intentions, making them more willing to demand immediate independence rather than gradual reforms after World War II. College Board emphasizes this long-term context because it shows how the failure to address legitimate political aspirations after WWI contributed to more radical demands for complete independence after WWII.

## KEY TERMS
- **Cold War** — Ideological and geopolitical tension between the United States and Soviet Union from 1945-1991
- **Superpowers** — The United States and Soviet Union, the two dominant global powers after WWII
- **Capitalism** — Economic system promoted by the US emphasizing private property and free markets
- **Communist ideology** — Political and economic system promoted by the USSR emphasizing state control and equality
- **Decolonization** — Process by which European colonies gained independence after WWII
- **Anti-imperialist sentiment** — Growing opposition to European colonial rule among colonized peoples
- **Atlantic Charter** — 1941 agreement between Britain and the US promising self-determination for all peoples
- **Self-determination** — Principle that peoples should choose their own form of government
- **Mandate system** — League of Nations arrangement giving European powers temporary control over former Ottoman and German territories
- **Nuclear weapons** — Atomic bombs first used by the US in 1945, giving it initial technological superiority
- **Economic disruption** — Financial devastation of European powers during WWII that weakened their ability to maintain empires
- **Technological capabilities** — Advanced military and industrial technologies developed during WWII
- **Democratic governance** — Political system promoted by the US emphasizing elections and individual rights
