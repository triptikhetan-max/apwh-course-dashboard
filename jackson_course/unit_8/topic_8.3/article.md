## ARTICLE CONTENT

The Cold War wasn't just a standoff between two superpowers — it was a global competition that reshaped the entire world from 1945 to 1991. College Board loves testing this topic because it demonstrates how ideological conflicts can drive international relations and affect newly independent nations worldwide. Understanding how the United States and Soviet Union competed for influence will help you analyze modern geopolitics and connect decolonization to global power struggles.

Both superpowers created opposing military alliances to project their influence globally. The United States established the **North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO)** in 1949, bringing together Western European nations, Canada, and later Turkey and Greece under a collective security agreement. This alliance provided American military protection to Western Europe while containing Soviet expansion. The Soviet Union responded by creating the **Warsaw Pact** in 1955, uniting Eastern European communist states under Soviet military command. College Board frequently tests how these alliances represented different ideological approaches — NATO emphasized voluntary membership among democratic allies, while the Warsaw Pact enforced Soviet control over satellite states. Both alliances extended beyond Europe, with the US creating additional partnerships like **SEATO** (Southeast Asia Treaty Organization) and bilateral defense agreements throughout Asia and Latin America.

The development of nuclear weapons fundamentally changed how the superpowers competed. **Nuclear proliferation** began with the US atomic bomb in 1945, followed by the Soviet bomb in 1949, creating a **nuclear arms race** that included Britain (1952), France (1960), and China (1964). The doctrine of **Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD)** meant that direct military confrontation between the superpowers became too dangerous, forcing them to compete through indirect means. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how nuclear weapons paradoxically created both global tension and strategic stability — neither superpower could risk direct war, but both constantly prepared for it through massive military buildup.

Unable to fight each other directly, the US and USSR engaged in **proxy wars** throughout the developing world, particularly in newly decolonized nations. In Latin America, the US supported anti-communist governments and movements, intervening directly in Guatemala (1954) and Chile (1973), while backing the **Contras** in Nicaragua during the 1980s. The Soviet Union supported the **Cuban Revolution** and various socialist movements across the region. In Africa, both superpowers competed intensely — the US and USSR backed opposing sides in the **Angolan Civil War** (1975-2002), while the **Congo Crisis** (1960-1965) saw both powers supporting different factions. Asian proxy conflicts included the **Korean War** (1950-1953), where US-led UN forces fought Chinese and North Korean troops, and the **Vietnam War** (1955-1975), where the US directly intervened to prevent communist victory. These conflicts were devastating for local populations but allowed superpowers to test weapons, strategies, and influence without triggering nuclear war.

The effects of Cold War competition extended far beyond military conflicts. Both superpowers used economic aid, cultural exchange, and ideological propaganda to win allies among newly independent nations. The **Marshall Plan** helped rebuild Western Europe under American influence, while Soviet technical assistance supported industrial development in allies like Egypt and India. College Board emphasizes that this competition often forced developing nations to choose sides, limiting their independence even as they escaped colonial rule.

## KEY TERMS
- **North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO)** — US-led military alliance formed in 1949 for collective defense against Soviet expansion
- **Warsaw Pact** — Soviet-led military alliance of Eastern European communist states formed in 1955
- **Nuclear proliferation** — The spread of nuclear weapons to additional countries during the Cold War
- **Nuclear arms race** — Competition between superpowers to build increasingly powerful nuclear arsenals
- **Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD)** — Doctrine that nuclear war would destroy both sides, deterring direct conflict
- **Proxy wars** — Conflicts where superpowers supported opposing sides without directly fighting each other
- **Cuban Revolution** — 1959 communist revolution led by Fidel Castro, creating a Soviet ally in the Americas
- **Contras** — US-backed anti-communist rebels who fought Nicaragua's Sandinista government in the 1980s
- **Angolan Civil War** — Long conflict (1975-2002) where US and USSR backed opposing factions
- **Congo Crisis** — Post-independence conflict (1960-1965) involving superpower competition in Central Africa
- **Korean War** — First major proxy war (1950-1953) between US-led and communist forces
- **Vietnam War** — Major US military intervention (1955-1975) to prevent communist victory in Southeast Asia
- **Marshall Plan** — US economic aid program that rebuilt Western Europe while extending American influence
- **SEATO** — Southeast Asia Treaty Organization, US-led alliance to contain communism in Asia
