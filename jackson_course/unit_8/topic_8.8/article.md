## ARTICLE CONTENT

After nearly five decades of global tension, the **Cold War** ended not with nuclear catastrophe, but with the collapse of one of the world's superpowers. The period from 1979-1991 saw a cascade of failures that brought down the Soviet Union and transformed the global political landscape. College Board frequently tests this topic because it demonstrates how military overextension, technological gaps, economic stagnation, and popular resistance can topple even the most powerful empires.

The Soviet Union's **invasion of Afghanistan** in 1979 became what many historians call "the Soviet Union's Vietnam." This costly military intervention aimed to prop up a communist government in Kabul, but instead trapped Soviet forces in a brutal guerrilla war against **mujahideen** fighters. The conflict drained enormous resources from an already struggling Soviet economy — estimates suggest the war cost over $15 billion annually. More importantly, it exposed the limitations of Soviet military power and created massive public discontent as body bags returned home. The **Afghan War** lasted a devastating ten years (1979-1989), weakening Soviet prestige both domestically and internationally. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions connecting this military failure to broader patterns of imperial overextension that you've studied throughout the course.

Meanwhile, advances in **U.S. military and technological development** created an arms race the Soviets couldn't win. President Ronald Reagan's **Strategic Defense Initiative (SDI)**, nicknamed "Star Wars," proposed a space-based missile defense system that would have required cutting-edge technology the USSR simply couldn't match. While SDI was never fully implemented, it forced Soviet leaders to realize they were falling dangerously behind in the technological competition. The personal computer revolution, satellite communications, and precision-guided weapons all demonstrated American technological superiority. College Board loves testing how this "**technology gap**" pressured Soviet leaders like **Mikhail Gorbachev** to pursue reforms rather than continue the expensive arms race.

Economic weakness and public discontent created the final nails in the Soviet coffin. The **command economy** that had industrialized Russia in the 1930s proved incapable of producing consumer goods or adapting to technological change. By the 1980s, Soviet citizens faced chronic shortages of basic items like food, clothing, and household goods, while black market prices soared. **Glasnost** (openness) and **perestroika** (restructuring), Gorbachev's reform policies introduced after 1985, were desperate attempts to revitalize the system. However, these policies backfired by allowing open criticism of the government for the first time in decades. When people could finally speak freely, they demanded fundamental change, not just reform.

The **fall of the Berlin Wall** in November 1989 symbolized the broader collapse of communist control across Eastern Europe. Popular movements in Poland (**Solidarity**), Hungary, and Czechoslovakia (**Velvet Revolution**) overthrew communist governments with surprising speed. Gorbachev's decision not to use military force to preserve these satellite states marked a dramatic break from previous Soviet policy. The Soviet Union itself dissolved in December 1991, ending the Cold War and leaving the United States as the world's sole superpower. Understanding this collapse helps you see how internal contradictions and external pressures can bring down even the most seemingly powerful states.

## KEY TERMS
- **Cold War** — Global ideological conflict (1945-1991) between the United States and Soviet Union
- **Soviet invasion of Afghanistan** — Failed military intervention (1979-1989) that drained Soviet resources
- **Mujahideen** — Afghan guerrilla fighters who resisted Soviet occupation with U.S. support
- **Strategic Defense Initiative (SDI)** — Reagan's proposed space-based missile defense system
- **Technology gap** — Growing disparity between U.S. and Soviet technological capabilities
- **Mikhail Gorbachev** — Soviet leader (1985-1991) who introduced reforms that accelerated Soviet collapse
- **Command economy** — Soviet centrally-planned economic system that proved inflexible
- **Glasnost** — Policy of openness allowing free speech and criticism in the Soviet Union
- **Perestroika** — Economic restructuring policy aimed at revitalizing the Soviet system
- **Fall of the Berlin Wall** — 1989 event symbolizing the end of communist control in Eastern Europe
- **Solidarity** — Polish trade union movement that challenged communist rule
- **Velvet Revolution** — Peaceful overthrow of communist government in Czechoslovakia (1989)
