## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to one of College Board's favorite comparison topics! The period from 1200 to 1450 CE witnessed an explosion of trade networks that connected Afro-Eurasia like never before. Understanding how these networks compared — their similarities and differences — is crucial for the AP exam because College Board loves testing your ability to analyze multiple trade systems simultaneously and explain their varying impacts on different regions.

The major trade networks of this period shared several key similarities that College Board wants you to recognize. First, **improved commercial practices** revolutionized trade across all networks. The **Silk Roads** benefited from innovations like **caravanserai** (roadside inns), standardized currencies, and credit systems. Maritime networks in the **Indian Ocean** used **monsoon winds** and improved ship designs, while **trans-Saharan routes** employed **camel caravans** and oasis trading posts. Second, all networks experienced **increased volume of trade** and **expanded geographical range**. The Silk Roads stretched from China to the Mediterranean, Indian Ocean routes connected East Africa to Southeast Asia, and trans-Saharan paths linked North and West Africa. Third, these networks promoted the **growth of powerful new trading cities** — think **Samarkand** on the Silk Roads, **Malacca** in the Indian Ocean, and **Timbuktu** in West Africa. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking you to identify these similarities across different regions.

However, College Board also tests the crucial differences between these networks. The **Silk Roads** primarily moved luxury goods overland using **camel and horse caravans**, making transportation expensive and limiting bulk goods. In contrast, **maritime networks** in the Indian Ocean could transport larger quantities more efficiently, leading to greater volume of everyday goods alongside luxuries. The **trans-Saharan routes** specialized in specific commodities like **gold and salt**, while Mediterranean networks focused on manufactured goods and spices. Additionally, each network faced different challenges: Silk Road merchants dealt with bandits and political instability, Indian Ocean traders navigated seasonal monsoons and pirates, while trans-Saharan commerce required surviving harsh desert conditions.

The increasing **demand for luxury goods** across Afro-Eurasia created a feedback loop that College Board considers essential knowledge. **Chinese artisans** expanded production of **textiles and porcelains for export**, with Chinese silk and ceramics becoming status symbols from Europe to Africa. **Persian craftsmen** specialized in carpets and metalwork, while **Indian merchants** dominated cotton textile production and spice trade. The **manufacture of iron and steel expanded in China**, supporting both domestic needs and export markets. This increased productive capacity had major social implications — merchant classes gained wealth and status, while specialized craft production created new urban centers and altered traditional social hierarchies.

Understanding these trade network comparisons is essential because they demonstrate how economic exchange drives historical change through cultural diffusion, technological innovation, and social transformation. These patterns will help you analyze the dramatic changes coming with the Age of Exploration, when European maritime networks will challenge these established Afro-Eurasian systems.

## KEY TERMS
- **Improved commercial practices** — Innovations like banking, credit systems, and standardized currencies that facilitated trade
- **Silk Roads** — Overland trade networks connecting East Asia to the Mediterranean via Central Asia
- **Caravanserai** — Roadside inns providing shelter and services for merchants and travelers
- **Indian Ocean trade networks** — Maritime routes connecting East Africa, Middle East, India, and Southeast Asia
- **Monsoon winds** — Seasonal wind patterns that enabled predictable sailing schedules in the Indian Ocean
- **Trans-Saharan trade routes** — Overland networks connecting North and West Africa across the Sahara Desert
- **Camel caravans** — Groups of camels used to transport goods across desert regions
- **Trading cities** — Urban centers that emerged at strategic points along trade routes
- **Samarkand** — Major Silk Road trading city in Central Asia
- **Malacca** — Strategic port city controlling maritime trade in Southeast Asia
- **Timbuktu** — Important trading center in West Africa along trans-Saharan routes
- **Demand for luxury goods** — Increased desire for high-value items across Afro-Eurasian societies
- **Textiles and porcelains for export** — Chinese manufactured goods that became major trade commodities
- **Iron and steel production** — Manufacturing sector that expanded to meet growing trade demands
