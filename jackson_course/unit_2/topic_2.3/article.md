## ARTICLE CONTENT

The **Indian Ocean trade network** became the world's most important maritime commercial system from 1200-1450, and College Board wants you to understand exactly why this happened when it did. Unlike the overland Silk Roads, the Indian Ocean routes depended heavily on environmental knowledge — specifically understanding the **monsoon winds** that predictably changed direction seasonally. From April to October, monsoons blew from southwest to northeast, carrying merchants from the Arabian Peninsula and East Africa toward India and Southeast Asia. From November to March, the winds reversed, bringing traders back home. This wasn't just convenient — it was revolutionary, allowing merchants to plan reliable round-trip voyages that could take advantage of seasonal demand in different regions.

College Board loves testing the improved technologies that made Indian Ocean trade boom after 1200. The **dhow**, with its triangular lateen sails, could efficiently harness monsoon winds from multiple directions. The **compass** (originally from China) allowed for more precise navigation, while the **astrolabe** helped sailors determine their position using celestial bodies. Most importantly, these improvements dramatically increased the volume and geographic range of trade. Ships could carry more cargo over longer distances with greater safety, connecting East Africa, the Middle East, India, Southeast Asia, and China in an integrated commercial network. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking you to explain how these technological innovations caused the expansion of existing trade routes.

The massive increase in trade volume led to the growth of powerful new **trading cities** that College Board considers essential knowledge. The **city-states of the Swahili coast** — including Kilwa, Mogadishu, and Malindi — became wealthy intermediaries between the African interior and Indian Ocean merchants. These cities exported gold, ivory, and enslaved people while importing luxury goods from Asia. **Gujarat** in western India emerged as a crucial manufacturing and trading hub, producing high-quality textiles that were in demand across the network. The **Sultanate of Malacca** controlled the strategic Strait of Malacca, the narrow waterway between the Indian Ocean and South China Sea, making it incredibly wealthy through trade taxes and port fees.

Perhaps most importantly for the AP exam, College Board emphasizes how increased trade led to the formation of **diasporic communities** — groups of merchants who settled permanently in foreign trading cities. These communities created fascinating cultural blends as merchants introduced their own traditions while adopting local customs. Arab and Persian merchants brought Islam to Southeast Asia and the Swahili coast, but local cultures shaped how these religions were practiced. Chinese merchants in Malacca maintained their cultural identity while adopting local business practices. This cultural exchange accelerated during **Chinese maritime expeditions led by Ming Admiral Zheng He** (1405-1433), which College Board wants you to know demonstrated Chinese naval capabilities while promoting diplomatic and commercial relationships across the Indian Ocean.

Understanding Indian Ocean trade networks is crucial because they demonstrate how environmental knowledge, technological innovation, and cultural exchange work together to create economic growth — patterns you'll see repeated when we examine Atlantic trade networks in later periods.

## KEY TERMS
- **Indian Ocean trade network** — Maritime commercial system connecting East Africa, Middle East, India, Southeast Asia, and China
- **Monsoon winds** — Seasonal wind patterns that reversed direction predictably, enabling reliable round-trip voyages
- **Dhow** — Ship with triangular lateen sails designed to efficiently use monsoon winds from multiple directions
- **Compass** — Chinese navigation instrument that allowed more precise maritime navigation
- **Astrolabe** — Navigation tool using celestial bodies to determine location and direction
- **City-states of the Swahili coast** — Trading cities like Kilwa and Mogadishu that became wealthy intermediaries in Indian Ocean trade
- **Gujarat** — Western Indian region that became a major manufacturing and trading hub for textiles
- **Sultanate of Malacca** — Southeast Asian state controlling the strategic Strait of Malacca and its trade taxes
- **Diasporic communities** — Groups of merchants who settled permanently in foreign trading cities
- **Zheng He** — Ming Chinese admiral who led maritime expeditions (1405-1433) promoting Chinese influence in Indian Ocean
- **Lateen sails** — Triangular sail design that could catch wind from multiple directions
- **Strait of Malacca** — Narrow waterway between Indian Ocean and South China Sea, crucial for maritime trade
