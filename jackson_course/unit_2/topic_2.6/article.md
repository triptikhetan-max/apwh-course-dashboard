## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome back! Now that we've explored how trade networks connected the world from 1200-1450, it's time to examine their environmental consequences. College Board wants you to understand that increased connectivity didn't just move luxury goods and ideas — it fundamentally changed ecosystems and human populations across Afro-Eurasia through the **diffusion of crops and pathogens**.

The positive environmental consequences of connectivity came through **crop diffusion**, which College Board considers essential knowledge. **Bananas**, originally from Southeast Asia, spread along Indian Ocean trade routes to reach Africa, where they became a crucial food source in tropical regions. This wasn't just about adding variety to diets — bananas could grow in areas where traditional grains struggled, supporting larger populations and more complex societies. In East Asia, **new rice varieties** moved along both land and sea routes, with innovations like **Champa rice** (which you learned about in Song China) spreading throughout the region. These drought-resistant, fast-ripening varieties allowed for multiple harvests per year and cultivation in previously unsuitable areas. Meanwhile, **citrus fruits** spread throughout the Mediterranean via Islamic trade networks, transforming agriculture in Southern Europe and North Africa. On the AP exam, remember that crop diffusion generally had positive effects — it increased food security, supported population growth, and allowed societies to thrive in diverse environments.

However, the same trade networks that carried beneficial crops also transported deadly **pathogens**, creating one of history's most devastating environmental consequences. The **bubonic plague** (also called the **Black Death**) represents the perfect example College Board uses to show connectivity's dark side. Originating in Central Asia around 1347, plague bacteria traveled along the **Silk Roads** and **Indian Ocean routes**, carried by infected fleas on rats that lived on merchant ships and caravans. The disease reached Europe by 1347, killing an estimated one-third of the population by 1351. This wasn't just a European disaster — the plague devastated populations across Asia, the Middle East, and North Africa. College Board frequently tests your understanding that increased trade meant increased disease transmission, fundamentally altering demographics across Eurasia.

The environmental consequences of connectivity created a pattern you'll see throughout world history: **biological exchange** through trade networks brings both opportunities and catastrophes. The same merchant vessels that carried valuable spices and silks also transported rats, fleas, bacteria, and seeds that would reshape entire continents. This demonstrates how human activity — in this case, expanded trade — can have unintended environmental consequences that affect millions of people.

Understanding these environmental consequences prepares you for later topics where biological exchange becomes even more dramatic, particularly when we examine how European exploration after 1450 will create global biological exchanges that transform entire hemispheres.

## KEY TERMS
- **Diffusion of crops** — Spread of agricultural plants along trade routes, improving food security
- **Diffusion of pathogens** — Transmission of disease-causing organisms through trade networks
- **Bananas in Africa** — Southeast Asian crop that spread via Indian Ocean trade to become staple food
- **New rice varieties in East Asia** — Improved rice strains that spread along trade routes, increasing yields
- **Champa rice** — Fast-ripening, drought-resistant rice variety that supported population growth
- **Citrus in the Mediterranean** — Fruits spread by Islamic traders that transformed regional agriculture
- **Bubonic plague** — Bacterial disease that spread along trade routes, killing millions across Eurasia
- **Black Death** — European name for bubonic plague outbreak (1347-1351) that killed one-third of population
- **Biological exchange** — Transfer of living organisms (crops, animals, diseases) between regions
- **Trade route transmission** — Process by which goods, ideas, and diseases spread along commercial networks
- **Demographic impact** — Changes in population size and structure caused by disease or improved nutrition
- **Environmental consequences** — Long-term effects of human activity on ecosystems and populations
