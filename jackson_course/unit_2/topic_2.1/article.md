## ARTICLE CONTENT

The **Silk Roads** weren't just one road — they were a massive network of trade routes connecting Europe, Asia, and Africa that experienced explosive growth after 1200 CE. College Board loves testing this topic because it perfectly demonstrates how economic innovations can transform entire civilizations and create interconnected world systems.

The growth of the Silk Roads after 1200 was driven by several key innovations in commercial practices that College Board considers essential knowledge. **Improved commercial practices** revolutionized how merchants conducted business across vast distances. The development of **bills of exchange** meant merchants no longer had to carry dangerous amounts of physical currency — instead, they could deposit money in one city and withdraw it in another through a paper document. **Banking houses** emerged to facilitate these transactions, with families like the Medici in Europe creating international financial networks. Most importantly, the widespread **use of paper money**, pioneered in Song China, made transactions lighter and more efficient than hauling heavy coins across deserts and mountains.

These commercial innovations led directly to an **increased volume of trade** and **expanded the geographical range of existing trade routes**. On the AP exam, you'll frequently see cause-and-effect questions about this relationship — the new financial tools made long-distance trade less risky and more profitable, which encouraged more merchants to participate. This expansion **promoted the growth of powerful new trading cities** like **Kashgar** in western China and **Samarkand** in Central Asia. These cities became wealthy not through manufacturing, but by serving as crucial stops where merchants could rest, resupply, and exchange goods. College Board wants you to understand that these weren't just random settlements — they were strategic commercial hubs that grew rich by facilitating trade.

The economic boom created by improved Silk Road networks had global effects that you'll see tested on the AP exam. **Demand for luxury goods increased in Afro-Eurasia**, creating new opportunities for producers. **Chinese, Persian, and Indian artisans and merchants expanded their production of textiles and porcelains for export** to meet this growing international demand. The **manufacture of iron and steel expanded in China** not just for domestic use, but specifically to supply the tools, weapons, and goods that traders needed. This wasn't just economic growth — it was economic specialization on a global scale, with different regions focusing on producing what they did best for international markets.

Understanding the Silk Roads' growth after 1200 is crucial because it demonstrates how technological and commercial innovations can create the world's first truly global economy. These same patterns of financial innovation driving trade expansion will appear throughout your AP World studies, from the Indian Ocean networks to the Atlantic trade systems.

## KEY TERMS
- **Silk Roads** — Network of trade routes connecting Europe, Asia, and Africa that expanded significantly after 1200
- **Bills of exchange** — Paper documents allowing merchants to deposit money in one location and withdraw it in another
- **Banking houses** — Financial institutions that facilitated international trade through credit and money exchange
- **Use of paper money** — Lightweight currency system pioneered in China that replaced heavy metal coins
- **Improved commercial practices** — Innovations in trade methods that reduced risk and increased efficiency
- **Increased volume of trade** — Growth in the amount of goods exchanged along trade networks after 1200
- **Kashgar** — Major trading city in western China that grew wealthy from Silk Road commerce
- **Samarkand** — Powerful Central Asian trading city that served as a crucial commercial hub
- **Demand for luxury goods** — Growing desire for expensive items across Afro-Eurasia that drove trade expansion
- **Textiles and porcelains for export** — Chinese luxury goods produced specifically for international markets
- **Iron and steel manufacture** — Expanded Chinese production of metal goods for domestic and international trade
- **Trading cities** — Urban centers that grew wealthy by facilitating commerce rather than manufacturing
