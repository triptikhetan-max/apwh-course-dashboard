## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to the final unit of AP World History! We're exploring how technology revolutionized human connections after 1900, creating the **globalized world** you live in today. College Board emphasizes this topic because it demonstrates how technological innovation drives historical change — a pattern you've seen throughout the course, but never at this speed or scale.

The most dramatic transformation came through **communication technologies** that virtually eliminated geographic barriers. **Radio communication**, first developed in the early 1900s, allowed instant voice transmission across continents for the first time in human history. By the 1920s, families could hear news, music, and entertainment from thousands of miles away. **Cellular communication** emerged in the 1970s-80s, making personal communication portable and universal. Then came the **internet** in the 1990s, connecting billions of people instantly regardless of location. College Board frequently tests how these technologies compressed time and space — consider how a message that once took months by ship now arrives in milliseconds. These innovations didn't just change how people communicated; they transformed politics, economics, and culture by creating shared global experiences.

Transportation technologies paralleled this communication revolution, making **physical movement** of people and goods faster and cheaper than ever before. **Air travel**, which began with the Wright brothers in 1903, evolved from a novelty to mass transportation. By the 1970s, **jumbo jets** could carry hundreds of passengers across oceans in hours rather than weeks. Meanwhile, **shipping containers**, standardized in the 1950s-60s, revolutionized global trade by making it easy to transfer goods between ships, trains, and trucks. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how these transportation advances facilitated globalization — remember that a product today might be designed in California, manufactured in China, and sold worldwide, all because of these innovations.

Perhaps no technological advance had more personal impact than developments in **birth control**. More effective contraceptives, including **the birth control pill** (approved in 1960), gave women unprecedented control over their fertility. This wasn't just a medical breakthrough — it was a social revolution that College Board considers essential knowledge. Women could delay childbearing for education and careers, leading to **declining fertility rates** in much of the developed world. Countries like Japan and Germany saw birth rates fall below replacement levels, while regions with limited access to birth control continued experiencing population growth. These **transformed reproductive practices** reshaped family structures, women's roles, and national demographics in ways that continue influencing politics and economics today.

Understanding these technological advances is crucial because they created the interconnected world that defines the contemporary era. Unlike previous periods where change occurred over centuries, the pace of technological innovation after 1900 accelerated dramatically, compressing historical change into decades or even years.

## KEY TERMS
- **Globalized world** — Interconnected global system where events in one region rapidly affect others
- **Radio communication** — Technology allowing instant voice transmission across long distances
- **Cellular communication** — Mobile telephone technology enabling personal communication anywhere
- **Internet** — Global network connecting billions of computers and devices instantly
- **Air travel** — Transportation by aircraft that revolutionized long-distance movement
- **Jumbo jets** — Large passenger aircraft that made international travel accessible to masses
- **Shipping containers** — Standardized cargo boxes that revolutionized global trade
- **Birth control** — Technologies and methods allowing people to control fertility
- **The birth control pill** — Oral contraceptive that gave women reliable fertility control
- **Declining fertility rates** — Reduction in birth rates in much of the developed world
- **Transformed reproductive practices** — Changes in family planning and childbearing patterns
- **Communication revolution** — Dramatic transformation in how humans share information
- **Transportation revolution** — Major changes in how people and goods move globally
