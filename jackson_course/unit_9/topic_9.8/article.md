## ARTICLE CONTENT

As globalization accelerated in the 20th century, world leaders realized that traditional diplomacy wasn't enough to handle increasingly complex international challenges. The devastation of two world wars, nuclear weapons, economic interdependence, and global environmental problems demanded new approaches. College Board frequently tests how these challenges led to the creation of **international institutions** — formal organizations where multiple countries work together to solve shared problems.

The most significant institution was the **United Nations**, founded in 1945 to replace the failed League of Nations. Unlike its predecessor, the UN had real enforcement power through the **Security Council**, whose five permanent members (US, USSR/Russia, UK, France, China) could authorize military action. The UN's structure reflected post-WWII power realities while its **General Assembly** gave all member nations a voice. Beyond peacekeeping, the UN created specialized agencies like **UNICEF** (children's welfare), **WHO** (World Health Organization), and the **International Court of Justice** to address specific global challenges. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how the UN's structure both enabled international cooperation and reflected existing power imbalances — notice how the Security Council's permanent members were the WWII victors.

Economic globalization produced its own institutions. The **Bretton Woods System** (1944) created the **International Monetary Fund (IMF)** to stabilize currencies and the **World Bank** to fund development projects. Later, the **World Trade Organization (WTO)** emerged to reduce trade barriers and resolve disputes. These institutions promoted **economic liberalization** — the idea that free markets and reduced government intervention would boost global prosperity. However, critics argued they primarily served wealthy nations' interests, as College Board loves to test through questions about how globalization created both opportunities and inequalities.

Regional institutions also flourished as countries sought cooperation with their neighbors. The **European Union** evolved from economic cooperation (European Coal and Steel Community, 1951) into political integration, eventually adopting a common currency and removing internal borders. **ASEAN** (Association of Southeast Asian Nations) promoted regional stability and economic growth. **NAFTA** (North American Free Trade Agreement) eliminated trade barriers between the US, Canada, and Mexico. These regional blocs showed how globalization operated at multiple levels — global institutions like the UN coexisted with regional ones addressing specific geographic concerns.

Environmental challenges highlighted both the potential and limitations of international institutions. The **Montreal Protocol** (1987) successfully addressed ozone depletion through coordinated action, while climate change negotiations like the **Kyoto Protocol** (1997) and **Paris Agreement** (2015) struggled with enforcement and compliance. These examples perfectly illustrate College Board's favorite theme: international institutions can facilitate cooperation, but their effectiveness depends on member states' willingness to sacrifice some sovereignty for collective action.

Understanding these institutions is crucial because they represent humanity's attempt to govern an increasingly interconnected world — a challenge that continues today as you'll see in our final topic on globalization's ongoing debates.

## KEY TERMS
- **International institutions** — Formal organizations where multiple countries cooperate to address shared challenges
- **United Nations (UN)** — International organization founded in 1945 to maintain world peace and facilitate cooperation
- **Security Council** — UN body with five permanent members having veto power over international military action
- **General Assembly** — UN body where all member nations have equal representation and voting rights
- **UNICEF** — UN agency focused on children's welfare and rights globally
- **World Health Organization (WHO)** — UN agency coordinating international health efforts and disease prevention
- **International Court of Justice** — UN judicial body settling legal disputes between nations
- **Bretton Woods System** — 1944 agreement creating international monetary and financial institutions
- **International Monetary Fund (IMF)** — Institution providing financial assistance and promoting monetary cooperation
- **World Bank** — Institution providing development loans and technical assistance to developing countries
- **World Trade Organization (WTO)** — Institution promoting free trade and resolving international trade disputes
- **Economic liberalization** — Policy approach emphasizing free markets and reduced government intervention
- **European Union (EU)** — Regional organization promoting European political and economic integration
- **ASEAN** — Association of Southeast Asian Nations promoting regional cooperation and stability
- **Montreal Protocol** — 1987 international treaty successfully addressing ozone layer depletion
