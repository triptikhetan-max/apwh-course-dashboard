## ARTICLE CONTENT

As we enter the 20th and 21st centuries, traditional social hierarchies faced unprecedented challenges from **rights-based movements** that fundamentally questioned age-old assumptions about who deserved power, education, and opportunity. College Board frequently tests these reform movements because they represent a global shift toward more inclusive societies — though the responses to these calls for reform varied dramatically across different regions and governments.

The **civil rights movement** in the United States exemplifies how rights-based discourses challenged racial hierarchies. Beginning in the 1950s, leaders like Martin Luther King Jr. used constitutional language and Christian ethics to demand equal treatment, directly confronting centuries of **Jim Crow laws** and segregation. Similarly, **anti-apartheid movements** in South Africa, led by figures like Nelson Mandela and the **African National Congress (ANC)**, challenged the rigid racial classification system that had excluded Black South Africans from political participation since 1948. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions comparing these movements' different strategies — from nonviolent resistance to armed struggle — and how governments responded with everything from violent repression to eventual reform. The **feminist movement** likewise challenged gender assumptions globally, from the **suffragette movement** in early 1900s Britain and the United States to second-wave feminism in the 1960s-70s that demanded workplace equality and reproductive rights.

Access to education became a crucial battleground for social reform, with previously excluded groups demanding inclusion in schools and universities. The 1954 **Brown v. Board of Education** decision in the United States legally ended educational segregation, though implementation took decades and required federal intervention. **Dalit rights movements** in India challenged the traditional **caste system** by demanding access to schools and temples previously reserved for higher castes, while leaders like **B.R. Ambedkar** used constitutional law to secure legal protections. Women's education expanded dramatically worldwide — by 2000, female literacy rates had increased from roughly 25% in 1900 to over 80% globally. College Board loves testing these statistics because they demonstrate how social change can be measured quantitatively.

Religious reform movements also challenged traditional hierarchies, often from within established institutions. **Liberation theology** in Latin America, led by priests like **Gustavo Gutiérrez**, argued that Christianity required fighting poverty and social injustice, directly challenging the Catholic Church's traditional alliance with wealthy elites. **Islamic feminists** like **Fatema Mernissi** reinterpreted Quranic texts to argue for women's rights, while **Vatican II** (1962-1965) modernized Catholic practices and emphasized social justice. These movements show how reformers often used traditional religious language to justify radical social changes.

More recently, **environmental justice movements** have protested how globalization's costs fall disproportionately on poor and marginalized communities. The **Chipko movement** in India saw rural women literally hugging trees to prevent deforestation that threatened their livelihoods. **Indigenous rights movements** from the Amazon to Australia have fought mining and logging companies, arguing that economic development destroys traditional ways of life. College Board increasingly tests these contemporary movements because they demonstrate how calls for reform continue to evolve, now focusing on environmental racism and climate justice rather than just traditional civil rights.

Understanding these reform movements is essential because they show how social change happens — through organized resistance, legal challenges, and shifting cultural attitudes — while also revealing how established powers resist change through violence, legal maneuvering, and gradual accommodation.

## KEY TERMS
- **Rights-based movements** — Social movements using legal and moral arguments about human rights to demand equality
- **Civil rights movement** — 1950s-60s movement demanding racial equality in the United States
- **Jim Crow laws** — State and local laws enforcing racial segregation in the American South
- **Anti-apartheid movement** — Resistance to South Africa's system of racial segregation and white minority rule
- **African National Congress (ANC)** — South African political party that led resistance to apartheid
- **Suffragette movement** — Early 20th century movement demanding women's voting rights
- **Brown v. Board of Education** — 1954 Supreme Court decision ending legal school segregation
- **Dalit rights movements** — Movements by India's "untouchable" caste demanding equal treatment
- **Caste system** — Hindu social hierarchy determining occupation and social status by birth
- **B.R. Ambedkar** — Indian lawyer and politician who fought caste discrimination and wrote India's constitution
- **Liberation theology** — Christian movement emphasizing social justice and fighting poverty
- **Islamic feminism** — Movement reinterpreting Islamic texts to support women's rights
- **Vatican II** — 1962-65 Catholic Church council that modernized practices and emphasized social justice
- **Environmental justice** — Movement addressing how environmental problems disproportionately affect marginalized communities
- **Chipko movement** — Indian environmental movement where villagers hugged trees to prevent deforestation
