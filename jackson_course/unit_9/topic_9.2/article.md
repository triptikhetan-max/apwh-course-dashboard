## ARTICLE CONTENT

Disease has been one of the most powerful forces shaping human history, and the period after 1900 perfectly demonstrates this relationship between humans and their environment. College Board emphasizes this topic because it shows how global interconnectedness can both spread deadly diseases and accelerate medical breakthroughs — a pattern that became especially relevant during the COVID-19 pandemic. Understanding these disease patterns will help you analyze how environmental factors continue to shape human societies.

The **1918 influenza pandemic** (Spanish flu) exemplifies how modern transportation and warfare created perfect conditions for global disease spread. Unlike typical seasonal flu that primarily killed the elderly and weak, this H1N1 strain killed healthy adults aged 20-40, devastating populations worldwide. The pandemic infected one-third of the global population and killed an estimated 50-100 million people — more than World War I itself. College Board often tests this pandemic because it demonstrates how **increased global connectivity** through steamships, railroads, and troop movements accelerated disease transmission. The pandemic's impact varied globally: while Western nations eventually recovered, many colonized regions in Africa and Asia suffered prolonged demographic catastrophe due to limited medical infrastructure and ongoing colonial exploitation.

Medical advances after 1900 dramatically transformed global health patterns, though benefits were unevenly distributed. The discovery of **antibiotics**, particularly **penicillin** in 1928, revolutionized treatment of bacterial infections that had killed millions throughout history. **Vaccine development** expanded rapidly, with successful campaigns against polio, measles, and smallpox — the latter becoming the first disease eradicated through human intervention in 1980. These advances contributed to the **demographic transition** in developed nations, where death rates plummeted and life expectancy soared. However, on the AP exam, you'll need to analyze how these medical breakthroughs remained largely concentrated in wealthy, industrialized nations while many developing countries continued struggling with preventable diseases.

The late 20th and early 21st centuries revealed new challenges in the human-disease relationship. **HIV/AIDS**, first identified in 1981, demonstrated how social stigma and economic inequality could worsen health crises. The pandemic particularly devastated sub-Saharan Africa, where limited healthcare infrastructure and social taboos prevented effective treatment distribution. More recently, **SARS** (2003), **H1N1 swine flu** (2009), and **COVID-19** (2019-present) have shown how rapid international travel and urbanization create conditions for **emerging infectious diseases**. College Board frequently tests these modern pandemics because they illustrate ongoing tensions between globalization's benefits and risks.

Environmental factors continue shaping disease patterns in predictable ways that you'll encounter throughout the AP exam. **Climate change** has expanded the range of vector-borne diseases like malaria and Zika virus, while **deforestation** and **urbanization** bring humans into contact with new pathogens. The concept of **One Health** — recognizing connections between human, animal, and environmental health — has become crucial for understanding modern disease emergence. These patterns connect directly to other Course topics about globalization, economic development, and environmental change.

## KEY TERMS
- **1918 influenza pandemic (Spanish flu)** — Global H1N1 outbreak that killed 50-100 million people, demonstrating effects of increased connectivity
- **Global connectivity** — Rapid transportation and communication systems that accelerated disease transmission after 1900
- **Antibiotics** — Medicines that kill or inhibit bacterial growth, revolutionizing treatment of infectious diseases
- **Penicillin** — First widely-used antibiotic, discovered in 1928, that dramatically reduced deaths from bacterial infections
- **Vaccine development** — Creation of biological preparations that provide immunity against specific diseases
- **Demographic transition** — Shift from high birth/death rates to low birth/death rates, partly driven by medical advances
- **HIV/AIDS** — Viral pandemic beginning in 1981 that highlighted health inequalities and social stigma effects
- **Emerging infectious diseases** — New or resurging pathogens like SARS, H1N1, and COVID-19 linked to globalization
- **Vector-borne diseases** — Illnesses transmitted by insects or animals, expanding due to climate change
- **One Health** — Approach recognizing interconnections between human, animal, and environmental health
- **Eradication** — Complete elimination of a disease, achieved only with smallpox through global vaccination campaigns
- **Health inequalities** — Uneven distribution of medical advances between developed and developing nations
