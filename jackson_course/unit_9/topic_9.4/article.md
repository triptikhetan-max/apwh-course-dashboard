## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to one of the most important economic transformations in modern history! The late 20th and early 21st centuries witnessed a dramatic shift toward **free-market economics** that fundamentally reshaped how the world does business. College Board loves testing this topic because it demonstrates how political events (like the Cold War's end) can trigger massive economic changes that affect billions of people's daily lives.

The collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 marked a turning point that accelerated a trend already underway: governments worldwide began embracing **economic liberalization** and reducing state control over their economies. This wasn't just about former communist countries — even nations with mixed economies started privatizing state-owned industries, reducing regulations, and opening their markets to international competition. Countries like India dismantled their "License Raj" system, while Latin American nations implemented **structural adjustment programs** often required by international lending institutions. On the AP exam, you'll frequently see questions comparing how different regions adapted to these free-market pressures, so remember that this was a global phenomenon with regional variations.

The spread of free-market principles created entirely new economic institutions that College Board considers essential knowledge. **Multinational corporations** exploded in size and influence, with companies like McDonald's, Coca-Cola, and later tech giants like Apple operating across dozens of countries simultaneously. These corporations didn't just export products — they exported business practices, labor standards, and consumer culture. The **World Trade Organization** (WTO), established in 1995, created global rules for international trade that favored reducing barriers and promoting competition. Meanwhile, the **International Monetary Fund** and **World Bank** increasingly tied their loans to free-market reforms, essentially forcing developing nations to adopt capitalist policies in exchange for financial assistance.

**Regional trade agreements** became the building blocks of this new global economy, creating economic blocs that reduced tariffs and promoted cross-border investment. **NAFTA** (1994) integrated the North American economies, while the **European Union** expanded eastward after the Cold War, bringing former Soviet satellites into the Western economic orbit. Asia saw the rise of **ASEAN** and various bilateral trade deals, while Latin America created **MERCOSUR**. These agreements didn't just reduce trade barriers — they standardized regulations, protected intellectual property, and created dispute resolution mechanisms that favored market-based solutions. College Board often tests how these regional agreements both promoted global integration and created new forms of economic nationalism.

However, this economic transformation came with significant consequences that you'll need to understand for the exam. While global GDP grew dramatically and consumer goods became more widely available, **income inequality** increased both within and between nations. The **Asian Financial Crisis** of 1997 and the **Global Financial Crisis** of 2008 revealed how interconnected markets could spread economic disasters across continents in mere hours. Environmental degradation accelerated as developing nations competed to attract foreign investment, often by relaxing environmental standards. Understanding both the benefits and costs of economic globalization is crucial because College Board frequently asks students to evaluate the overall impact of these changes.

## KEY TERMS
- **Free-market economics** — Economic system based on private ownership, minimal government regulation, and market competition
- **Economic liberalization** — Process of reducing government restrictions on economic activity and opening markets to competition
- **Structural adjustment programs** — Economic policies imposed by international lenders requiring free-market reforms
- **Multinational corporations** — Companies operating in multiple countries with integrated global strategies
- **World Trade Organization (WTO)** — International body established in 1995 to regulate global trade and reduce barriers
- **International Monetary Fund (IMF)** — International organization providing loans often tied to free-market reforms
- **NAFTA** — North American Free Trade Agreement creating integrated market between US, Canada, and Mexico
- **European Union** — Economic and political union expanding eastward after Cold War to include former Soviet satellites
- **ASEAN** — Association of Southeast Asian Nations promoting regional economic integration
- **MERCOSUR** — South American trade bloc reducing barriers between member countries
- **Income inequality** — Growing gap between rich and poor both within and between nations
- **Asian Financial Crisis** — 1997 economic collapse showing dangers of interconnected global markets
- **Global Financial Crisis** — 2008 worldwide economic recession originating in US housing market
