## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to one of the most visible aspects of our modern world — **globalized culture**! Since 1900, technological advances and economic integration have created shared cultural experiences that transcend national boundaries. College Board loves testing this topic because it demonstrates how globalization affects daily life in ways you personally experience, from the movies you watch to the brands you buy.

The political and social upheavals of the 20th century — including two world wars, decolonization, and the Cold War — fundamentally transformed how art and culture developed. As Heimler explains in the video, **popular and consumer culture** became increasingly global in the second half of the century. This wasn't just about American cultural dominance; it represented a true mixing of influences. **Reggae music** from Jamaica, pioneered by artists like Bob Marley, spread worldwide and influenced musicians from Africa to Asia. **Bollywood** films from India became global phenomena, watched not just by diaspora communities but by diverse audiences seeking alternatives to Hollywood productions. The **BBC** transformed from a British broadcaster into a global media presence, setting standards for international news coverage. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking you to analyze how these cultural products both reflected local traditions and appealed to global audiences.

Technology revolutionized how quickly cultural products could spread across borders. The rise of **television** in the mid-20th century created shared viewing experiences — millions worldwide watched the same Olympic Games broadcasts or World Cup soccer matches. The **Olympics** and **World Cup soccer** became particularly powerful examples of globalized culture because they combined national pride with international cooperation, creating truly global spectacles that transcended political differences. Later, **social media platforms** like **Facebook and Twitter** accelerated this process exponentially, allowing cultural trends to spread in real-time across continents. College Board frequently tests how these technologies both homogenized global culture and provided platforms for local cultures to gain worldwide audiences.

Perhaps most dramatically, **consumer culture became globalized** and transcended national borders through the rise of **global brands** and **online commerce**. Companies like **Toyota** and **Coca-Cola** became household names from Detroit to Delhi, creating shared consumer experiences that often carried cultural meanings beyond the products themselves — Coca-Cola, for example, became associated with American lifestyle and values. The development of **online commerce** through platforms like **Alibaba** and **eBay** further broke down barriers, allowing consumers anywhere to access products from around the world. This created both opportunities for small producers to reach global markets and concerns about the homogenization of local cultures. Remember for the AP exam that globalized consumer culture represents both economic integration and cultural transformation — it's never just about business.

The emergence of globalized culture after 1900 illustrates how technological innovation, economic integration, and cultural exchange can create new forms of human connection while also raising questions about cultural authenticity and diversity that continue to shape our world today.

## KEY TERMS
- **Globalized culture** — Shared cultural practices, products, and experiences that transcend national boundaries
- **Popular and consumer culture** — Mass-produced entertainment and goods designed for widespread consumption
- **Reggae music** — Jamaican musical genre that gained global influence, particularly through Bob Marley
- **Bollywood** — Indian film industry that produces movies with global appeal and distribution
- **BBC** — British Broadcasting Corporation that evolved into a global media presence
- **Television** — Broadcasting technology that created shared global viewing experiences
- **Olympics** — International sporting competition that became a major globalized cultural event
- **World Cup soccer** — Global football tournament representing the world's most popular sport
- **Social media platforms** — Digital networks like Facebook and Twitter that accelerate cultural exchange
- **Global brands** — Companies like Toyota and Coca-Cola recognized worldwide with shared cultural meanings
- **Online commerce** — Internet-based trading through platforms like Alibaba and eBay
- **Cultural homogenization** — Process by which local cultures become more similar due to global influences
- **Consumer culture** — Society organized around the purchase and consumption of goods and services
