## ARTICLE CONTENT

As globalization accelerated throughout the 20th and 21st centuries, it didn't spread unopposed. College Board emphasizes that responses to globalization took many different forms — some violent, some peaceful, some religious, some secular. Understanding these resistance movements is crucial because they reveal how local communities and nations have struggled to maintain their identities in an increasingly interconnected world.

Religious fundamentalism emerged as one of the most significant forms of resistance to cultural globalization. **Islamic fundamentalism** gained momentum as Muslim communities rejected what they saw as Western secular values threatening traditional Islamic society. The **Iranian Revolution** of 1979 exemplifies this perfectly — Ayatollah Khomeini's movement overthrew the Western-backed Shah and established a theocracy that explicitly rejected Western cultural influence. Similarly, **Hindu nationalism** in India has grown stronger since the 1980s, with groups like the BJP promoting "Hindutva" ideology that resists Western cultural influence and emphasizes Hindu identity. **Christian fundamentalism** in the United States has also responded to globalization by promoting traditional values and opposing secular influences. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking you to compare how different religious fundamentalist movements responded to similar globalizing pressures.

Economic resistance to globalization has taken various forms, from violent protests to alternative economic systems. The **anti-globalization movement** gained international attention during the 1999 **World Trade Organization protests in Seattle**, where activists argued that free trade agreements primarily benefited wealthy corporations at the expense of workers and the environment. **Fair trade movements** emerged as a peaceful alternative, promoting ethical consumption and direct relationships between producers and consumers. College Board frequently tests your understanding of how these movements challenged the assumptions of **neoliberal economics** that dominated globalization policies. Labor unions worldwide have also organized resistance to **outsourcing** and **deindustrialization**, arguing that globalization has led to job losses and wage stagnation in developed countries.

Political resistance has manifested through both democratic and violent means. **Populist movements** in recent decades, from Brexit in Britain to the election of leaders like Donald Trump and Jair Bolsonaro, have explicitly campaigned against globalization, promising to prioritize national sovereignty over international cooperation. More extreme resistance has included **terrorist organizations** like Al-Qaeda and ISIS, which have used globalized communication and transportation networks paradoxically to organize attacks against Western influence and secular governments. Environmental movements have also resisted certain aspects of globalization, arguing that unrestricted global capitalism has accelerated **climate change** and environmental degradation.

Some resistance movements have focused on preserving local cultures and languages threatened by global homogenization. The **Slow Food movement** began in Italy as a response to the spread of fast food chains like McDonald's, promoting local food traditions and sustainable agriculture. **Language preservation movements** worldwide have worked to maintain indigenous and minority languages threatened by the dominance of English and other global languages. Even **cultural protectionism** policies, like France's quotas on American films and music, represent institutional resistance to cultural globalization.

Understanding these various forms of resistance helps explain why globalization remains contested and uneven across different regions and communities, setting up the ongoing debates about global governance that continue today.

## KEY TERMS
- **Islamic fundamentalism** — Religious movement rejecting Western secular values and promoting traditional Islamic governance
- **Iranian Revolution** — 1979 overthrow of the Shah that established a theocracy opposing Western influence
- **Hindu nationalism** — Movement promoting Hindu identity and resisting Western cultural influence in India
- **Anti-globalization movement** — International coalition opposing corporate-led globalization and free trade agreements
- **World Trade Organization protests** — 1999 Seattle demonstrations against global trade policies
- **Fair trade movements** — Alternative economic system promoting ethical production and consumption
- **Neoliberal economics** — Free-market policies emphasizing deregulation and privatization
- **Outsourcing** — Practice of moving jobs to countries with lower labor costs
- **Populist movements** — Political movements claiming to represent ordinary people against elite interests
- **Brexit** — Britain's withdrawal from the European Union partly motivated by anti-globalization sentiment
- **Slow Food movement** — Cultural resistance to fast food promoting local food traditions
- **Language preservation movements** — Efforts to maintain indigenous and minority languages against global homogenization
- **Cultural protectionism** — Government policies limiting foreign cultural products to protect local traditions
- **Environmental movements** — Organizations opposing aspects of globalization that accelerate climate change
- **Terrorist organizations** — Violent groups using global networks to resist Western influence and secular governments
