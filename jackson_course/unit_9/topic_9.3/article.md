## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to one of the most relevant topics for your generation! Since 1900, human activities have dramatically transformed Earth's environment, creating new forms of international competition and cooperation. College Board frequently tests this topic because it perfectly demonstrates how technological progress can create unintended consequences that reshape global politics and economics.

The twentieth century witnessed unprecedented **deforestation** as growing populations and industrial demands drove massive clearing of forests worldwide. The **Amazon rainforest** lost over 17% of its original area, primarily for cattle ranching and agriculture, while **Southeast Asian forests** disappeared for palm oil plantations and logging. Simultaneously, **desertification** expanded as poor farming practices and overgrazing converted fertile land into desert, particularly affecting Africa's **Sahel region** and parts of China and the Middle Western United States. As the video explains, these weren't isolated problems — they represented a global pattern where short-term economic gains came at massive environmental costs. College Board loves asking about the connection between industrial agriculture and environmental degradation, so remember that the Green Revolution's pesticides and monoculture farming often accelerated these problems.

**Air quality decline** became a defining feature of the industrial age, with cities like London experiencing deadly **smog events** in the 1950s that killed thousands. The burning of fossil fuels created **acid rain** that damaged forests across Europe and North America, while **carbon dioxide emissions** began altering global climate patterns. Meanwhile, competition for **freshwater resources** intensified as populations grew and industrial demands increased. The **Aral Sea** in Central Asia virtually disappeared due to irrigation projects, while major rivers like the **Colorado** and **Yellow River** often run dry before reaching the ocean. On the AP exam, you'll frequently see questions connecting these environmental changes to geopolitical tensions — countries now fight over water rights just as they once fought over territory.

This environmental degradation created new forms of international competition that didn't exist before 1900. Nations began competing for remaining **arable land**, **clean water sources**, and even **clean air**. The 1970s **oil crises** demonstrated how resource scarcity could reshape global politics overnight, while **climate change** created new categories of **environmental refugees** fleeing droughts, floods, and rising sea levels. College Board often tests how environmental problems transcend national borders — acid rain from one country affects neighboring nations, while **ocean pollution** and **overfishing** require international cooperation to address.

Understanding environmental changes since 1900 is crucial because they've created entirely new categories of global challenges. Unlike traditional political or economic problems, environmental issues often require unprecedented levels of international cooperation and force us to think about the long-term consequences of short-term decisions.

## KEY TERMS
- **Deforestation** — Large-scale clearing of forests for agriculture, logging, or development
- **Amazon rainforest** — World's largest tropical rainforest, lost 17% of original area since 1900
- **Desertification** — Process where fertile land becomes desert due to drought, deforestation, or poor agriculture
- **Sahel region** — Semi-arid zone in Africa experiencing severe desertification and resource competition
- **Air quality decline** — Deterioration of atmospheric conditions due to pollution from industry and transportation
- **Smog events** — Severe air pollution episodes, like London's 1952 Great Smog that killed 4,000 people
- **Acid rain** — Precipitation made acidic by air pollution, damaging forests and buildings
- **Carbon dioxide emissions** — Greenhouse gases from burning fossil fuels, contributing to climate change
- **Freshwater depletion** — Overconsumption and pollution of available drinking water supplies
- **Aral Sea** — Central Asian sea that nearly disappeared due to irrigation diversions
- **Environmental refugees** — People forced to migrate due to environmental degradation or climate change
- **Overfishing** — Harvesting fish faster than populations can reproduce, leading to species collapse
- **Climate change** — Long-term alteration of global weather patterns due to human activities
