## ARTICLE CONTENT

Just as Southeast Asian states used maritime connections to build power, American civilizations built impressive states through different methods — and College Board loves testing these differences! As Heimler explains, American states "demonstrated continuity, innovation, and diversity" just like their Afro-Eurasian counterparts.

Let's start with North America. The **Mississippian culture** centered at **Cahokia** (near modern St. Louis) built massive earthen mounds — some 100 feet tall! College Board wants you to know their rigid class system: the **Great Sun** ruler on top, then priests and nobles, then everyone else. They mysteriously abandoned Cahokia around 1450, possibly due to environmental disaster. Meanwhile, the **Chaco** and **Mesa Verde** civilizations in the Southwest built impressive cliff dwellings but declined by 1300 when the climate got drier.

Now for the heavy hitters — the three civilizations College Board ALWAYS tests. First, the **Maya city-states** (like **Chichen Itza**) never formed one unified empire. Instead, independent city-states with their own kings fought each other for tribute and captives for human sacrifice. The Maya developed the only **fully formed writing system** in the Americas and created incredibly accurate calendars. On the AP exam, remember: Maya = city-states, not empire! They declined around 900 CE due to drought and warfare.

The **Mexica** (Aztecs) built their capital **Tenochtitlan** in 1325 on an island in Lake Texcoco. As Heimler notes, they controlled their massive empire through a **tribute system** — conquered peoples paid goods, provided labor, and gave captives for sacrifice. The Aztecs innovated **chinampas** (floating gardens) to feed their 200,000-person capital. Remember: **Triple Alliance** = Tenochtitlan + two other city-states working together to dominate Mesoamerica.

The **Inca** created the largest empire in the Americas, stretching from Ecuador to Chile. Instead of tribute, they used the **mit'a system** — mandatory public service where subjects built roads and worked state lands. Unlike the Maya and Aztec, the Inca had no writing system, instead using **quipu** (knotted strings) to record information. Their **road system** covered 25,000 miles through the mountains — that's longer than the distance around Earth's equator!

These American states show the same patterns as Afro-Eurasian ones: they centralized power, extracted resources from subjects, and justified rule through religion — proving humans everywhere build complex societies in surprisingly similar ways.

## KEY TERMS
- **Cahokia** — largest Mississippian settlement with monumental earthen mounds
- **Great Sun** — title of Mississippian rulers in rigid class hierarchy
- **Chaco** — Southwestern civilization that built multi-room stone structures
- **Mesa Verde** — Southwestern civilization famous for cliff dwellings
- **Maya city-states** — independent political units that never unified into empire
- **Chichen Itza** — major Maya city-state and ceremonial center
- **Mexica** — the Aztec people who built empire from Tenochtitlan
- **Triple Alliance** — Aztec political system uniting three city-states
- **Tenochtitlan** — Aztec capital city built on island in Lake Texcoco
- **Tribute system** — Aztec method of controlling empire through payment collection
- **Chinampas** — Aztec floating gardens for agriculture
- **Inca** — South American empire stretching from Ecuador to Chile
- **Mit'a system** — Inca mandatory public service (labor tax)
- **Quipu** — Inca knotted string system for recording information
- **Carpa Nan** — Inca road system spanning 25,000 miles
