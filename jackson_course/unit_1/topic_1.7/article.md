## ARTICLE CONTENT

In Topic 1.6, you learned how European states relied on feudalism and Christianity to organize society. Now it's time to zoom out and **compare how different civilizations built and maintained states from 1200-1450** — a critical skill College Board tests heavily on the AP exam.

**The AP Comparison Framework** requires you to identify BOTH similarities AND differences, then explain their significance. On the exam, you'll see questions like "Compare the processes of state formation in TWO of the following regions..." Here's what College Board wants: First, identify **similarities** (what patterns appear across regions?). Second, identify **differences** (what unique features set regions apart?). Third, explain **why these matter** (what caused them? what resulted?).

As Heimler emphasizes in his review, the key **similarity** across all regions was that states used **belief systems** to maintain power. **Song China** promoted **Neo-Confucianism** with its hierarchical social order and civil service exams. **Dar al-Islam** implemented **Sharia law** and spread through military conquest, merchants, and **Sufi** missionaries. The **Delhi Sultanate** imposed Islamic rule over a Hindu-majority population. **European states** relied on **Roman Catholicism** to unify fragmented territories. Even the **Aztec Empire** used religious human sacrifice to demonstrate power. College Board loves testing this pattern — religion wasn't just personal belief, it was a political tool for state-building.

But here's where **differences** matter for your exam score. While Song China created a **centralized bureaucracy** through merit-based exams, European states remained **decentralized** under **feudalism**. The **Inca Empire** used the **mit'a system** for direct labor control, while the **Aztec Empire** relied on a **tribute system** from conquered peoples. As the **Abbasid Caliphate** fragmented, new **Turkic** Muslim empires (like the **Seljuk Empire**) replaced Arab leadership — showing how ethnic changes accompanied political shifts. The **Vijayanagara Empire** arose as Hindu resistance to Muslim expansion, demonstrating how religious conflict shaped state formation differently across regions.

**Key Takeaway:** College Board tests comparison skills because they reveal larger patterns — understanding HOW states formed differently helps explain WHY some lasted while others collapsed.

## KEY TERMS
- **State formation** — the process of creating and organizing political entities with sovereignty over territory
- **Neo-Confucianism** — revival of Confucian thought emphasizing social hierarchy and civil service
- **Dar al-Islam** — "house of Islam"; territories where Islamic faith organized society
- **Sharia law** — Islamic legal code based on the Quran
- **Sufi** — mystical Islamic missionaries who adapted to local beliefs
- **Abbasid Caliphate** — Arab-dominated Islamic empire that fragmented by 1200
- **Seljuk Empire** — Turkic Muslim empire that replaced Abbasid power
- **Delhi Sultanate** — Islamic state ruling over Hindu-majority northern India
- **Vijayanagara Empire** — Hindu state established as resistance to Islamic expansion
- **Mit'a system** — Inca labor obligation system for state projects
- **Tribute system** — Aztec practice requiring conquered peoples to provide goods/labor
- **Feudalism** — decentralized European system of land-for-loyalty exchange
- **Civil service examination** — Chinese merit-based test for government positions
