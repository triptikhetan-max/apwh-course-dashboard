## ARTICLE CONTENT

Remember how the **Delhi Sultanate** from Topic 1.2 brought Islam into northern India? Well, South and Southeast Asia's story from 1200-1450 is all about what happens when Islamic expansion meets established Hindu and Buddhist societies. College Board loves to test how different religions coexisted and competed in this region.

In South Asia, the **Delhi Sultanate** controlled northern India but couldn't convert most Hindus to Islam. As Heimler explains, when two Muslim brothers were sent south to expand the sultanate's territory, they actually reconverted to Hinduism and founded the **Vijayanagara Empire** (1336-1646) instead — showing how resilient Hindu culture remained. Meanwhile, the **Rajput kingdoms** in the north were decentralized Hindu states constantly fighting each other, making them vulnerable to Muslim invasions. This political fragmentation is a key theme College Board emphasizes.

Religious developments shaped society profoundly. The **Bhakti movement** emerged as a Hindu revival emphasizing emotional devotion to one deity rather than rigid rituals — similar to how **Sufism** worked in Islam. Both movements appealed to common people and women, spreading their respective faiths. On the AP exam, you'll need to know that **Buddhist monasticism** created centers of learning like Nalanda University, though Buddhism declined in India as many Buddhists converted to Islam. The caste system remained India's strongest continuity, even absorbing Muslim merchants into occupation-based subcastes.

Southeast Asia tells a different story. The **Srivijaya Empire** (670-1025) in Sumatra and later the **Majapahit** kingdom (1293-1520) in Java controlled crucial sea trade routes between India and China. Land-based powers included the **Khmer Empire** (802-1431) in Cambodia, famous for Angkor Wat, and the **Sukhothai** kingdom in Thailand. The **Sinhala dynasties** in Sri Lanka became Buddhist centers of learning. These states demonstrate how Indian religions spread through trade networks — Hinduism and Buddhism arrived peacefully through merchants, not conquest. By 1450, Islam had also spread to maritime Southeast Asia through trade, making Indonesia eventually the world's most populous Muslim nation. This peaceful religious diffusion through trade networks is exactly the kind of comparison College Board tests.

Understanding South and Southeast Asia's religious diversity and decentralized politics sets up why the region remained vulnerable to future invasions — including the Mughals you'll study in Unit 3.

## KEY TERMS
- **Delhi Sultanate** — Islamic state ruling northern India (1206-1526) that failed to convert most Hindus
- **Vijayanagara Empire** — Hindu empire in southern India (1336-1646) founded by reconverted Muslims
- **Rajput kingdoms** — Decentralized Hindu kingdoms in northern India, often fighting each other
- **Bhakti movement** — Hindu devotional movement emphasizing emotional worship of one deity
- **Sufism** — Mystical Islamic movement emphasizing personal spiritual experience
- **Buddhist monasticism** — System of monks and nuns that created centers of learning like Nalanda
- **Srivijaya Empire** — Hindu sea-based empire in Sumatra controlling Indian Ocean trade (670-1025)
- **Majapahit** — Hindu-Buddhist sea-based kingdom in Java (1293-1520)
- **Khmer Empire** — Land-based empire in Cambodia famous for Angkor Wat (802-1431)
- **Sukhothai** — Early Thai kingdom (c. 1238-1438) that was later absorbed by the Ayutthaya Kingdom, which conquered Angkor in 1431
- **Sinhala dynasties** — Buddhist kingdoms in Sri Lanka that became centers of learning
