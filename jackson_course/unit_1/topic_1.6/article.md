## ARTICLE CONTENT

Just as Mali controlled trans-Saharan trade routes connecting Africa to the Mediterranean world, Europe from 1200-1450 was experiencing its own transformations. **College Board loves to test how Europe's political fragmentation contrasted with the centralized states you studied in Africa and Asia.**

Medieval Europe was characterized by **feudalism** — a decentralized political system based on exchanges of land for loyalty. As Heimler explains, this created a four-tiered social hierarchy: kings granted land (**fiefs**) to lords, who contracted knights for protection, while **serfs** (peasants tied to the land) worked the bottom tier. This system emerged because, unlike Song China or the Abbasid Caliphate, Europe lacked strong central governments after Rome's fall. **On the AP exam, you'll need to distinguish between feudalism (political relationships) and manorialism (economic self-sufficiency on manor estates).**

The **Roman Catholic Church** remained Europe's most powerful institution throughout this period, establishing the first **universities** and promoting **Scholasticism** — using logic to reconcile faith with reason. **This is where Judaism and Islam critically influenced Europe:** Jewish communities in cities like Paris and Toledo served as crucial intermediaries in trade and finance (since Christians couldn't charge interest to other Christians). Islamic scholarship entered Europe through Spain during the **Reconquista** and through the **Crusades**. Muslim texts on mathematics, medicine, and philosophy were translated in cities like Toledo, introducing Europeans to preserved Greek knowledge and new Islamic innovations. **College Board often tests this cultural exchange — Europe wasn't isolated but deeply influenced by its religious minorities.**

By 1200, Europe began transforming. Monarchs like England's King John (forced to sign the **Magna Carta** in 1215) and France's Philip IV (who created the **Estates-General**) started consolidating power. Agricultural innovations like the **three-field system** increased productivity, supporting **urban growth** and a new middle class called the **bourgeoisie**. Cities developed **guilds** — associations of craftspeople that regulated trade and quality. These changes, combined with the **Renaissance** revival of classical learning and the **printing press** (1439), set Europe on a path toward the centralized nation-states that would dominate the next era.

**This topic matters because College Board tests how Europe's decentralized feudalism contrasted with centralized states elsewhere, and how religious diversity — not just Christianity — shaped medieval European development.**

## KEY TERMS
- **Feudalism** — political system based on land-for-loyalty exchanges between social classes
- **Manorialism** — economic system of self-sufficient agricultural estates
- **Serfs** — peasants legally tied to the land they worked
- **Fiefs** — land grants given by lords in exchange for service
- **Roman Catholic Church** — dominant religious institution providing unity across fragmented Europe
- **Universities** — educational institutions established by the Church (Paris, Oxford)
- **Scholasticism** — intellectual movement using logic to reconcile faith and reason
- **Magna Carta (1215)** — document limiting English royal power
- **Estates-General** — French assembly representing clergy, nobility, and commoners
- **Three-field system** — agricultural rotation method increasing productivity
- **Guilds** — urban associations regulating crafts and trade
- **Bourgeoisie** — emerging middle class of merchants and artisans
- **Renaissance** — cultural revival of classical Greek and Roman learning
- **Reconquista** — Christian reconquest of Spain from Muslims (completed 1492)
- **Crusades** — military campaigns to reclaim the Holy Land (1095-1291)
