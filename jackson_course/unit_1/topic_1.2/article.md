## ARTICLE CONTENT

Remember how Song China's maritime trade pushed goods into Indian Ocean networks? Well, those networks connected directly to the Islamic world, or **Dar al-Islam** ("house of Islam"), which by 1200 stretched from Spain to India. But here's what College Board wants you to know: the old Arab-dominated Abbasid Caliphate was fragmenting, and new players were taking over.

As Heimler explains, the big change was that **Turkic peoples** (not Arabs!) established the new Islamic empires. Three key examples you MUST know: the **Seljuk Empire** in Central Asia, the **Mamluk Sultanate** in Egypt, and the **Delhi Sultanates** in South Asia. The Seljuks started as military slaves brought in by the Abbasids (ironic, right?), but by 1200 they controlled most political power. The Mamluks? Same story — enslaved Turkic warriors who seized control of Egypt in 1250. The Delhi Sultanates established Muslim rule in northern India for 300 years. On the AP exam, if they ask about Islamic political changes after 1200, think "Turkic takeover."

But here's what really matters for the exam: intellectual innovations. College Board loves testing this! **Nasir al-Din al-Tusi** (1201-1274) invented trigonometry and built the world's most advanced observatory. **Ibn al-Haytham** revolutionized optics and the scientific method. **Al-Khwarizmi's** earlier work on algebra continued influencing mathematicians. Islamic cities like Cairo pioneered **hospitals** with medical licensing exams. The **House of Wisdom** in Baghdad preserved Greek philosophy — without Muslim scholars translating Aristotle and Plato, Europe would never have had a Renaissance!

Now here's the part students often miss: **Judaism and Christianity** weren't just tolerated — they actively shaped Islamic societies. As "People of the Book," Jews and Christians served as merchants, scholars, and administrators throughout Dar al-Islam. In Spain's **al-Andalus**, Jewish philosopher Maimonides and Muslim scholar Ibn Rushd (Averroes) exchanged ideas that later influenced Christian thought. This religious diversity actually strengthened Islamic states economically and intellectually. The AP exam often asks about this cultural exchange, so remember: Islamic tolerance of monotheistic minorities led to intellectual flourishing that benefited everyone.

**KEY TAKEAWAY:** The fragmentation of Arab Islamic power led to Turkic-dominated states that fostered unprecedented intellectual achievements and religious tolerance — setting up the cultural exchanges that would spark the European Renaissance.

## KEY TERMS
- **Dar al-Islam** — "House of Islam"; regions where Islam was the dominant religion (c. 1200-1450)
- **Abbasid Caliphate** — Arab-dominated Islamic empire that fragmented by 1200
- **Seljuk Empire** — Turkic Muslim state in Central Asia; reduced Abbasid power
- **Mamluk Sultanate** — Turkic slave-soldiers who seized control of Egypt (1250-1517)
- **Delhi Sultanates** — Series of Turkic Muslim states ruling northern India
- **Turkic peoples** — Central Asian groups who established new Islamic states after 1200
- **House of Wisdom** — Baghdad's center of learning; preserved Greek philosophy
- **Nasir al-Din al-Tusi** — Persian scholar who invented trigonometry (1201-1274)
- **Ibn al-Haytham** — "Father of optics"; pioneered scientific method
- **Al-Khwarizmi** — Mathematician whose algebra work continued influencing Islamic scholarship
- **Al-Andalus** — Muslim-ruled Spain; center of religious tolerance and learning
- **People of the Book** — Islamic term for Jews and Christians as fellow monotheists
