## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to AP World History! We're starting our journey in East Asia, where the **Song Dynasty** (960-1279 CE) created one of history's most impressive civilizations. College Board loves testing this topic because it perfectly demonstrates how empires maintain power through traditional methods while also innovating — a pattern you'll see throughout the course.

As Heimler explains in the video, Song rulers maintained power through two key traditional methods. First, they used **Neo-Confucianism**, a revival of Confucian philosophy that blended Buddhist and Taoist ideas. This philosophy emphasized hierarchical relationships and **filial piety** (honoring one's ancestors and parents), which conveniently taught people to obey their superiors — including the emperor. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how this philosophy reinforced social hierarchy, particularly through practices like **foot binding** for elite women, which physically limited their mobility and demonstrated their families' wealth. More broadly, Confucian traditions created a dual expectation for women — they were respected as mothers and moral educators within the family, yet expected to show deference to fathers, husbands, and sons, reinforcing a patriarchal family structure that limited their public roles. Second, Song rulers expanded the **imperial bureaucracy**, staffing it through **civil service examinations** based on Confucian texts. This created a **meritocracy** where jobs went to the most qualified candidates, not just the well-connected.

China's cultural influence spread throughout East Asia via the **tributary system**, where neighboring states acknowledged Chinese superiority in exchange for trade benefits. **Heian Japan** (794-1185 CE) voluntarily adopted Chinese Buddhism, writing systems, and literary traditions like poetry. Korea maintained closer ties due to its land border, adopting Confucianism, Buddhism, and the civil service exam system. Vietnam, while adopting many Chinese practices, notably rejected foot binding and maintained greater independence for women. College Board frequently tests these different responses to Chinese influence — remember that each country adapted Chinese culture differently based on their own needs.

The Song economy boomed through several innovations that College Board considers essential knowledge. **Champa rice** from Vietnam could be harvested twice yearly, leading to population growth. The expansion of the **Grand Canal** facilitated internal trade. Manufacturing exploded with innovations in **steel and iron production**, while **textiles and porcelains for export** dominated international markets. As Heimler emphasizes, these weren't just random developments — they created the world's most commercialized economy before industrialization.

Understanding Song China is crucial because it sets up patterns you'll see throughout the course: how empires use ideology to maintain power, how cultural diffusion works differently across regions, and how economic innovations drive historical change.

## KEY TERMS
- **Song Dynasty** — Chinese dynasty (960-1279) that ruled with Confucian principles and expanded bureaucracy
- **Neo-Confucianism** — Revival of Confucian thought incorporating Buddhist and Taoist elements
- **Filial piety** — Practice of honoring ancestors and parents, reinforcing social hierarchy
- **Foot binding** — Practice of breaking and binding young girls' feet to show elite status
- **Imperial bureaucracy** — Government system where appointed officials carry out emperor's policies
- **Civil service examination** — Merit-based test on Confucian texts for government positions
- **Meritocracy** — System where positions are earned through demonstrated ability
- **Tributary system** — Arrangement where neighboring states pay tribute to acknowledge Chinese superiority
- **Heian Japan** — Japanese period (794-1185) marked by voluntary adoption of Chinese culture
- **Champa rice** — Fast-ripening, drought-resistant rice variety that increased agricultural output
- **Grand Canal** — Extensive waterway system facilitating internal Chinese trade
- **Steel and iron production** — Manufacturing innovations that supported tools, weapons, and infrastructure
- **Textiles and porcelains for export** — Luxury goods that dominated Chinese international trade
- **Theravada Buddhism** — Oldest branch of Buddhism emphasizing monastic discipline and individual enlightenment, dominant in Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia
- **Mahayana Buddhism** — Branch of Buddhism emphasizing compassion for all beings and the bodhisattva ideal, widespread in East Asia
- **Tibetan Buddhism** — Branch of Buddhism blending Mahayana teachings with local Tibetan traditions, featuring elaborate rituals and a monastic hierarchy
