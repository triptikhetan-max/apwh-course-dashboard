## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to the age of maritime empires! From 1450 to 1750, European powers didn't just explore the world — they completely transformed how empires maintained power through economic control. College Board loves testing this topic because it shows how rulers adapted traditional governance methods to manage global trade networks and overseas territories.

The key innovation European rulers used to maintain power was **mercantilism**, an economic theory that viewed wealth as finite and emphasized accumulating precious metals while maintaining a favorable balance of trade. Mercantilist policies required strict government control over colonial economies — colonies existed solely to benefit the mother country by providing raw materials and buying finished goods. To finance this expensive global expansion, rulers turned to **joint-stock companies** like the Dutch East India Company and English East India Company. These companies allowed rulers to compete in global trade without risking royal treasuries, while merchants could pool resources for risky but potentially profitable ventures. On the AP exam, you'll frequently see questions about how these companies functioned as extensions of state power, controlling territories and even maintaining armies.

These new economic systems created intense rivalries that College Board considers essential knowledge. **Economic disputes led to conflicts between states**, most notably the **Muslim-European rivalry in the Indian Ocean**. European companies challenged existing Muslim trade networks that had dominated Indian Ocean commerce for centuries, leading to naval conflicts and competition for control of key ports and trade routes. Similarly, the **Moroccan conflict with the Songhai Empire** demonstrates how established land-based empires clashed with new economic pressures — Morocco's access to European firearms through Atlantic trade networks allowed them to conquer the wealthy but technologically disadvantaged Songhai Empire in 1591.

The intensification of global trade created significant social changes that College Board frequently tests. **Peasant and artisan labor continued and intensified in many regions** as demand for food and consumer goods skyrocketed. From Chinese porcelain workshops to Indian textile production, traditional craftspeople found themselves working harder to meet global demand. Most dramatically, **demographic changes in Africa resulted from the trade of enslaved persons**, fundamentally restructuring gender and family relationships as predominantly male populations were forcibly removed, leaving women to take on new economic and social roles.

Religious interactions also transformed during this period. The **expansion of connections between hemispheres** spread existing religions to new areas — Christianity reached the Americas and parts of Asia, while Islam expanded in Southeast Asia through trade networks. However, these encounters also created **religious conflicts** as different belief systems clashed, and led to **syncretic belief systems** that blended traditional practices with introduced religions, such as the incorporation of indigenous elements into Christianity in Latin America.

Understanding how maritime empires maintained power through economic control sets up the next phase of our study — how these same economic pressures eventually challenged traditional political structures and contributed to revolutionary movements worldwide.

## KEY TERMS
- **Mercantilism** — Economic theory emphasizing government control of trade to accumulate wealth and maintain favorable balance of trade
- **Joint-stock companies** — Business organizations where investors pooled capital to finance expensive overseas ventures while sharing risks and profits
- **Dutch East India Company** — Powerful joint-stock company that controlled Dutch trade in the Indian Ocean and Southeast Asia
- **English East India Company** — British joint-stock company that eventually controlled large portions of India and dominated British trade in Asia
- **Muslim-European rivalry in the Indian Ocean** — Competition between established Muslim trade networks and emerging European commercial companies for control of maritime trade routes
- **Moroccan conflict with the Songhai Empire** — 1591 conquest of the Songhai Empire by Morocco using European firearms acquired through Atlantic trade
- **Demographic changes in Africa** — Population shifts and social restructuring caused by the Atlantic slave trade, particularly affecting gender ratios and family structures
- **Syncretic belief systems** — Religious practices that blend elements from different traditions, often resulting from cultural encounters during this period
- **Religious conflicts** — Disputes arising from the spread and interaction of different belief systems in newly connected regions
- **Balance of trade** — Economic concept measuring the difference between a country's exports and imports, central to mercantilist policy
- **Favorable balance of trade** — Economic situation where exports exceed imports, viewed as essential for national wealth under mercantilism
- **Colonial economies** — Economic systems in overseas territories designed to benefit the mother country through resource extraction and controlled markets
