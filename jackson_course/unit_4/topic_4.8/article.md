## ARTICLE CONTENT

The period from 1450-1750 represents one of history's most dramatic transformations, as **transoceanic voyaging** connected the Eastern and Western Hemispheres for the first time. College Board loves testing this topic because it demonstrates how economic changes create massive social upheavals — a pattern that continues into the modern world. This era didn't just change what people traded; it fundamentally altered who held power, how societies were organized, and where wealth was concentrated globally.

The foundation for this transformation came from **knowledge transfer** between civilizations. European technological developments built directly on innovations from the Classical, Islamic, and Asian worlds. **Navigational tools** like the compass (from China), **astronomical knowledge** from Islamic scholars, and **shipbuilding techniques** from various maritime cultures enabled Europeans to undertake oceanic voyages. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how this **technological diffusion** wasn't one-directional — Europeans adapted and combined existing knowledge rather than creating everything from scratch. This knowledge exchange facilitated the **Scientific Revolution** and gave Europeans the tools to establish global trade networks.

The most significant change involved labor systems responding to intensifying global demand. College Board wants you to understand that while agriculture remained the foundation of most economies, how agricultural work was organized changed dramatically. **Traditional peasant agriculture** expanded to meet export demands, but more importantly, entirely new systems emerged. **Plantation agriculture** developed in the Americas to produce cash crops like sugar, tobacco, and cotton for global markets. This created an insatiable demand for workers, leading to the expansion and intensification of the **Atlantic slave trade**. The **encomienda** and **hacienda systems** in Spanish America, **indentured servitude** across various colonies, and **serfdom** in Russia all represented different responses to the same economic pressure: the need for cheap, controlled labor to feed global commerce.

These economic developments created profound social transformations that College Board frequently tests. The growth of global trade led to the emergence of new **merchant classes** who accumulated unprecedented wealth, challenging traditional aristocratic power structures. In many societies, **social mobility** increased for those who could participate in long-distance trade, while those trapped in agricultural labor systems found their positions more rigidly defined. **Gender roles** also shifted as global trade created new economic opportunities for some women (particularly in textile production) while plantation systems often subjected enslaved women to both productive and reproductive exploitation. The **Columbian Exchange** didn't just move crops and diseases — it restructured entire social hierarchies.

Finally, this period saw empires achieve massive expansion while being fundamentally changed by the diverse populations they incorporated. The **Spanish Empire** in the Americas, the **Mughal Empire** in India, and the **Ottoman Empire** across three continents all had to develop new methods of governing diverse populations. These **economic disputes** over trade routes, resources, and markets led to increasing **rivalries and conflicts between states**, from the Anglo-Dutch Wars to Spanish-Portuguese competition in the Americas. Understanding these transformations prepares you for analyzing how industrial capitalism would later reshape these same relationships in even more dramatic ways.

## KEY TERMS
- **Transoceanic voyaging** — Long-distance sea travel connecting Eastern and Western Hemispheres for sustained contact
- **Knowledge transfer** — Spread of scientific learning and technology between Classical, Islamic, Asian, and European civilizations
- **Technological diffusion** — Process by which innovations spread across cultures and were adapted for new uses
- **Plantation agriculture** — Large-scale farming system using forced labor to produce cash crops for export
- **Atlantic slave trade** — Forced migration system bringing enslaved Africans to the Americas for agricultural labor
- **Encomienda system** — Spanish colonial labor system granting colonists control over indigenous workers and tribute
- **Hacienda system** — Large estate system in Spanish America combining agriculture and ranching with dependent labor
- **Indentured servitude** — Labor system where workers contracted to work for a fixed period in exchange for passage or debt payment
- **Merchant classes** — New social groups who accumulated wealth through long-distance trade and commercial activities
- **Social mobility** — Ability to move between social classes, often increased by participation in global trade
- **Columbian Exchange** — Transfer of plants, animals, diseases, and people between Americas and Europe/Africa/Asia
- **Economic disputes** — Conflicts between states over trade routes, resources, and market access
