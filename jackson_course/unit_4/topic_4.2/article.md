## ARTICLE CONTENT

The period from 1450 to 1750 marked a revolutionary shift in global connections as European states launched **state-sponsored transoceanic maritime exploration**. College Board wants you to understand that this wasn't just about brave explorers seeking adventure — it was a systematic effort by governments to expand their political and economic power through overseas expansion. Unlike earlier periods when exploration was primarily driven by individual merchants or religious groups, this era saw monarchs directly funding, organizing, and controlling maritime expeditions as official state policy.

European states had compelling economic motivations for sponsoring these expensive and risky voyages. The **Portuguese** under **Prince Henry the Navigator** sought direct access to African gold and slaves, while also finding a sea route to Asian spices that would bypass expensive Muslim middlemen. **Spain** quickly followed, hoping to find their own route to Asia but instead discovering the Americas and their vast silver deposits. On the AP exam, you'll frequently see questions about how the **Ottoman Empire's control of traditional trade routes** to Asia created economic pressure that pushed Europeans to find alternative sea routes. The **Dutch** and **English** joined later, motivated by the massive profits they witnessed from Portuguese and Spanish ventures, particularly the wealth flowing from American silver and Asian luxury goods.

College Board loves testing the direct role states played in organizing and controlling these expeditions. **Portugal** established the **Casa da India** to regulate all Asian trade, while **Spain** created the **Casa de Contratación** to oversee all American colonial activities. These weren't private companies — they were government institutions that controlled licensing, collected taxes, and enforced trade monopolies. The **Dutch East India Company** and **English East India Company**, while technically private corporations, operated with explicit government charters and military support. States provided the capital for ships, weapons, and crews because individual merchants couldn't afford the enormous upfront costs of transoceanic voyages.

The economic effects of state-sponsored exploration transformed both European and global economies. European states gained access to new sources of wealth: **Spanish silver from Potosí**, **Portuguese profits from Asian spice trade**, and **Dutch control of Southeast Asian commerce**. This influx of wealth funded further military expansion and state building. However, College Board also wants you to recognize the global effects — the integration of the Americas into global trade networks, the rise of Atlantic slavery to support plantation agriculture, and the gradual decline of traditional Asian and Muslim trade dominance. The **price revolution** in Europe, caused by American silver inflation, demonstrates how maritime exploration had far-reaching economic consequences beyond simple profit-making.

Understanding state-sponsored exploration is crucial because it sets up the colonial systems and global trade networks you'll study next. These voyages weren't isolated events — they created the foundation for centuries of European colonial dominance and global economic integration.

## KEY TERMS
- **State-sponsored transoceanic maritime exploration** — Government-funded and controlled overseas voyages for political and economic expansion
- **Prince Henry the Navigator** — Portuguese prince who systematically organized early African exploration expeditions
- **Casa da India** — Portuguese government institution that regulated all trade with Asia
- **Casa de Contratación** — Spanish government agency that controlled all trade and colonial activities in the Americas
- **Ottoman Empire's control of traditional trade routes** — Turkish dominance over land routes to Asia that motivated European sea exploration
- **Dutch East India Company** — Government-chartered corporation that controlled Dutch trade in Asia
- **English East India Company** — Government-chartered corporation that managed English trade in Asia
- **Spanish silver from Potosí** — Massive wealth extracted from Bolivian silver mines that funded Spanish expansion
- **Portuguese profits from Asian spice trade** — Wealth gained from direct sea trade with Asian spice producers
- **Price revolution** — European inflation caused by influx of American silver into European economy
- **Atlantic slavery** — Forced labor system that developed to support American plantation agriculture
- **Trade monopolies** — Exclusive government-granted rights to conduct commerce in specific regions
