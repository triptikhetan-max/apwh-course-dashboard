## ARTICLE CONTENT

The **Columbian Exchange** represents one of history's most significant turning points — the massive transfer of plants, animals, diseases, and people between the **Eastern and Western Hemispheres** after 1492. College Board considers this absolutely essential knowledge because it demonstrates how transoceanic connections transformed entire civilizations and ecosystems. This exchange was named after Christopher Columbus, whose voyages initiated permanent contact between the Americas and the rest of the world.

The primary cause of the Columbian Exchange was European maritime technology and exploration that created sustained contact between previously isolated hemispheres. European explorers, conquistadors, and colonists brought **domesticated animals** that revolutionized life in the Americas. **Horses** transformed Native American societies, particularly Plains peoples who became skilled mounted hunters and warriors. **Cattle** provided new sources of protein, leather, and labor, while **pigs** thrived in American environments and became crucial protein sources for colonists. On the AP exam, College Board frequently tests how these animals didn't just provide food — they completely altered indigenous ways of life, military tactics, and agricultural practices.

The exchange of crops created what historians call the first truly global food system. American crops like **maize (corn)**, potatoes, and tomatoes dramatically increased food production in Europe, Africa, and Asia, supporting population growth worldwide. Meanwhile, European crops like wheat and rice took root in the Americas. College Board wants you to understand that **enslaved Africans** played a crucial role in this agricultural exchange, bringing crops like **okra** and **rice** cultivation techniques that became essential to colonial economies, particularly in South Carolina and Georgia. These contributions are often overlooked but were vital to colonial survival and prosperity.

Perhaps most devastating was the exchange of diseases. European diseases like smallpox, measles, and typhus decimated Native American populations who lacked immunity, killing an estimated 90% of indigenous peoples in some regions. This **demographic catastrophe** created labor shortages that drove the expansion of African slavery. Conversely, diseases like syphilis may have traveled from the Americas to Europe. College Board emphasizes understanding this as environmental history — how biological factors shaped human societies and power relationships.

The Columbian Exchange fundamentally altered global demographics, economics, and environments. It connected previously isolated regions into an integrated world system, but at enormous human and environmental costs. Understanding these exchanges prepares you to analyze how global trade networks continued expanding and intensifying in the following centuries, creating the foundation for our modern interconnected world.

## KEY TERMS
- **Columbian Exchange** — Transfer of plants, animals, diseases, and people between Eastern and Western Hemispheres after 1492
- **Eastern Hemisphere** — Europe, Asia, and Africa; regions with previous contact before Columbus
- **Western Hemisphere** — The Americas; isolated from Old World contact until 1492
- **Domesticated animals** — Tamed animals like horses, cattle, and pigs brought by Europeans to the Americas
- **Horses** — Animals that transformed Native American hunting, warfare, and transportation
- **Cattle** — Livestock providing protein, leather, and agricultural labor in the Americas
- **Pigs** — Hardy animals that became crucial protein sources for American colonists
- **Maize (corn)** — American crop that increased food production globally after reaching Europe, Africa, and Asia
- **Okra** — West African vegetable brought to the Americas by enslaved Africans
- **Rice** — Asian grain successfully cultivated in the Americas using African agricultural knowledge
- **Demographic catastrophe** — Massive population decline of Native Americans due to European diseases
- **Disease exchange** — Transfer of illnesses like smallpox to Americas and possibly syphilis to Europe
- **Global food system** — Worldwide network of crop cultivation created by the Columbian Exchange
