## ARTICLE CONTENT

As states expanded and centralized their power from 1450 to 1750, they inevitably faced pushback from groups who lost autonomy or suffered under new policies. College Board wants you to understand this fundamental pattern: **state expansion creates resistance**. This wasn't just random violence — it was organized opposition from people defending their traditional ways of life, economic interests, or basic human rights against increasingly powerful central governments.

Local resistance took many forms across different regions, and the AP exam frequently tests your knowledge of specific examples. In North America, the **Pueblo Revolts** (1680) saw Pueblo peoples unite against Spanish colonial rule, temporarily driving colonizers from New Mexico for over a decade. Similarly, **Metacom's War** (King Philip's War, 1675-1676) represented organized Native American resistance against English colonial expansion in New England. In France, the **Fronde** (1648-1653) saw nobles and parlements rebel against royal centralization under Louis XIV. College Board loves testing the **Cossack revolts** in Russia, where these semi-autonomous warrior communities resisted increasing state control over their traditional freedoms. In South Asia, **Maratha conflicts with the Mughals** demonstrated how local powers could successfully challenge even mighty empires. Perhaps most remarkably, **Ana Nzinga's resistance** as ruler of Ndongo and Matamba showed how African leaders fought Portuguese expansion and the slave trade for decades.

Enslaved persons created their own forms of resistance that College Board considers essential knowledge. **Maroon societies** in the Caribbean and Brazil represented the most successful form of resistance — these were communities of escaped enslaved people who established independent settlements, often in mountainous or swampy terrain that was difficult for colonial authorities to penetrate. These societies preserved African cultural traditions and sometimes signed treaties with colonial governments, forcing recognition of their autonomy. In North America, **resistance of enslaved persons** took different forms due to geographic constraints, including work slowdowns, sabotage, escape attempts via networks like the Underground Railroad, and occasional armed revolts like the Stono Rebellion (1739).

What makes these resistance movements historically significant is how they forced states to adapt their strategies. Successful resistance movements like the Marathas or Maroon societies demonstrated the limits of state power, while unsuccessful ones still showed the human cost of centralization. College Board wants you to recognize that resistance wasn't just about individual acts of defiance — it represented organized political responses to changing power structures.

Understanding patterns of resistance to state power prepares you for analyzing how these same tensions would intensify during the Industrial Revolution, when states gained even more tools for control but also faced new forms of organized opposition from workers and nationalist movements.

## KEY TERMS
- **State expansion** — Process of governments extending their territorial control and administrative reach
- **Centralization** — Concentration of political power in central authorities at the expense of local autonomy
- **Pueblo Revolts** — 1680 uprising of Pueblo peoples against Spanish colonial rule in New Mexico
- **Metacom's War (King Philip's War)** — 1675-1676 conflict between Native Americans and English colonists in New England
- **Fronde** — 1648-1653 series of civil wars in France opposing royal centralization
- **Cossack revolts** — Uprisings by semi-autonomous warrior communities against Russian state control
- **Maratha conflicts** — Series of wars between Maratha confederacy and Mughal Empire in South Asia
- **Ana Nzinga's resistance** — Decades-long opposition by the ruler of Ndongo and Matamba against Portuguese expansion
- **Maroon societies** — Independent communities established by escaped enslaved people in the Americas
- **Resistance of enslaved persons** — Various forms of opposition by enslaved people including escape, sabotage, and revolt
- **Local resistance** — Opposition by regional groups to central government policies and expansion
- **Organized resistance** — Coordinated opposition movements involving multiple participants and strategic planning
