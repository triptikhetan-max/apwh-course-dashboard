## ARTICLE CONTENT

The period from 1450 to 1750 witnessed dramatic transformations in social hierarchies worldwide as imperial expansion and global trade networks created new opportunities and challenges. College Board loves testing this topic because it demonstrates how empires managed diversity differently — some embracing it for practical benefits, others suppressing it to maintain control. Understanding these patterns is crucial for analyzing how power structures adapt to changing circumstances.

Many powerful empires like the **Mughal** and **Ottoman** empires adopted pragmatic approaches to accommodate ethnic and religious diversity among their subjects. The **Ottoman Empire** exemplified this strategy through their **timar system**, where land grants were given to military officers regardless of their ethnic background, creating loyalty across diverse populations. The Ottomans also welcomed **expelled Jews from Spain and Portugal** after 1492, recognizing their valuable commercial and administrative skills. Within the empire, women's status varied significantly by class — elite Ottoman women could wield considerable influence in the imperial court, while lower-class women faced greater restrictions. The Mughal Empire similarly accommodated religious diversity, with Akbar famously promoting religious tolerance to maintain stability across his Hindu-majority, Muslim-ruled empire. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions contrasting these inclusive policies with more restrictive approaches.

However, not all states embraced diversity. The **Qing Dynasty** in China implemented **restrictive policies against Han Chinese**, forcing them to adopt Manchu hairstyles and clothing while prohibiting intermarriage. This demonstrates how conquering minorities sometimes suppress majorities to maintain distinct identities and control. Spain and Portugal's **expulsion of Jews** in the 1490s shows how states sometimes chose religious uniformity over economic benefits, forcing out populations that had contributed significantly to commerce and scholarship. College Board frequently tests these contrasting approaches to diversity — remember that states' choices often reflected their specific security concerns and ideological commitments.

Imperial conquests and expanding global trade networks created entirely new social hierarchies and transformed existing ones. In the Americas, Spanish colonial rule produced the **Casta system**, a complex racial hierarchy that classified people based on their mixed European, African, and indigenous ancestry. This system created new social categories like mestizo and mulatto while ensuring Spanish-born peninsulares remained at the top. Meanwhile, China's transition to the **Qing Dynasty** saw Manchu conquerors become the new political elite while traditional Chinese scholarly elites adapted to foreign rule. In Russia, traditional **boyars** (hereditary nobles) found their power challenged by expanding imperial bureaucracy, while **European nobility** across the continent adapted to new economic opportunities from global trade. These changes show how global connections didn't just affect economics — they fundamentally reshaped who held power and status in societies worldwide.

Understanding these shifting social hierarchies prepares you for analyzing how Enlightenment ideas would later challenge these very power structures, as new concepts of natural rights and equality began to question traditional justifications for social inequality.

## KEY TERMS
- **Mughal Empire** — Islamic empire in India that accommodated religious diversity to maintain stability over Hindu-majority population
- **Ottoman Empire** — Turkish empire that welcomed diverse ethnic and religious groups for their economic and military contributions
- **Timar system** — Ottoman land-grant system that rewarded military service regardless of ethnic background
- **Expelled Jews from Spain and Portugal** — Jewish populations forced out by Christian monarchs but welcomed by the Ottoman Empire after 1492
- **Qing Dynasty** — Manchu dynasty in China that imposed restrictive policies on the Han Chinese majority
- **Restrictive policies against Han Chinese** — Qing requirements forcing Han Chinese to adopt Manchu customs like queue hairstyles
- **Casta system** — Spanish colonial racial hierarchy classifying people by European, African, and indigenous ancestry
- **Russian boyars** — Traditional hereditary nobles whose power was challenged by expanding imperial bureaucracy
- **European nobility** — Traditional aristocratic class that adapted to new wealth from global trade networks
- **Peninsulares** — Spanish-born colonists who held the highest social position in the Casta system
- **Mestizo** — People of mixed European and indigenous ancestry in Spanish colonial hierarchy
- **Mulatto** — People of mixed European and African ancestry in Spanish colonial social system
