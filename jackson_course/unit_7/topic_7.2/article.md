## ARTICLE CONTENT

World War I didn't just "happen" — it was the devastating result of multiple interconnected tensions that had been building across Europe for decades. College Board frequently tests your ability to analyze these **multiple causation** patterns, so understanding how imperialist competition, flawed alliances, and nationalism worked together is crucial for exam success.

**Imperialist expansion and competition for resources** created the first major source of tension. By 1914, European powers were locked in fierce competition for colonies and markets worldwide. Germany's rapid industrial growth after 1871 challenged British economic dominance, while the **"Scramble for Africa"** had created numerous colonial disputes. France and Germany clashed over Morocco in 1905 and 1911, while Austria-Hungary's expansion into the Balkans threatened Russian interests in the region. This wasn't just about prestige — colonies provided raw materials, markets for manufactured goods, and naval bases essential for global trade. The AP exam often asks you to connect this imperial competition to the alliance system, because nations sought military partners to protect their colonial interests.

The **alliance system** that was supposed to maintain peace actually made war more likely by creating a web of obligations that could turn any regional conflict into a continental war. The **Triple Alliance** (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) faced off against the **Triple Entente** (France, Russia, Britain), while smaller nations like Serbia had their own protective agreements with major powers. When Archduke Franz Ferdinand was assassinated by a Serbian nationalist in Sarajevo on June 28, 1914, this flawed system kicked into gear: Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia, Russia mobilized to protect Serbia, Germany declared war on Russia to support Austria-Hungary, and France and Britain entered to honor their commitments to Russia. College Board loves testing this **"domino effect"** — how a regional incident in the Balkans became a world war through alliance obligations.

**Intense nationalism** provided the emotional fuel that made these political and economic tensions explosive. In the **Austro-Hungarian Empire**, Slavic peoples like Serbs, Croats, and Czechs increasingly demanded independence or unification with their ethnic kin in other nations. **Pan-Slavism** encouraged Russia to see itself as protector of all Slavic peoples, putting it on a collision course with Austria-Hungary in the Balkans. Meanwhile, French **revanchism** (desire for revenge) over the loss of Alsace-Lorraine to Germany in 1871 kept Franco-German tensions high. German nationalism, fueled by rapid economic growth, demanded a "place in the sun" equal to Britain's global empire. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how these competing nationalisms made compromise impossible — each nation saw backing down as a betrayal of national honor.

**Territorial and regional conflicts** in the Balkans created the immediate spark for war. The decline of the **Ottoman Empire** had created a power vacuum that Austria-Hungary and Russia both sought to fill. The **Balkan Wars** of 1912-1913 had already demonstrated how quickly regional conflicts could escalate, while Serbia's success in these wars made it increasingly bold in challenging Austrian authority. When the **Black Hand**, a Serbian nationalist organization, orchestrated Franz Ferdinand's assassination, it gave Austria-Hungary the pretext it needed to crush Serbian nationalism once and for all. Understanding these Balkan tensions is essential because College Board frequently tests how the war's immediate cause (the assassination) connected to its deeper structural causes (imperialism, alliances, nationalism).

## KEY TERMS
- **Multiple causation** — Historical concept showing how several interconnected factors combine to cause major events
- **Scramble for Africa** — Late 19th-century competition among European powers to colonize African territories
- **Triple Alliance** — Military alliance between Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy (formed 1882)
- **Triple Entente** — Military understanding between France, Russia, and Britain (formed 1907)
- **Pan-Slavism** — Nationalist movement promoting unity among all Slavic peoples
- **Revanchism** — French nationalist desire to regain Alsace-Lorraine from Germany
- **Balkan Wars** — Two conflicts (1912-1913) that increased tensions in southeastern Europe
- **Black Hand** — Serbian nationalist secret society responsible for Franz Ferdinand's assassination
- **Archduke Franz Ferdinand** — Heir to Austro-Hungarian throne whose assassination triggered WWI
- **Alliance system** — Network of military agreements that divided Europe into opposing camps
- **Nationalism** — Political ideology emphasizing loyalty to one's nation above other considerations
- **Imperial competition** — Rivalry between major powers for colonies, resources, and global influence
- **Balkan powder keg** — Metaphor describing how Balkan tensions could explode into wider conflict
