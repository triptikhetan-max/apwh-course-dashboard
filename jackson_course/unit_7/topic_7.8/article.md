## ARTICLE CONTENT

The twentieth and twenty-first centuries witnessed unprecedented mass atrocities that targeted specific ethnic, religious, and political groups. College Board emphasizes this topic because it demonstrates how modern technology, extreme ideologies, and state power combined to create systematic campaigns of destruction that affected millions of people worldwide.

The **Armenian Genocide** (1915-1923) set a horrific precedent for modern mass killing. As the Ottoman Empire crumbled during World War I, the **Young Turk** government blamed Armenians for military defeats and accused them of collaborating with Russia. Beginning in April 1915, Ottoman authorities systematically deported over one million Armenians from their homes, forcing them on death marches into the Syrian desert. An estimated 800,000 to 1.5 million Armenians died through execution, starvation, and disease. This genocide introduced methods later used by other regimes: **dehumanization** through propaganda, systematic deportation, and the use of modern transportation and communication to coordinate mass killing. The international community's limited response would unfortunately become a pattern throughout the century.

The **Holocaust** (1941-1945) represents the most systematically planned genocide in human history. Nazi Germany's **Final Solution** aimed to completely exterminate European Jewry, ultimately murdering six million Jews along with millions of others including Roma, disabled individuals, and political prisoners. The Nazis perfected **industrial killing** through concentration camps like Auschwitz, using gas chambers, crematoria, and detailed record-keeping that reflected modern bureaucratic efficiency. College Board frequently tests how the Holocaust built upon earlier anti-Semitic traditions while using uniquely modern methods — railroads transported victims, IBM punch cards tracked deportations, and scientific racism provided ideological justification. The scale and systematic nature of Nazi crimes led to the creation of new international laws against **genocide** and **crimes against humanity**.

The Soviet Union's **Holodomor** (1932-1933) demonstrated how authoritarian regimes could weaponize food to eliminate unwanted populations. Stalin's government deliberately created famine conditions in Ukraine through **forced collectivization** and grain requisitions, resulting in 3-7 million Ukrainian deaths. This **man-made famine** targeted Ukrainian nationalism and peasant resistance to collectivization. Similarly, the **Cambodian Genocide** (1975-1979) under the **Khmer Rouge** killed approximately 1.5-2 million people — nearly 25% of Cambodia's population. Pol Pot's regime targeted intellectuals, religious minorities, and anyone associated with urban or foreign culture in their radical attempt to create an agrarian utopia. The **Rwandan Genocide** (1994) showed how quickly mass killing could unfold in the modern era, with Hutu extremists murdering approximately 800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus in just 100 days using primarily machetes and clubs, coordinated through radio broadcasts.

Understanding these atrocities reveals common patterns that College Board wants you to recognize: extremist ideologies that dehumanize target groups, the use of propaganda to mobilize ordinary citizens as perpetrators, and the exploitation of existing ethnic or religious tensions. These events also demonstrate the international community's ongoing struggle to prevent and respond to genocide, leading to concepts like "responsibility to protect" and international criminal tribunals.

## KEY TERMS
- **Armenian Genocide** — Systematic extermination of 1-1.5 million Armenians by Ottoman Empire (1915-1923)
- **Young Turks** — Nationalist political movement that controlled Ottoman Empire during WWI
- **Dehumanization** — Process of portraying targeted groups as less than human to justify violence
- **Holocaust** — Nazi Germany's systematic murder of six million Jews and millions of others (1941-1945)
- **Final Solution** — Nazi plan to completely exterminate European Jewish population
- **Industrial killing** — Use of modern technology and methods to systematically murder large populations
- **Genocide** — Deliberate attempt to destroy a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group
- **Crimes against humanity** — Systematic attacks against civilian populations
- **Holodomor** — Man-made famine in Soviet Ukraine that killed 3-7 million (1932-1933)
- **Forced collectivization** — Stalin's policy requiring peasants to give up private farms for collective ones
- **Khmer Rouge** — Communist regime in Cambodia responsible for genocide (1975-1979)
- **Rwandan Genocide** — Mass killing of approximately 800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus in 1994
- **Responsibility to protect** — International principle that states must protect populations from mass atrocities
