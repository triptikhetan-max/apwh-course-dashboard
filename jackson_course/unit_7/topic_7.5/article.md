## ARTICLE CONTENT

The end of World War I didn't bring lasting peace — instead, it created a powder keg of unresolved tensions that would explode into an even deadlier conflict just twenty years later. College Board focuses heavily on this topic because it perfectly demonstrates how imperial competition and colonial resistance movements shaped the interwar period, setting up the causes of World War II.

The **League of Nations mandate system** fundamentally reshaped the imperial landscape after Germany's defeat. Rather than granting independence to former German colonies, the victorious Allied powers simply redistributed them among themselves under the guise of "preparing" these territories for eventual self-rule. Britain gained control over German East Africa (Tanganyika) and parts of German New Guinea, while France received Cameroon and Togo. This system was imperial expansion disguised as international cooperation — the mandates gave Western powers legal justification for controlling resource-rich territories while paying lip service to self-determination. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions contrasting Wilson's Fourteen Points rhetoric about self-determination with the reality of continued imperial control through mandates.

Meanwhile, Japan aggressively expanded its territorial holdings in East Asia, creating major tensions with Western powers and local populations. The Japanese invasion of Manchuria in 1931 established the puppet state of **Manchukuo**, giving Japan control over crucial industrial resources and strategic territory. This expansion accelerated under the ideology of the **Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere**, Japan's propaganda campaign that claimed to be liberating Asia from Western imperialism while actually establishing Japanese hegemony. College Board frequently tests how Japan's imperial expansion in China violated the Washington Naval Conference agreements and League of Nations principles, demonstrating the weakness of interwar international institutions.

However, the most significant development was the rise of organized **anti-imperial resistance movements** that would eventually dismantle the colonial system. In India, the **Indian National Congress** transformed from a moderate reform organization into a mass independence movement under leaders like Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru. The Congress organized boycotts, non-cooperation campaigns, and civil disobedience that challenged British rule's legitimacy. In West Africa, colonial subjects used **strikes and political congresses** to resist French rule, particularly protesting forced labor, taxation, and cultural suppression. These movements were crucial because they developed the organizational structures, ideologies, and leadership that would ultimately achieve decolonization after World War II.

The territorial changes and resistance movements of the interwar period reveal a fundamental contradiction: while imperial powers expanded their control through mandates and conquest, they simultaneously faced increasingly sophisticated challenges to colonial rule. This tension between imperial expansion and anti-colonial resistance created the unstable conditions that contributed to World War II's outbreak and eventual decolonization.

## KEY TERMS
- **League of Nations mandate system** — International system that redistributed German colonies to Allied powers under supposed temporary administration
- **Manchukuo** — Japanese puppet state established in Manchuria (1932-1945) following Japanese invasion
- **Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere** — Japanese propaganda concept claiming to liberate Asia from Western imperialism while establishing Japanese control
- **Indian National Congress** — Indian political organization that evolved into the leading independence movement against British rule
- **Anti-imperial resistance** — Organized opposition movements challenging colonial rule through political, economic, and cultural means
- **West African resistance movements** — Colonial subjects' use of strikes and congresses to oppose French colonial policies
- **Mandate territories** — Former German and Ottoman territories administered by Allied powers under League of Nations supervision
- **Self-determination** — Principle that peoples should choose their own government, promoted by Wilson but selectively applied
- **Non-cooperation campaigns** — Indian resistance strategy of refusing to participate in British colonial institutions
- **Colonial nationalism** — Political movements seeking independence from imperial rule based on shared identity and opposition to foreign control
- **Imperial expansion** — Process of extending political and economic control over foreign territories
- **Decolonization** — Process of colonies gaining independence from imperial powers
