## ARTICLE CONTENT

Understanding the causes of global conflict from 1900 to present is crucial for AP World History because College Board wants you to think like a historian — analyzing multiple causation and determining which factors were most significant. The 20th and 21st centuries witnessed unprecedented global warfare, from two World Wars to regional conflicts that reshaped the entire world order. These conflicts didn't emerge from single causes but from complex webs of **technological change**, **imperial competition**, **ideological conflicts**, and **challenges to established power structures**.

**Technological and scientific advances** fundamentally altered how conflicts developed and were fought. The Second Industrial Revolution produced new weapons of mass destruction — machine guns, chemical weapons, aircraft, and eventually nuclear weapons — that made warfare deadlier than ever before. But technology also created new tensions: the telegraph and radio enabled faster communication that could escalate diplomatic crises, while industrial capacity became essential for military power, intensifying competition between nations. College Board frequently tests how technological change didn't just affect how wars were fought, but actually became a cause of conflict as nations competed for technological and industrial supremacy. For example, the naval arms race between Britain and Germany before World War I directly contributed to rising tensions, while the development of nuclear weapons during World War II fundamentally altered the Cold War dynamic.

The collapse of traditional imperial systems created power vacuums that sparked numerous conflicts throughout the 20th century. As the video likely explains, both **land-based empires** (like the Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian empires) and **maritime empires** (like British and French colonial systems) gave way to new states by the century's end. This process wasn't peaceful — the breakup of empires created ethnic conflicts, border disputes, and competition for resources. The **Mexican Revolution** (1910-1920) exemplifies how internal political crises within established systems could spiral into major conflicts that influenced global patterns. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions comparing different decolonization processes and how the end of imperial control created new sources of conflict in regions from Africa to Southeast Asia.

**Ideological competition** became perhaps the most significant cause of global conflict as traditional political and social orders faced unprecedented challenges. The rise of **fascism**, **communism**, and competing forms of **nationalism** created irreconcilable worldviews that drove countries into conflict. Unlike earlier conflicts fought primarily over territory or resources, 20th-century wars often became struggles over fundamental questions about how societies should be organized. The **Cold War** perfectly demonstrates this — it wasn't just a power struggle between the United States and Soviet Union, but a global ideological conflict that sparked proxy wars across multiple continents. College Board loves testing how these ideological conflicts intersected with decolonization movements, creating complex situations where local independence struggles became part of larger global conflicts.

The relative significance of these causes varied by time period and region, but College Board expects you to argue that the combination of technological change, imperial collapse, and ideological competition created a perfect storm for global conflict. Understanding these patterns helps explain not just historical conflicts, but ongoing tensions in the contemporary world.

## KEY TERMS
- **Second Industrial Revolution** — Period of rapid technological advancement (1870-1914) that transformed military capabilities
- **Imperial competition** — Rivalry between empires for territory, resources, and global influence
- **Land-based empires** — Traditional empires like Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian that collapsed in early 20th century
- **Maritime empires** — Overseas colonial empires like British and French systems that ended through decolonization
- **Mexican Revolution** — Major political and social revolution (1910-1920) arising from internal political crisis
- **Fascism** — Authoritarian ultranationalist ideology that emerged in early 20th century Europe
- **Communism** — Socialist ideology advocating collective ownership and revolutionary change
- **Nationalism** — Ideology emphasizing national identity and self-determination, often leading to conflicts
- **Cold War** — Ideological and geopolitical conflict between capitalist and communist blocs (1947-1991)
- **Proxy wars** — Conflicts where major powers support opposing sides without direct confrontation
- **Decolonization** — Process of colonies gaining independence from imperial powers
- **Power vacuum** — Situation where collapse of authority creates instability and competition
- **Weapons of mass destruction** — Chemical, biological, and nuclear weapons that caused unprecedented casualties
- **Arms race** — Competitive military buildup between rival nations or blocs
