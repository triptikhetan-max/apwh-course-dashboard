## ARTICLE CONTENT

World War II didn't just happen overnight — it was the devastating result of multiple interconnected causes that built up over two decades. College Board consistently tests this topic because it demonstrates how unresolved problems from one conflict can directly cause another, a pattern that repeats throughout history. Understanding these causes is crucial for analyzing how global conflicts develop and why the "war to end all wars" actually led to an even more destructive conflict just twenty years later.

The **Treaty of Versailles** (1919) created an unsustainable peace that practically guaranteed future conflict. This treaty imposed crushing **war guilt** and **reparations** on Germany, forcing them to accept full responsibility for World War I and pay enormous sums to the Allies. The treaty also stripped Germany of significant territory, including the **Rhineland**, **Alsace-Lorraine**, and all overseas colonies, while limiting their military to just 100,000 men. As the video explains, these harsh terms humiliated Germany and created deep resentment that **Adolf Hitler** would later exploit. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions about how the treaty's punitive measures backfired — instead of preventing German aggression, they created the perfect conditions for a revanchist leader to gain power by promising to restore German greatness.

The **Great Depression** (1929-1939) transformed economic crisis into political catastrophe across the globe. When the American stock market crashed, it triggered a worldwide economic collapse that devastated Germany, which was already struggling with war reparations. Unemployment soared to over 6 million Germans by 1932, creating desperation that extremist parties exploited. **Fascist** and **totalitarian** movements like Hitler's **Nazi Party** in Germany and **Mussolini's** regime in Italy gained power by promising simple solutions to complex problems. In Japan, military leaders blamed the West for their economic troubles and pushed for aggressive expansion to secure resources. College Board frequently tests how economic instability enables radical political movements — remember that people facing unemployment and poverty become willing to support extreme solutions.

**Aggressive militarism** and continued **imperialist aspirations** turned these unstable conditions into actual warfare. Nazi Germany began **rearmament** in violation of Versailles, remilitarized the Rhineland (1936), annexed **Austria** in the **Anschluss** (1938), and demanded the **Sudetenland** from Czechoslovakia. Meanwhile, Japan had already begun its imperial expansion by invading **Manchuria** (1931) and launching a full-scale war against **China** (1937). Italy conquered **Ethiopia** (1935-36) to build its African empire. The policy of **appeasement** by Britain and France — most famously at the **Munich Conference** (1938) — only encouraged further aggression by showing that fascist powers could take territory without facing serious consequences.

The final spark came when Hitler invaded **Poland** on September 1, 1939, finally forcing Britain and France to honor their guarantees and declare war. But this wasn't a sudden decision — it was the inevitable result of the toxic combination of Versailles resentment, economic desperation, fascist ideology, and failed diplomacy that had been building since 1919. Understanding these interconnected causes helps explain why World War II became even more devastating than its predecessor.

## KEY TERMS
- **Treaty of Versailles** — 1919 peace treaty that imposed harsh terms on Germany after WWI
- **War guilt clause** — Article 231 of Versailles forcing Germany to accept full responsibility for WWI
- **Reparations** — Massive payments Germany was required to make to Allied nations
- **Adolf Hitler** — Nazi leader who exploited German resentment to gain power and start WWII
- **Great Depression** — Global economic collapse (1929-1939) that destabilized governments worldwide
- **Fascist** — Extreme nationalist, authoritarian political movement that emerged after WWI
- **Totalitarian** — Government system with complete control over all aspects of society
- **Nazi Party** — Hitler's fascist party that gained power in Germany during the Depression
- **Aggressive militarism** — Policy of building up military forces and using them to achieve political goals
- **Rearmament** — Germany's rebuilding of military forces in violation of Versailles Treaty
- **Anschluss** — Nazi Germany's annexation of Austria in 1938
- **Appeasement** — Policy of making concessions to aggressive powers to avoid conflict
- **Munich Conference** — 1938 meeting where Britain and France gave Hitler the Sudetenland
- **Manchuria** — Chinese territory invaded by Japan in 1931, beginning Japanese expansion
