## ARTICLE CONTENT

Welcome to Unit 7! As we enter the 20th century, you're witnessing one of history's most dramatic power shifts. At 1900, **Western imperial powers** — Britain, France, Germany, and others — dominated the global political order through vast **maritime empires** and **land-based empires**. But by 2000, this world had completely transformed. College Board loves testing this transformation because it demonstrates how both internal pressures and external forces can rapidly reshape entire civilizations.

The decline of Western dominance didn't happen overnight — it resulted from multiple internal and external factors working together. Externally, two devastating **World Wars** drained European resources and exposed the vulnerability of imperial powers. The rise of **anti-colonial movements** across Asia and Africa challenged European control, while new ideologies like **communism** and **nationalism** provided alternatives to Western political models. Internally, European societies faced economic crises, political instability, and growing demands for democracy and social reform. As you'll see throughout this unit, these external pressures and internal contradictions created perfect conditions for revolutionary change.

One crucial example that College Board frequently tests is the **Mexican Revolution** (1910-1920), which perfectly illustrates how internal political crisis can trigger massive social transformation. Under dictator **Porfirio Díaz**, Mexico had achieved economic growth but at enormous social cost — foreign companies dominated key industries, **hacienda** owners controlled vast estates, and peasants lived in poverty. When Díaz rigged the 1910 election, it sparked a decade-long revolution involving multiple factions. **Francisco Madero** initially led the movement for political democracy, while **Emiliano Zapata** fought for **land redistribution** under the slogan "Tierra y Libertad" (Land and Liberty). **Pancho Villa** represented northern revolutionaries seeking social justice. The revolution ultimately produced the **Constitution of 1917**, which established land reform, workers' rights, and limits on foreign ownership — directly challenging the existing political and social order that had favored elites and foreign investors.

The broader pattern you should recognize is how **land-based empires** like the Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, and Russian empires collapsed under the pressure of modern warfare and internal nationalism, while **maritime empires** like the British and French gradually lost their overseas territories to independence movements. By century's end, new states had emerged across Africa, Asia, and Latin America, while established powers had to adapt to a **multipolar world** where Western dominance was no longer guaranteed. On the AP exam, you'll often see questions asking you to compare how different regions responded to these pressures — some through revolution, others through gradual reform, and still others through violent conflict.

Understanding these shifting power dynamics after 1900 sets the foundation for everything that follows in Unit 7. The collapse of the old order created the conditions for global conflicts, new ideologies, and the modern state system we know today.

## KEY TERMS
- **Western imperial powers** — European nations (Britain, France, Germany, etc.) that dominated global politics through colonial empires
- **Maritime empires** — Overseas colonial empires controlled through naval power and distant territories
- **Land-based empires** — Contiguous empires like Ottoman, Russian, and Austro-Hungarian controlled through land armies
- **Anti-colonial movements** — Political and military resistance to European imperial rule in Asia and Africa
- **Nationalism** — Ideology emphasizing shared identity and self-determination of distinct peoples
- **Mexican Revolution** — Social revolution (1910-1920) that overthrew dictatorship and established social reforms
- **Porfirio Díaz** — Mexican dictator (1876-1911) whose policies favored foreign investors and wealthy elites
- **Hacienda** — Large estates in Latin America that concentrated land ownership among elites
- **Francisco Madero** — Mexican revolutionary leader who initially championed political democracy
- **Emiliano Zapata** — Revolutionary leader who fought for land redistribution and peasant rights
- **Pancho Villa** — Northern Mexican revolutionary representing social justice movements
- **Constitution of 1917** — Mexican constitution establishing land reform, workers' rights, and economic nationalism
- **Land redistribution** — Policy of breaking up large estates and distributing land to peasants
- **Multipolar world** — Global system with multiple centers of power rather than Western dominance
