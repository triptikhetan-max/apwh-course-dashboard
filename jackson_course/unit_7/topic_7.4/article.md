## ARTICLE CONTENT

The period between World War I and World War II marked a dramatic shift in how governments approached their economies. The devastation of WWI, followed by the catastrophic **Great Depression** beginning in 1929, forced governments worldwide to abandon the traditional **laissez-faire** approach and take unprecedented control over their economic systems. College Board frequently tests this fundamental transformation because it represents one of the most significant changes in state power in modern history.

In the United States, President Franklin D. Roosevelt's **New Deal** (1933-1939) exemplified the new activist government approach. Facing 25% unemployment and massive bank failures, Roosevelt implemented programs like the **Works Progress Administration (WPA)**, which employed millions in public works projects, and **Social Security**, which provided safety nets for the elderly and unemployed. The **Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA)** demonstrated direct government involvement in economic development through infrastructure projects. As you'll see on AP exams, the New Deal represented a middle path between capitalism and socialism — maintaining private property while dramatically expanding government regulation and spending. This wasn't just American innovation; it reflected a global trend toward **Keynesian economics**, which argued that governments must actively manage demand during economic downturns.

Fascist nations took government economic intervention even further through **corporatist economies**. In Nazi Germany and Mussolini's Italy, the state coordinated production between government, business leaders, and controlled labor unions, eliminating independent worker organizations. This system maintained private ownership while ensuring all economic activity served state goals, particularly rearmament. **Autarky** (economic self-sufficiency) became a key fascist goal, as these regimes prepared for war. College Board often contrasts this with democratic responses — fascist corporatism used economic control to build military power and suppress opposition, while democratic interventions like the New Deal aimed to preserve individual freedoms while providing economic security.

Beyond Europe and North America, **governments with strong popular support in Brazil and Mexico** also expanded their economic roles during this period. In Brazil, **Getúlio Vargas** (1930-1945) implemented **Estado Novo** (New State), promoting industrialization through government investment and nationalist economic policies. Similarly, Mexico under **Lázaro Cárdenas** (1934-1940) nationalized the oil industry and redistributed land, demonstrating how developing nations used state power to reduce foreign economic domination. These examples show how economic nationalism spread globally — governments worldwide recognized that passive approaches couldn't address the scale of interwar economic challenges.

The interwar period fundamentally transformed the relationship between states and economies. Whether through democratic reforms, fascist coordination, or nationalist development programs, governments everywhere abandoned the pre-1914 assumption that economies functioned best with minimal state interference. This shift would define economic policy for the rest of the twentieth century.

## KEY TERMS
- **Great Depression** — Global economic collapse beginning in 1929 that lasted throughout the 1930s
- **Laissez-faire** — Economic philosophy advocating minimal government intervention in markets
- **New Deal** — Franklin D. Roosevelt's programs (1933-1939) using government spending to combat the Depression
- **Works Progress Administration (WPA)** — New Deal program employing millions in public works projects
- **Social Security** — New Deal program providing safety nets for elderly, unemployed, and disabled Americans
- **Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA)** — Government program developing infrastructure in the American South
- **Keynesian economics** — Theory that governments should actively manage economic demand during downturns
- **Corporatist economies** — Fascist system coordinating production between state, business, and controlled labor
- **Autarky** — Economic policy aimed at national self-sufficiency and reduced foreign dependence
- **Estado Novo** — Getúlio Vargas's Brazilian state (1937-1945) promoting industrialization and nationalism
- **Getúlio Vargas** — Brazilian leader who expanded government economic role and promoted industrialization
- **Lázaro Cárdenas** — Mexican president (1934-1940) who nationalized oil and redistributed land
- **Economic nationalism** — Policy prioritizing national economic interests over international cooperation
