### ITEM 2: Article — "What is Historical Situation?"

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "What is Historical Situation?"
- **Source:** GENERATE from `/Users/tripti/Downloads/final_pdfs/qc_pipeline/reports/hipp_course/module_2_historical_situation.md`, sections "Before You Read a Single Word" and "Why This Matters on the Exam (The Point Math)"
- **Scrape Instructions:** None — content is already written in the module article.
- **Content:** Build the article from these two sections verbatim. The content covers:

**Section A: "Before You Read a Single Word"**

Every document on the DBQ comes with a source line — an italicized blurb above the text that tells you who wrote it, when, where, and why. Most students skip past this. Historians do the opposite. They read the source line first, because it tells them how to interpret everything that follows.

Historical Situation — the "H" in HIPP — is about reading that source line and asking one question: **What was happening in the world when and where this document was created?**

Not what the document says. What was going on around it.

Think of it like the opening shot of a movie. Before any character speaks, the camera pans across a city, a battlefield, a factory floor. That shot tells you where you are and what kind of story you are about to hear. Historical Situation is that opening shot for a document.

**Section B: "Why This Matters on the Exam (The Point Math)"**

- The DBQ is scored on a 7-point rubric. One point is the sourcing point, earned by doing HIPP analysis on at least 2 documents.
- Historical Situation is the most straightforward of the four HIPP elements.
- Since 2023: HIPP analysis on 4+ documents can earn the complexity point. So strong sourcing can unlock 2 points total.
- Historical Situation also helps on MCQs: 39 of 55 MCQs are stimulus-based.

### ITEM 3: Article — "The Golden Rule"

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "The Golden Rule: '...and that matters because'"
- **Source:** GENERATE from `/Users/tripti/Downloads/final_pdfs/qc_pipeline/reports/hipp_course/module_2_historical_situation.md`, section "The Golden Rule"
- **Scrape Instructions:** None — content is already written.
- **Content:** Build the article from the Golden Rule section. Key content:

**The single most important idea in this module:**

Identifying Historical Situation is not enough. You must explain WHY it matters.

The AP rubric does not say "identify" the historical situation. It says "explain how or why" the historical situation is relevant. That is a two-step process:

1. **Identify** what was happening (Step 1)
2. **Explain** why that context shapes the document (Step 2)

**Bad example (0 points):**
> "This was written during the Industrial Revolution."

That is identification only. It names a time period. It does not explain anything.

**Good example (earns the point):**
> "This was written during the Industrial Revolution, and that matters because rapid factory growth created the exploitative labor conditions the author is describing, which explains why the tone is so urgent."

The difference between 0 points and 1 point is six words: **"and that matters because..."**

### ITEM 7: Article — Common Mistakes

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "4 Common Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)"
- **Source:** GENERATE (mistake descriptions already written in module article) + external scraping
- **Scrape Instructions:**

  5. Anonymize all student names (use "Student A", "Student B", etc.).

  **Source 2 — 2024 Chief Reader Report:**
  1. Navigate to: https://apcentral.collegeboard.org/media/pdf/ap24-cr-report-world-history-modern-set-1.pdf
  2. Find the section discussing the **sourcing point** (typically in the DBQ analysis section).
  3. Extract the Chief Reader's comments on common errors students made when attempting sourcing/HIPP — specifically the pattern of identification without explanation.
  4. Note any statistics on how many students earned vs. missed the sourcing point.

- **Content:**

**Mistake 1: Too vague.**
"This was written in China." When in China? What was happening? China has 5,000 years of history. You need to name a specific development, event, or trend. Not a country. Not a century. A situation.

**Mistake 2: Confusing Historical Situation with Contextualization.**
- Historical Situation (HIPP) = What was happening when THIS SPECIFIC DOCUMENT was created? Applied to individual documents inside body paragraphs.
- Contextualization (rubric point) = What broader historical background sets up the ENTIRE ESSAY topic? Goes in the introduction paragraph.

Historical Situation is zoomed in. Contextualization is zoomed out. They are related but NOT the same thing, and they earn different points on the rubric.

**Mistake 3: Writing about what the document SAYS instead of what was happening AROUND it.**
If your analysis starts with "The author argues that..." or "This document describes..." you are summarizing content, not analyzing context. Stop. Look at the source line again. What was happening in that place, at that time, that made this document come into existence?

**Mistake 4: Identification without explanation.**
The most common mistake on the entire exam. You name the event. You do not explain why it matters. Go back and add the "and that matters because" clause. Every single time.

### SELF-CHECK CHECKLIST (displayed above ALL practice docs)

**Before you submit ANY Historical Situation analysis, ask yourself these 4 questions:**

1. ✅ Did I name a **specific** historical event, trend, or development? (Not just "this was written in China")
