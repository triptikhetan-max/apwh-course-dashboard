### ITEM 1: Article — Review and Self-Check Checklist

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "Full HIPP: Before You Start, Review the Checklist"
- **Source:** GENERATE
- **Scrape Instructions:** None.
- **Content:**

*"You have now learned all four HIPP elements individually. In this module, you will apply ALL of them to each document — just like you will on the real DBQ.*

*Before each document, use this self-check checklist. It combines everything you have learned."*

**FULL HIPP SELF-CHECK CHECKLIST**

Before you submit ANY full HIPP analysis, ask yourself:

**H — Historical Situation:**
1. Did I name a specific historical event, trend, or development?
2. Did I include "...and that matters because" or equivalent?
3. Am I describing what was happening AROUND the document, not what the document SAYS?

**I — Intended Audience:**
4. Did I name a SPECIFIC audience? (Not "everyone" or "the public")
5. Did I explain how knowing the audience shapes the document?

**P — Purpose:**
6. Did I use a specific action verb? (Not "to inform" or "to share")
7. Did I explain why the purpose matters to how we read the document?

**POV — Point of View:**
8. Did I use the "As a [role/identity]..." frame or equivalent?
9. Did I explain HOW the author's identity shapes THIS specific document?
10. Did I avoid just saying "biased" without explaining how or why?

> **AKSHIT:** Display this checklist as a persistent collapsible card above EVERY practice document in this module. Students should reference it before each submission.

### ITEM 2: Practice Doc 1 — Full HIPP with Guided Questions

- **Content Type:** frq_writing (multi-part)
- **Title:** "Full HIPP: Document 1 (Guided)"
- **Source:** 2018 FRQ PDF, Document 2 (Memorandum to Qing court on railroads)
- **Scrape Instructions:**
  1. Open `/Users/tripti/Downloads/final_pdfs/qc_pipeline/reports/hipp_research/released_ap_docs/frq_sets/2018_frq.pdf`
  2. Navigate to the DBQ section. Find **Document 2**.
  3. Extract the **full source line** verbatim. Per the catalog: *"Memorandum to the Qing court, late 19th century, discussing whether China should build telegraphs and railroads; argues foreigners must not build them"*
  4. Extract the **full document text** below the source line.
  5. Preserve all original formatting.
- **Content:**

**Document displayed to student:**
Show the complete Document 2 from the 2018 DBQ (source line + full text) in a styled document card.

**Guided Questions (one text box per element):**

**H — Historical Situation:** "What was happening in China in the late 19th century that makes this memorandum significant? Think about the self-strengthening movement, foreign encroachment, and debates about modernization."

**I — Intended Audience:** "Who was this memorandum written for? What does the audience's power and position tell you about why the author framed the argument this way?"

**P — Purpose:** "What was the author trying to get the Qing court to DO? Not what does the memorandum SAY, but what action does the author want?"

**POV — Point of View:** "How does the author's identity as a Chinese official shape this memorandum? What would a foreign advisor writing about the same topic emphasize differently?"

**Historical Context Hint (collapsible):**
> In the late 19th century, the Qing Dynasty was debating how to modernize China while resisting foreign control. The "self-strengthening movement" advocated adopting Western technology (railroads, telegraphs) but keeping it under Chinese control — "Chinese learning as the foundation, Western learning for practical use."

- **Autograder Rubric (score each element separately: H: 0/1, I: 0/1, P: 0/1, POV: 0/1):**

  **H — Score 0:** Names China or Qing Dynasty without explanation. **Score 1:** References self-strengthening movement, foreign encroachment, or modernization debates AND explains why that context matters to this memorandum.

  **I — Score 0:** Says "the government" or "everyone." **Score 1:** Identifies the Qing court/emperor AND explains how that audience shapes the argument (e.g., author must persuade imperial decision-makers, so frames argument in terms of sovereignty and national security).

  **P — Score 0:** Summarizes content or says "to inform." **Score 1:** Identifies a specific purpose (persuade the court to allow modernization but block foreign control) AND explains why that purpose shapes the document.

  **POV — Score 0:** Says "biased" or summarizes. **Score 1:** Identifies the author as a Chinese official AND explains how that identity shapes the memorandum (e.g., suspicion of foreign influence, emphasis on sovereignty, desire to control modernization from within).

- **Model Answer (shown after submission — H5P Reveal):**

  **H:** "The historical situation was the late Qing self-strengthening movement, when Chinese officials debated whether to adopt Western technology while resisting foreign political control. This matters because the memorandum's central argument — build railroads and telegraphs, but do not let foreigners build them — directly reflects this tension between modernization and sovereignty."

  **I:** "The intended audience was the Qing court and the emperor, who had the authority to approve or reject infrastructure projects. This matters because the author framed the argument in terms of sovereignty and national security — the language most likely to persuade imperial decision-makers — rather than in terms of economic profit or technological progress."

  **P:** "The purpose was to persuade the Qing court to adopt Western technology under Chinese control while blocking foreign companies from building infrastructure inside China. This matters because the memorandum's emphasis on the dangers of foreign-built railroads (loss of sovereignty, foreign military access) was designed to convince the court that Chinese-controlled modernization was the only safe path."

  **POV:** "As a Chinese official writing during a period of foreign encroachment, the author's perspective was shaped by deep suspicion of foreign intentions. This explains why the memorandum frames railroad construction primarily as a sovereignty issue rather than an economic opportunity — the author assumes that foreign involvement inevitably leads to foreign control, a perspective that reflects Chinese officials' experience with unequal treaties and foreign concessions."

- **Feedback:** Show element-by-element scores with targeted feedback for any element scoring 0.
- **Order:** 2

  **P — Score 1 requires:** Specific action (request policy reform, report labor conditions, justify changes) + explanation.
  **POV — Score 1 requires:** European factory manager identity + explanation of how that shapes observations (e.g., notices inefficiency and labor supply issues from a managerial perspective, not from the perspective of the exploited).

- **Model Answer (H5P Reveal):**

  **H:** "The historical situation was the Dutch Cultivation System in Java, which forced Javanese peasants to dedicate land and labor to growing export crops for Dutch profit. This matters because Edwards's report on labor conditions reflects the tensions inherent in a system that depended on forced labor to generate colonial revenue."

  **I:** "The intended audience was the Dutch colonial government in Jakarta, which controlled labor policy. This matters because Edwards frames his report in terms the government cares about — productivity, labor supply, and economic efficiency — rather than in terms of human suffering or justice."

  **P:** "The purpose was to report on labor conditions and request changes to labor policy that would improve factory productivity. This matters because Edwards was not documenting exploitation for moral reasons — he was identifying obstacles to production, which explains why the letter focuses on practical problems rather than human costs."

  **POV:** "As a European factory manager, Edwards's perspective was shaped by his role in the colonial economic system. This explains why he describes forced labor in terms of productivity and labor supply rather than in terms of human suffering — he sees Javanese peasants as a workforce to be managed, not as people being exploited. A Javanese peasant would describe the same conditions as oppression; Edwards describes them as inefficiency."

**Prompt:**
"Using Document 1 from this module (the Qing court memorandum on railroads), write a short answer (4-6 sentences) that:

1. Performs HIPP analysis on the document (at least 2 elements)
2. Connects your HIPP analysis to a historical argument about how the Qing Dynasty responded to Western influence

- **Autograder Rubric:**
  - **Score 0:** Student summarizes the document without HIPP analysis, OR does HIPP analysis that is disconnected from any historical argument.
  - **Score 1:** Student correctly applies at least 1 HIPP element AND attempts to connect it to an argument about Qing responses to Western influence.

- **Model Answer (H5P Reveal):**
> "The Qing court memorandum arguing that China should build railroads and telegraphs but prevent foreigners from constructing them reflects the self-strengthening movement's core tension: modernize, but on Chinese terms. The document's purpose was to persuade the emperor to adopt Western technology while maintaining Chinese sovereignty (Purpose), and the author's perspective as a Chinese official suspicious of foreign intentions explains why the memorandum frames every aspect of railroad construction as a sovereignty issue rather than an economic opportunity (POV). This document illustrates how Qing officials tried to selectively adopt Western technology without ceding political control — an approach that ultimately failed to prevent foreign encroachment because it could not address the structural economic and military advantages that Western powers held."

- **Feedback if Score 0:** "Your HIPP analysis needs to connect to a historical argument. Do not just analyze the document — use your analysis to say something about how the Qing Dynasty responded to Western influence."
