### ITEM 2: Article — "What is Intended Audience?"

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "What is Intended Audience?"
- **Source:** GENERATE
- **Scrape Instructions:** None.
- **Content:**

**Who Was This Written FOR?**

Intended Audience is the simplest HIPP element to define: **Who was this document created for?**

But here is what makes it powerful: knowing the audience tells you something about the document that the document itself cannot tell you. It reveals what the author thought they needed to say — and what they thought they could leave unsaid — based on who would be reading, viewing, or hearing it.

Think about how differently you write a text to your best friend versus an email to your teacher. Same you. Same topic. Completely different language, tone, and level of detail. That is the power of audience.

A petition to the British colonial government uses formal, deferential language — because the petitioners need the government to listen. A propaganda poster for public display uses bold images and simple slogans — because it needs to reach illiterate or semi-literate audiences. A diary entry is raw and unfiltered — because the audience is only the author.

**The Contrast That Makes Audience Click**

Here is the thought experiment that makes Intended Audience intuitive:

Imagine the **same author** writing about the **same topic** for **three different audiences:**

1. **A personal letter to a friend:** "The factory conditions are killing us. I cannot take much more of this. I cried yesterday."
2. **A political speech at a rally:** "Workers of this nation, we will not be broken! Our suffering is the fuel of a revolution that will transform this country!"
3. **A government report filed to superiors:** "Factory mortality rates have increased 12% year over year. Recommended intervention: enhanced ventilation systems and reduced shift duration."

Same person. Same conditions. Three completely different documents. The audience dictated the tone, the language, the evidence, and the emotional register.

That is what Intended Audience analysis is about: understanding how the recipient shapes the message.

**Why This Matters on the Exam**

Like all HIPP elements, Intended Audience earns the sourcing point through a two-step process:

1. **Identify** who the document was written for (Step 1)
2. **Explain** why knowing the audience matters to how we read the document (Step 2)

Naming the audience is not enough. You must explain how knowing the audience changes your interpretation.

### ITEM 3: Article — "The Golden Rule for Audience"

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "The Golden Rule: 'The intended audience was X, and that matters because...'"
- **Source:** GENERATE
- **Scrape Instructions:** None.
- **Content:**

**Be Specific About Who**

The golden rule for Audience is: **name a specific group, and then explain why that audience shapes the document.**

**Bad example (0 points):**
> "The intended audience was everyone."

This is meaningless. No document is written for "everyone." Even a royal edict posted in the town square is addressed to the subjects of that kingdom, not to people on the other side of the world. Be specific.

**Better but still 0 points:**
> "The intended audience was the public."

Which public? The Soviet public? The British public? The Mexican peasant class? "The public" is only slightly better than "everyone." You need to specify which public, in which context.

**Good example (earns the point):**
> "The intended audience was the British Parliament, which had the power to approve military funding. This matters because the author framed the report as an urgent security crisis rather than a routine administrative update — the language of danger and urgency was calibrated to persuade parliamentarians to authorize reinforcements, not to provide a balanced assessment of the situation."

See the structure? Specific audience (British Parliament) + why that audience matters (the report's urgency was calibrated to persuade them to act).

**The Key Insight: Audience Shapes the Message**

When you name the audience, you unlock analytical insights:
- Writing to **superiors** = deferential tone, formal language, emphasis on what the superior needs to hear
- Writing to **the public** = emotional appeals, simplified arguments, call to action
- Writing to **fellow believers** = insider language, assumed shared values, no need to justify basic premises
- Writing to **opponents** = defensive framing, preemptive rebuttals, careful evidence selection
- Writing to **posterity** = legacy construction, selective memory, heroic narrative

**Three attempts at Intended Audience, ranked from worst to best:**

**ATTEMPT 1 (Bad — 0 points):**

**ATTEMPT 2 (Better, but still 0 points):**

The deferential framing ('I urge you to return to work') masks what is essentially a threat: comply, or face consequences. Knowing the audience helps us understand that the letter's measured tone was a strategic choice — the minister was performing reasonableness for the public record while making clear that the government would not tolerate labor disruption."

*Why it earns the point:* Names a specific audience (striking factory workers). Explains why that audience shapes the document (the power dynamic between minister and workers explains the letter's controlled tone and implicit threats). Connects audience to the document's language and framing.

### ITEM 7: Article — Common Mistakes

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "4 Common Mistakes with Intended Audience (and How to Avoid Them)"
- **Source:** GENERATE + external scraping
- **Scrape Instructions:**

  **Source 1 — Fiveable HIPP Submissions:**

  **Source 2 — 2024 Chief Reader Report:**
  1. Navigate to: https://apcentral.collegeboard.org/media/pdf/ap24-cr-report-world-history-modern-set-1.pdf
  2. Find commentary on common Audience errors.
  3. Extract relevant commentary.

- **Content:**

**Mistake 1: Saying "everyone" or "the public."**
No document is written for "everyone." Even a propaganda poster displayed in public is targeted at a specific population — Soviet citizens, German workers, Chinese peasants. Name the specific group. If the source line does not tell you explicitly, use clues: What language was it written in? Where was it published? What kind of literacy or knowledge does it assume?

**Mistake 2: Naming the audience but not explaining why it matters.**
"The intended audience was the king." Okay — but so what? How does writing to the king shape what the author says? Does the author use deferential language? Do they emphasize threats to justify requests? Do they avoid criticizing the king's policies? The audience identification is Step 1. The explanation is Step 2. Both are required.

**Mistake 3: Confusing the audience with the author.**
"The intended audience was the finance minister." If the finance minister WROTE the letter, they are the author, not the audience. The audience is who RECEIVES the document. Read the source line carefully: who wrote it? Who is it addressed to?

**Mistake 4: Ignoring the audience-message connection.**
Even when students correctly identify the audience, they often fail to explain how knowing the audience changes their interpretation. The analysis should answer: "If this had been written for a DIFFERENT audience, how would it be different?" If you can answer that, you understand the audience-message connection.

- **(C)** "This is Point of View, not Intended Audience. It describes the author's perspective, not who the document was written for. 0 points."
- **(D)** "This is a reasonable guess but not supported by the source line. Hagiographies (saint biographies) were written for religious communities as moral exemplars, not as family memorials. 0 points."
