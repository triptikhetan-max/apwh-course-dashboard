### ITEM 2: Article — "What is Point of View?"

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "What is Point of View?"
- **Source:** GENERATE
- **Scrape Instructions:** None.
- **Content:**

**The Hardest Element — and the Most Powerful**

Point of View is the HIPP element that separates good students from great ones. It is also the one that trips up the most people. Here is why:

Students constantly confuse POV with two other things:
1. **Summary:** "The author believes that factory conditions are terrible." (This is what they think, not why they think it.)
2. **Bias label:** "The author is biased." (This is a label, not analysis.)

Real POV analysis asks a deeper question: **Why does the author say what they say IN THE WAY they say it?**

That phrase — "in the way" — is everything. It shifts the focus from WHAT the author thinks to HOW their identity shapes the specific way they express it.

**The Core Idea: Identity Shapes Expression**

Every author brings an identity to a document — a collection of roles, experiences, beliefs, and social positions that shape how they see the world and what they choose to write.

- A **colonial governor** sees a railroad and thinks "infrastructure that strengthens our control."
- A **colonized farmer** sees the same railroad and thinks "the thing that took my land."
- A **British politician** sees the same railroad and thinks "strategic advantage against Russia."
- A **local merchant** sees the same railroad and thinks "faster access to markets — or faster access for my competitors."

Same railroad. Four completely different documents. The difference is Point of View.

POV analysis asks: **What about the author's identity, role, or position explains why they wrote THIS specific document in THIS specific way?**

**The "As a..." Test**

Here is a quick test to see if you are doing POV or just summary:

If you can start your sentence with "As a [role/identity], the author..." and it makes sense, you are doing POV.

- "As a **colonial governor**, the author emphasizes the railroad's benefits to imperial control because..." ✅ POV
- "As a **Muslim scholar** who witnessed Portuguese violence, the author emphasizes religious persecution because..." ✅ POV
- "The author believes factory conditions are bad." ❌ Summary — no identity, no "as a" framing

### ITEM 3: Article — "The Golden Rule for POV"

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "The Golden Rule: 'As a [role/identity], the author's perspective was shaped by...'"
- **Source:** GENERATE
- **Scrape Instructions:** None.
- **Content:**

**The Two-Step Pattern (One More Time)**

By now you know the pattern: identify, then explain. For POV:

1. **Identify** the author's relevant identity (role, social class, religion, gender, nationality, profession, lived experience)
2. **Explain** why that identity shapes what they wrote and HOW they wrote it

**Bad example (0 points):**
> "The author is biased."

This is a label, not analysis. "Biased" says nothing about WHO the author is, WHY they see things a certain way, or HOW their identity shapes the document. It is the single most common zero-point POV attempt on the exam.

**Better but still 0 points:**
> "The author is biased because he is a British colonial official."

Better — you named an identity. But you have not explained HOW being a British colonial official shapes this specific document. What does a colonial official care about? What do they assume? What do they ignore?

**Good example (earns the point):**
> "As a British colonial official responsible for economic development in West Africa, the author's perspective was shaped by his professional obligation to justify colonial expenditure to his superiors in London. This explains why the document emphasizes the economic benefits of railroad construction (increased trade, French revenue) while saying nothing about the forced labor, land seizure, or cultural disruption experienced by African communities — those realities were irrelevant to the audience and the author's professional worldview."

See what happened? The student named a specific identity (British colonial official responsible for economic development), connected it to professional incentives (justify expenditure to London), and explained how that shaped the document (emphasized benefits, ignored costs to Africans).

**Common but Deadly: "The author is biased"**

Let us be very clear about this. The word "biased" on its own earns zero points. Every single time. The rubric requires you to explain HOW or WHY — not just label. If you write "biased," you must immediately follow it with:
- Biased **HOW**? What specific perspective do they have?
- Biased **WHY**? What about their identity creates that perspective?
- Biased **SO WHAT**? How does that bias shape the specific content of the document?

If you cannot answer all three, do not use the word "biased." Use the "As a [role]..." frame instead.

**Frame 1 (Identity-shapes-expression):**
> "As a [role/identity], the author's perspective was shaped by [specific experience, obligation, or worldview], which explains why they [specific thing in the document: emphasize X, ignore Y, use certain language, take a particular tone]."

**Frame 2 (Motivation-explanation):**
> "The author, as a [role/identity], had [specific reason to see things this way], and that matters because [how it shapes the document — what they include, exclude, or frame in a particular way]."

**Frame 3 (Contrast):**
> "If this document had been written by a [different identity/role], it would likely [be different in specific ways], which shows that the author's [specific identity] shaped [specific aspect of the document]."

### ITEM 7: Article — Common Mistakes

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "4 Common Mistakes with Point of View (and How to Avoid Them)"

  2. Find commentary on common POV errors.
  3. Extract relevant commentary.

- **Content:**

**Mistake 1: Saying "the author is biased" without explaining HOW or WHY.**
The word "biased" on its own is worth zero points. Every single time. You must explain: biased HOW? Biased because of what identity or experience? And how does that bias shape THIS specific document? If you cannot answer those questions, use the "As a [role]..." frame instead.

**Mistake 2: Confusing POV with Purpose.**
Purpose = what the author was trying to DO. POV = how the author's identity shaped what they WROTE. A colonial governor might have the PURPOSE of requesting funding (that is what they are trying to accomplish) but a POV shaped by their belief in European superiority (that is how their identity shapes the document). Related, but not the same.

**Mistake 3: Confusing POV with summary.**
"The author believes that workers are exploited." This is what the author thinks — it is summary of their position. POV asks WHY they think that way. "As an indigenous anarchist intellectual, the author sees labor exploitation through the lens of land theft and colonial dispossession — which explains why he frames property rights as fundamentally illegitimate, not just in need of reform." Now you are doing POV.

**Mistake 4: Not connecting identity to the specific document.**
"As a woman, the author had a female perspective." This is a tautology — it says nothing analytical. You need to connect the author's specific identity to specific content in the document. What does the author include, exclude, emphasize, or frame in a particular way BECAUSE of their identity?

1. **POV is the hardest element** — students need more scaffolding. That is why this module has 3 guided docs instead of 2.

3. **The paired documents (2025 S2 Docs 6+7)** are a powerful teaching tool for POV. Use them in the worked example to show how the same topic (women and industrialization in South Africa) looks completely different depending on the author's racial identity.
