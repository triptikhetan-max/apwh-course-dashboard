### ITEM 3: Article — "What is HIPP and Why Should You Care?"

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "What is HIPP and Why Should You Care?"
- **Source:** GENERATE
- **Scrape Instructions:** None.
- **Content:**

**What HIPP Stands For**

HIPP is a framework for analyzing primary source documents on the AP World History exam. Each letter represents one way of "sourcing" a document — reading it not just for what it says, but for who wrote it, when, why, and for whom.

- **H — Historical Situation:** What was happening in the world when and where this document was created?
- **I — Intended Audience:** Who was this document written for?
- **P — Purpose:** Why did the author create this document? What were they trying to accomplish?
- **P — Point of View (POV):** How does the author's identity, role, or position shape what they wrote?

You just watched Heimler use the HAPPY framework. Same idea, different acronym. His "A" is our "I" (Intended Audience), and his "Y" (why it matters) is something we build into every HIPP element — the explanation step.

**Why HIPP Matters: The Point Math**

The DBQ is scored on a 7-point rubric. HIPP directly earns you points in two places:

1. **The Sourcing Point (1 point):** You earn this by doing HIPP analysis on at least 2 documents in your essay. You explain how or why a document's Historical Situation, Intended Audience, Purpose, or Point of View is relevant to your argument.

2. **The Complexity Point (1 point):** Since the 2023 rubric revision, one pathway to the complexity point is doing HIPP analysis on 4 or more documents. So strong sourcing does not just earn 1 point — it can unlock 2.

That means HIPP can be worth 2 out of 7 points on the DBQ. Almost 30% of your score. And here is the thing: these are the most learnable points on the exam. The sourcing point and the complexity point are skills you can practice and master. Unlike thesis writing, which depends on your historical knowledge, HIPP is a transferable method that works on any document from any time period.

**HIPP Also Helps on Multiple Choice**

Of the 55 MCQs on the AP World exam, 39 are stimulus-based — they give you a source and ask you to interpret it. Knowing how to read a source line, identify who wrote it, and think about why they wrote it is not just a DBQ skill. It is an everything skill.

**What This Course Will Do**

Over the next 9 modules, you will learn each HIPP element one at a time, practice on real documents from released AP exams, and build up to writing full DBQ essays with sourcing woven in. By the end, HIPP will be automatic — something you do without thinking every time you encounter a document.

- **Content Type:** article
- **Title:** "Worked Example 1: Full HIPP Analysis — Teacher Models the Thinking"
- **Source:** 2022 FRQ PDF, Document 4 (Lobengula concession agreement, 1888)
- **Scrape Instructions:**

  2. Navigate to the DBQ section. Find **Document 4**.
  3. Extract the **full source line** verbatim. Per the catalog: *"Ndebele ruler Lobengula Khumalo with Charles Rudd, Rochfort Maguire, and Francis Thompson, concession agreement, 1888"*
  4. Extract the **full document text** below the source line.
  5. Preserve all original formatting.
- **Content:**

**Document displayed to student:**
Show the complete Document 4 from the 2022 DBQ (source line + full text) in a styled document card.

**Teacher HIPP Analysis (all 4 elements modeled):**

*"Here is how I analyze this document using all four HIPP elements. In later modules, you will learn each one in depth. For now, just see how the framework works as a whole."*

**H — Historical Situation:**
> "This agreement was made during the Scramble for Africa, when European powers and private companies were using treaties and concession agreements to claim African resources and territory. By 1888, Cecil Rhodes's British South Africa Company was aggressively seeking mining rights across southern Africa. This matters because the concession format — offering rifles and money in exchange for exclusive mining rights — was a standard tool of imperial expansion, designed to create a legal veneer for resource extraction."

**I — Intended Audience:**
> "The intended audience was the British government and European investors, who needed a signed document from an African ruler to legitimize their claims under international law. This matters because the agreement was written in English and structured in European legal terms that Lobengula may not have fully understood — the document was designed to be legible and enforceable in London, not in the Ndebele capital."

**P — Purpose:**
> "The purpose of this document was to secure exclusive mining rights for Rhodes's associates while appearing to offer a fair exchange. The rifles, monthly payment, and steamboat were meant to make the agreement look like a negotiation between equals. This matters because the 'exchange' was wildly unequal — the mining rights were worth millions, while the compensation was negligible — and the purpose was to create a legal basis for resource extraction, not genuine partnership."

**POV — Point of View:**
> "This document represents the interests of European imperialists seeking to extract resources from southern Africa. As agents of Cecil Rhodes, the European signatories viewed African territory as a resource to be acquired through whatever means necessary. This matters because Lobengula later repudiated the agreement, claiming he had been deceived about its terms — which suggests the document reflects the perspective and goals of the European parties, not a genuine consensus."

*"Notice: each element starts with identification and ends with explanation ('this matters because...'). That two-step pattern is what earns points. You will practice it extensively starting in Module 2."*

- **Content:**

**Document displayed to student:**
Show the complete Document 1 from the 2023 Set 2 DBQ (source line + full text) in a styled document card.

**Teacher HIPP Analysis (all 4 elements modeled):**

*"Let's see how HIPP works on a completely different type of document — an essay by a woman writer published in a conservative newspaper."*

**H — Historical Situation:**
> "This essay was published in 1889, during a period of Egyptian modernization under British influence, when reformers debated how to strengthen Egyptian society. Education reform — particularly whether women should be educated — was a major point of contention. This matters because the call for girls' education was part of a broader modernization movement, and the author was entering a live political debate about the future of Egyptian society."

**I — Intended Audience:**
> "The intended audience was the readership of Al-Adab, described as a conservative Egyptian newspaper. This matters because al-Taymuriyya was making a progressive argument (educate girls) to a conservative audience — she had to frame her argument in terms her readers would accept, which likely meant grounding it in Islamic tradition and family values rather than Western liberal ideas."

**P — Purpose:**
> "The purpose was to persuade conservative Egyptians that educating girls was essential for family and social reform. This matters because the title itself — 'Family Reform Comes Only Through the Education of Girls' — frames education not as a feminist demand but as a practical necessity for family stability, which was a strategic rhetorical choice designed to appeal to her conservative audience."

**POV — Point of View:**
> "As an elite Egyptian woman poet and novelist, al-Taymuriyya had personal experience with the benefits of education and could speak from lived authority on the subject. This matters because her status as an educated woman gave her credibility — she was proof that women's education produced accomplished individuals, not social chaos — which strengthened her argument."

*"This document is very different from the Lobengula concession — different time, place, type, and purpose. But the HIPP framework works the same way. That is its power: it is a universal tool."*

### ITEM 7: MCQ 1 — "Which HIPP Element Is Being Used?"

- **Content Type:** mcq (4 choices)
- **Title:** "Which HIPP Element Is Being Used?"
- **Source:** GENERATE
- **Scrape Instructions:** None.
- **Content:**

**Stimulus displayed to student:**
> A student writes: "This petition was written during the Scramble for Africa, when European powers were imposing colonial control over African societies through new taxes and administrative systems. This matters because the tax represented British authority over local governance."

**Question:** Which HIPP element is this student using?

**(A)** Historical Situation
**(B)** Intended Audience
**(C)** Purpose
**(D)** Point of View

**Correct Answer:** A

**Feedback for each option:**
- **(A)** "Correct! The student is describing what was happening in the world when and where the document was created (the Scramble for Africa, colonial taxation) and explaining why it matters. This is Historical Situation."
- **(B)** "Not quite. Intended Audience would focus on WHO the document was written for — the recipient, not the historical context. This student is analyzing what was happening around the document."
- **(C)** "Not quite. Purpose would focus on WHY the author created the document — what they were trying to accomplish. This student is analyzing the historical backdrop, not the author's goals."
- **(D)** "Not quite. Point of View would focus on HOW the author's identity or position shaped what they wrote. This student is analyzing the historical context, not the author's perspective."
