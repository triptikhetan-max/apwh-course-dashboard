# Module 2: Historical Situation — Student Dry Run Review

**Persona:** Emma Cotner, 10th grader at Alpha Austin. CED Skill 2 (Sourcing & Situation) score: 0%. Has never analyzed a primary source document. On her last DBQ, referenced "Confucian ideals" when writing about the Soviet Union.

**Reviewer approach:** Every section evaluated as Emma would experience it on a first read-through, with no prior HIPP instruction.

## Section-by-Section Evaluation

---

### 1. "Before You Read a Single Word" — Would Emma Understand This?

**Accessibility: Strong (9/10)**

This opening works well for Emma. The language is direct and conversational. The hook — "you can earn points before you even read the document itself" — is the kind of strategic, grade-oriented framing that gets a struggling student to pay attention. Emma does not care about historiography; she cares about not failing. This section speaks her language.

The movie analogy ("the opening shot of a movie") is effective. Emma watches movies. She understands that a camera panning across a battlefield tells you something before anyone speaks. That is an intuitive bridge to the concept.

The Heimler reference is also smart — many students already watch Heimler on YouTube. If Emma has seen even one of his videos, the connection lands. If she has not, the article gives enough context that it still makes sense.

**Potential confusion for Emma:**
- The phrase "source line" is introduced but not visually demonstrated until Worked Example 1. For Emma, who has literally never analyzed a primary source, the gap between "a little italicized blurb above the text" and actually seeing one is a problem. She might nod along here but not fully grasp what a source line looks like until page 3. A quick visual example right here — even a fake one — would close that gap.
- "Historians do the opposite" — Emma might not register this as an instruction for her. She is not a historian. The article could make the pivot more explicit: "On this exam, YOU need to do the opposite too."

**Does it teach the concept?** Partially. After reading this section, Emma could probably say "Historical Situation is about what was happening when the document was written." That is the right idea but still fuzzy. She would not yet know what to DO with that understanding. The section sets up the concept without operationalizing it, which is appropriate — the worked examples come later. But Emma needs the full module to actually learn the skill; this section alone would leave her vaguely aware but unable to execute.

### 2. "Why This Matters on the Exam (The Point Math)" — Would Emma Understand This?

**Accessibility: Good (7/10)**

The strategic framing is excellent for Emma. Telling her that Historical Situation "is the one students learn fastest and score most reliably on" is motivating. She is at 0% — she needs to believe improvement is possible. This section gives her that.

**Potential confusion for Emma:**
- "7-point rubric," "sourcing point," "complexity point" — these terms are thrown out quickly. Emma may not know what a rubric is in the AP context. The article assumes she has already seen the DBQ rubric breakdown, which a 0% student likely has not internalized. A parenthetical like "(we will break down the full rubric in Module X)" or a one-sentence definition would help.
- "39 are stimulus-based" — this is useful information but the term "stimulus-based" is AP-teacher jargon. Emma would benefit from: "39 of them give you a document, image, or chart and ask you questions about it."
- The complexity point pathway (HIPP on 4+ documents) is mentioned but not explained. Emma does not need to worry about the complexity point yet. For a 0% student, this paragraph risks overwhelming her with information she cannot act on. It is aspirational but premature.

**Does it teach the concept?** No, and it is not meant to. This section is motivational strategy. It works for that purpose.

### 3. "The Golden Rule: '...and that matters because'" — Would Emma Understand This?

**Accessibility: Excellent (10/10)**

This is the strongest section of the entire module for Emma. Here is why:

The side-by-side comparison of the zero-point response vs. the one-point response is crystal clear. Emma can SEE the difference. The zero-point example ("This was written during the Industrial Revolution") looks like something Emma would write. The one-point example adds the clause and suddenly it is a real analysis. The contrast is visceral.

"The difference between 0 points and 1 point is six words" — this is the kind of concrete, actionable insight that a struggling student can actually hold onto. Emma will remember this.

"Tape those words to your laptop. Tattoo them on your brain." — the tone is direct without being condescending. It feels like a coach talking, not a textbook lecturing. This is exactly right for Emma.

**Potential confusion for Emma:** None. This section is airtight.

**Does it teach the concept?** Yes. After reading this section, Emma could articulate: "I have to say what was happening AND explain why it matters to the document." She has the rule. She does not yet have the skill to execute it, but she has the rule. That is exactly what this section should accomplish.

### 4. Sentence Frames — Would Emma Understand These?

**Accessibility: Good (8/10)**

The three frames give Emma different entry points. Frame 1 is the most explicit. Frame 3 ("Given that...") is the most sophisticated. Emma would probably gravitate toward Frame 1 because it has the literal words "and that matters because" built in.

The instruction "Pick whichever frame feels most natural" is good. The follow-up — "The non-negotiable part is the second half" — reinforces the Golden Rule.

**Potential confusion for Emma:**
- "Training wheels" in the heading is a smart metaphor but could feel slightly patronizing to a 10th grader. Minor issue.
- Emma might not know how to fill in the brackets. The frames say "[event/trend]" and "[connection to what the document says, argues, or shows]" — but Emma does not yet know how to identify an event/trend from a source line, or how to articulate a connection. The frames give structure but assume knowledge she does not have yet. The worked examples that follow are essential.

**Does it teach the concept?** The frames are tools, not teaching. They are useful AFTER Emma understands the concept from the Golden Rule section and sees it modeled in the worked examples. On their own, they would not be enough.

### 5. Worked Example 1 (Teacher Models the Thinking) — Is It Clear?

**Accessibility: Very Good (8/10)**

The step-by-step thinking process is the most valuable part of this example. Emma gets to see what goes on inside a teacher's head when they encounter a source line. This is metacognitive modeling — showing the process, not just the product — and it is exactly what a 0% student needs.

Step 1 (pull out key details) gives Emma a concrete first action. She can do this. Look at the source line. Write down the date, place, author, document type. That is manageable.

Step 2 (ask what was happening) is where Emma might struggle. The example provides the historical knowledge — "1866 is the middle of the British Raj" — but Emma may not have that knowledge for other documents. The article does not address what to do when you do not KNOW the historical context. This is a significant gap. For Emma, who referenced Confucian ideals when writing about the Soviet Union, the issue is not just skill — it is content knowledge. The article teaches the analytical framework but assumes the student can supply the historical facts.

Step 3 (write it using the frame) produces a strong model answer. The explicit callout at the end — "Notice what I did NOT do" — is very effective. It anticipates Emma's likely mistakes (summarizing the document, quoting it) and preemptively corrects them.

**Could Emma replicate this on a new document?** Partially. She could replicate Steps 1 and 3 (extracting details from the source line and using the sentence frame). She would struggle with Step 2 because it requires historical knowledge she may not have. The article provides the knowledge for this example but does not teach Emma how to access it for unfamiliar documents. A brief note like "If you do not remember the historical context, use clues from the source line — the date, the region, the type of document — to narrow down what was happening" would help.

### 6. Worked Example 2 (Good vs. Bad Spectrum) — Does the Comparison Work?

**Accessibility: Excellent (9/10)**

This is extremely effective for Emma. Three attempts, worst to best, with explicit explanations of why each one fails or succeeds. Emma can map her own likely response onto the spectrum and see where she falls.

**Attempt 1 analysis:** "This is like saying a movie takes place 'on Earth.' Technically true, completely useless." — Emma would laugh at this. It is memorable and clarifying. She will not make this mistake.

**Attempt 2 analysis:** The distinction between "naming a period" and "explaining why it matters" is clearly drawn. The "Okay, and...?" reaction is something Emma can internalize as a self-check.

**Attempt 3 analysis:** The model answer is strong. It even pulls in the detail about the memoir never being published — showing Emma that the source line contains MORE information than she might initially notice.

**Potential confusion for Emma:**
- The content of Attempt 3 requires knowing what de-Stalinization was, who Khrushchev was, and what Stalin's cult of personality involved. Emma — who put Confucian ideals in a Soviet Union essay — may not have this knowledge. She can appreciate the STRUCTURE of the answer without understanding the CONTENT. That is still valuable, but it means the example teaches form more than substance.
- The leap in quality from Attempt 2 to Attempt 3 is large. A "Decent — still 0 points but almost there" example between them might help Emma see the incremental path to improvement. Right now, the jump from "de-Stalinization" (0 points) to the full paragraph (1 point) might feel like a canyon she cannot cross.

**Would Emma know the difference between 0-point and 1-point?** Yes. This section makes it unmistakable. The repeated pattern — identification alone = 0, identification + explanation = 1 — is driven home through multiple examples. Emma gets it.

### 8. Practice Documents — Emma's Attempts

#### Practice Doc 1 (Guided) — Chanakya, Arthashastra, c. 250 BCE

**Emma's attempt using the sentence frame:**

> "During this time period, the Mauryan Empire was like a really big empire in India that had a lot of land and people, and that matters because Chanakya was trying to help the emperor run everything and this book was like his advice on how to do that."

**Assessment of the scaffolding:** The sentence frame works — it forces Emma to produce a two-part answer. The hints are helpful ("What was the Mauryan Empire? Where was it? What was Chanakya's role?"). However, Emma's AP World knowledge may not include enough about the Mauryan Empire to produce a historically specific answer. The hints ask her to recall information she may never have learned or retained.

**What the article prepared her for:** Emma used the frame. She included the "and that matters because" clause. She attempted a connection to the document. Structurally, she is doing the right thing.

**What is still confusing:** Emma's answer is vague ("a really big empire") because she lacks the content knowledge. She cannot name specific developments (Mauryan expansion, Chanakya's role in political consolidation, challenges of governing a diverse subcontinent). The module teaches the analytical skill but cannot compensate for missing historical knowledge.

**Verdict:** The scaffolding is appropriate. Emma's answer would likely earn 0 points because it is too vague, but she is now producing the right STRUCTURE. That is progress from 0%.

#### Practice Doc 3 (Semi-Guided) — Jose Guadalupe Posada, Mexican printmaker, 1892

**Emma's attempt using only the prompt:**

> "In Mexico in 1892, there was probably a dictator or something because the document talks about government suppression of protests. And that matters because the printmaker was showing people what the government was doing to protesters, which was probably bad."

**Assessment of the scaffolding:** The prompt ("What was happening in Mexico in 1892?") points Emma in the right direction. The hint about "who was in power, how they maintained power" is useful. But Emma does not know about Porfirio Diaz or the Porfiriato. Her answer reveals that she is REASONING from the document content ("there was probably a dictator... because the document talks about government suppression") rather than bringing in outside knowledge.

**What the article prepared her for:** Emma remembered the "and that matters because" clause without being given the sentence frame. She is attempting to connect context to the document. She is NOT just describing the document content — she is trying to identify what was happening around it. These are all wins.

**What is still confusing:** Emma is inferring historical context from the document rather than recalling it independently. This is a common pattern for students with weak content knowledge. The module does not address what to do when you genuinely do not know the historical context — it assumes the student can supply it. For a 0% student, this is a significant gap.

**Verdict:** Emma's structural skills are improving. Her analysis is logically sound (she is making reasonable inferences) but historically thin. A real AP grader might give partial credit for the reasoning, but it would likely not earn the point because "there was probably a dictator or something" is not a specific historical development.

#### Practice Doc 5 (Independent) — Shenyang Daily article, 1957, "Hundred Flowers" speech

**Emma's attempt with no help:**

> "This article was published in China in 1957 when Mao told people they could criticize the Party. And that matters because since Mao said people could speak out, the newspaper was reporting on a meeting where people actually did that."

**Assessment:** This is noticeably better than Doc 1. Emma identified a specific historical development (Mao's Hundred Flowers speech encouraging criticism). She explained why it matters (the speech created the conditions for the meeting the article describes). She used the "and that matters because" clause naturally.

**Important caveat:** The source line for Doc 5 essentially GIVES Emma the historical context — it explicitly mentions "Mao Zedong's 'Let a Hundred Flowers Bloom' speech encouraging citizens to speak out against the Party." Emma did not need to recall this from memory; the source line handed it to her. This is worth noting because on the actual AP exam, source lines do sometimes contain this level of detail, but not always. Emma's success here is partly a function of how much context the source line provided, not just her growing skill.

**Verdict:** Emma's answer would likely earn the sourcing point. It identifies a specific development, explains its relevance, and connects context to the document. However, a stronger answer would note the irony or danger — that the Hundred Flowers campaign was followed by the Anti-Rightist Campaign, and that "speaking out" carried real risk. Emma does not have that knowledge.

**Overall practice progression assessment:** The scaffolding decrease from Doc 1 to Doc 5 is well-designed. Emma's structural improvement across the five documents is visible. Her main limitation is content knowledge, not analytical skill — which means the module is doing its job of teaching the SKILL even if it cannot teach all the CONTENT.

### 10. Overall Assessment

---

#### Would Emma's Skill 2 improve from 0%?

**Yes, meaningfully.** Before this module, Emma did not know what a source line was, did not know the HIPP framework existed, and could not distinguish between describing a document and analyzing its context. After this module, she can:

1. Identify the source line and extract key details (date, author, place, document type)
2. Understand that Historical Situation asks "what was happening around the document"
3. Use the "and that matters because" clause to bridge identification and explanation
4. Recognize the difference between 0-point and 1-point responses
5. Use sentence frames to structure her analysis
6. Distinguish Historical Situation from Contextualization (at least conceptually)

**Estimated post-module score: 40-55%.** Emma would now earn the sourcing point on some documents — particularly those with information-rich source lines that help her identify the historical context. She would still miss it on documents that require content knowledge she does not have.

---

#### What would Emma still struggle with?

1. **Content knowledge gaps.** The module teaches the analytical framework brilliantly but cannot teach all of AP World History content. Emma needs to KNOW what was happening in Mexico in 1892, in China in 1957, in India in 1866. If she does not have that knowledge, she cannot perform Step 2 (identify the historical context) no matter how well she understands the framework. This is the biggest remaining barrier.

2. **Generating context independently.** When the source line does not hand her the context (as Doc 5's source line did), Emma must recall it from memory. The module does not teach her strategies for accessing content knowledge she has learned but cannot readily retrieve.

3. **Specificity.** Emma's natural tendency is toward vague language ("a really big empire," "probably a dictator or something"). The module shows her what specificity looks like but does not drill her on HOW to be specific when she is unsure of the details.

4. **Integrating H into a full DBQ paragraph.** The module teaches Historical Situation as a standalone skill. Emma does not yet know how to weave an H analysis into a body paragraph that also includes evidence and argument. That presumably comes in a later module, but the gap is worth noting.

5. **Time pressure.** The module gives Emma unlimited time to think through each document. On the exam, she has roughly 60 minutes for the entire DBQ. She will need to perform this analysis quickly, on multiple documents, while also writing thesis statements, body paragraphs, and contextualization.

---

#### What is missing that would help Emma?

1. **A "what to do when you don't know the history" section.** This is the single most important addition. Emma will encounter documents from periods she barely remembers. The module should teach her to mine the source line for clues (the date narrows the era, the region narrows the developments, the document type suggests the context) and to make educated inferences even when she cannot recall the exact historical details. Something like: "Even if you do not remember the specific events, the source line gives you clues. A petition from 1896 Sierra Leone? You know European colonialism was happening in Africa in the 1890s. Start there."

2. **A brief content review table.** A one-page reference table mapping key AP World time periods to major developments (e.g., 1750-1900: industrialization, imperialism, nationalism; 1900-present: world wars, decolonization, Cold War) would give Emma a lifeline when her content knowledge fails. This does not need to be comprehensive — just enough to jog her memory.

3. **A "check your work" checklist.** After writing an H analysis, Emma should be able to run through: (a) Did I identify a specific historical development? (b) Did I include "and that matters because"? (c) Does my explanation connect to THIS document specifically? (d) Am I describing context, not content? Four yes answers = likely earns the point.

4. **One more intermediate example in the Good-vs-Bad spectrum.** The jump from Attempt 2 (0 points) to Attempt 3 (1 point) is steep. An Attempt 2.5 that has the right structure but weak specificity would show Emma the incremental path forward.

5. **A note about source lines that contain a lot vs. a little information.** Doc 5's source line practically writes the H analysis for you. Other source lines (like "Village elders in Caozhou, Shandong Province, northeastern China, report on local conditions, late 19th century") give much less. Emma should know that some source lines are generous and some are sparse, and that her strategy should adjust accordingly.

6. **Answer keys or model responses for the practice documents.** Emma completes Docs 1-5 but has no way to check her work. Without feedback, she may think her vague Doc 1 answer is acceptable. Model responses for each practice document — even brief ones — would let Emma self-assess and correct her understanding.

---

#### Rating: 7.5 / 10 for Teaching Effectiveness

**Strengths that justify a high score:**
- The "and that matters because" golden rule is the module's best contribution. It is memorable, actionable, and directly addresses the most common exam mistake. This alone would improve Emma's score.
- The bad-to-good spectrum is pedagogically excellent. Emma can see herself in the bad examples and aspire to the good ones.
- The scaffolded practice progression (guided to independent) is well-designed and appropriately paced.
- The tone is conversational without being dumbed down. It respects Emma's intelligence while meeting her where she is.
- The MCQs reinforce the core lesson effectively.

**Weaknesses that prevent a higher score:**
- No answer keys for practice documents. This is the single biggest gap. A student working independently has no way to know if she is on track.
- No strategy for handling content knowledge gaps — the module's biggest blind spot.
- The complexity point discussion is premature for a 0% student and adds cognitive load without immediate payoff.
- No self-check mechanism after practice (the reflection prompt at the end is good but general; a specific checklist would be more useful).
- The Fiveable scraping note (for Akshit) indicates planned content that is not yet present — those real student examples would strengthen the module significantly.

### 11. Flow Check — Learning vs. Textbook Feel

**Overall feel: Learning, not textbook.** The module reads like a knowledgeable coach talking directly to the student. The second-person address ("you"), the strategic framing ("let's talk about points"), the humor ("Tattoo them on your brain"), and the concrete examples all contribute to an engaged, conversational tone.

**Where engagement is strongest:**
- The "Golden Rule" section. The six-word insight is a dopamine hit. Emma feels like she just learned a cheat code.
- The Bad-to-Good spectrum. Seeing the contrast between attempts is inherently engaging because it activates judgment ("oh, I can see why that one is wrong").
- The opening hook ("you can earn points before you even read the document"). Emma's attention is captured immediately.

**Where engagement drops:**
- The "Point Math" section. The rubric discussion (7 points, sourcing point, complexity point, 39 stimulus-based MCQs) is dense and informational. Emma's eyes glaze over. She is processing numbers and terms she does not fully understand. This section could be shortened to: "Historical Situation is worth at least 1 point, possibly 2, and also helps you on multiple choice. Here is how."
- Practice Docs 2 and 4. By Doc 2, Emma has done one guided example and is being asked to do another at the same scaffolding level. The repetition is useful for skill-building but feels like homework. By Doc 4, she has had no feedback on Docs 1-3 and is starting to wonder if she is doing it right. The lack of model answers creates an anxiety gap that dampens engagement.
- The Key Terms section at the end. It reads like a glossary appendix. Emma will not read it. If these terms were introduced inline (which most of them are), the end-of-module list adds little value for a student like Emma. It might be useful as a study reference later, but in the flow of the module, it is a dead zone.

**Pacing:** The module is well-paced overall. The 25-30 minute estimate is realistic. The progression from concept introduction to modeling to guided practice to independent practice follows solid instructional design principles. The only pacing issue is the lack of feedback loops — Emma produces work but never gets to see a model response until the MCQs, which test a different skill (recognition vs. production).

## Summary of Specific Improvements Needed

1. **Add model responses for all 5 practice documents.** This is the highest-priority fix. Without answer keys, the practice section is incomplete.

2. **Add a "What to Do When You Don't Know the History" subsection** after Common Mistakes. Teach students to mine source lines for context clues and make educated inferences.

3. **Add a self-check checklist** (4 questions) that students can use after writing any H analysis.

4. **Show a visual example of a source line** in the opening section ("Before You Read a Single Word"), before Emma encounters one in Worked Example 1.

5. **Add an intermediate example (Attempt 2.5)** to the Bad-to-Good spectrum that shows the "right structure, weak specificity" pattern.

6. **Trim the "Point Math" section** to reduce cognitive load for struggling students. Move the complexity point discussion to a later module or a footnote.

7. **Add 1-2 real (anonymized) student examples** as noted in the Akshit build spec. These would powerfully reinforce the identification-vs-explanation distinction.

8. **Add a note about source line generosity** — some give you the context, some make you recall it. Teach Emma to recognize the difference.

9. **Replace "stimulus-based"** with plain language ("questions that give you a document and ask about it").

10. **Consider adding a brief content-review reference table** mapping time periods to major developments, either in this module or as a companion resource.
