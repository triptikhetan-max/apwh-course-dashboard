## ARTICLE CONTENT

### The Problem Most Students Have

Here is what happens to most students when they open the DBQ section of the AP World History exam:

They see seven documents — primary sources from different time periods, different regions, different perspectives. They read the documents. They pick out facts and quotes that seem relevant. They write an essay using those facts and quotes to support their thesis.

And then they lose 2 points — the sourcing point and the complexity point — because they never actually *analyzed* the documents. They used them as evidence, but they did not source them.

**This is not your fault.** Stanford historian Sam Wineburg has shown that historical thinking — especially sourcing documents — is "unnatural." It is a learned skill that requires explicit instruction and practice. In his research, professional historians sourced documents 99% of the time when reading primary sources, while students sourced them less than 33% of the time. Students do not skip sourcing because they are lazy or unprepared — they skip it because no one has taught them how to do it systematically.

That is what this course is for. By the end, sourcing will not feel unnatural anymore. It will be automatic — something you do every time you see a document, without thinking.

But first, let's understand what sourcing actually means.

What is the difference?

**Using a document as evidence:** "The author says that railroads brought economic benefits to India."

**Sourcing a document:** "As an Indian nationalist writing for a British colonial newspaper in 1920, the author strategically emphasizes economic benefits to make his argument for greater Indian autonomy more palatable to British readers, while downplaying the exploitative aspects that would alienate his audience."

The first version just repeats what the document says. The second version analyzes who wrote it, when, why, and for whom — and explains how that context shapes the document's meaning.

That second version is HIPP. And it is worth 2 out of 7 points on the DBQ.

---

### What HIPP Stands For

HIPP is a framework for analyzing primary source documents on the AP World History exam. Each letter represents one way of "sourcing" a document — reading it not just for what it says, but for *who* wrote it, *when*, *why*, and *for whom*.

**H — Historical Situation:** What was happening in the world when and where this document was created?

**I — Intended Audience:** Who was this document written for?

**P — Purpose:** Why did the author create this document? What were they trying to accomplish?

**P — Point of View (POV):** How does the author's identity, role, or position shape what they wrote?

You just watched Heimler use the HAPPY framework. Same idea, different acronym. His "A" is our "I" (Intended Audience), and his "Y" (why it matters) is something we build into every HIPP element — the explanation step that connects sourcing to your argument.

---

### Why HIPP Matters: The Point Math

Let's talk strategy. The DBQ is scored on a 7-point rubric. HIPP directly earns you points in two places:

**1. The Sourcing Point (1 point):** You earn this by doing HIPP analysis on at least 2 documents in your essay. You explain how or why a document's Historical Situation, Intended Audience, Purpose, or Point of View is relevant to your argument.

**2. The Complexity Point (1 point):** Since the 2023 rubric revision, one pathway to the complexity point is doing HIPP analysis on 4 or more documents. So strong sourcing does not just earn 1 point — it can unlock 2.

That means HIPP can be worth **2 out of 7 points on the DBQ**. Almost 30% of your score.

And here is the thing: these are the **most learnable points** on the exam. The sourcing point and the complexity point are skills you can practice and master. Unlike thesis writing, which depends on your historical knowledge, or evidence gathering, which depends on what you remember from the course, HIPP is a transferable method that works on *any* document from *any* time period.

You do not need to know everything about the Scramble for Africa to analyze a document from the Scramble for Africa. You just need to know how to read the source line, think about who wrote it and why, and connect that context to what the document says.

---

### HIPP Also Helps on Multiple Choice

Of the 55 MCQs on the AP World exam, 39 are stimulus-based — they give you a source and ask you to interpret it. Knowing how to read a source line, identify who wrote it, and think about why they wrote it is not just a DBQ skill. It is an *everything* skill.

When you see a stimulus-based MCQ, the first thing you should do is read the source line. Who wrote this? When? Why? That context tells you how to interpret the rest of the question.

## WORKED EXAMPLE 1: Full HIPP Analysis — Teacher Models the Thinking

Let's see how HIPP works on a real AP exam document. I will show you all four elements applied to the same document, and walk you through my thinking process.

First, read the document. Then I will show you my analysis.

---

**DOCUMENT 4 (2022 DBQ):**

*Source: Lobengula Khumalo, ruler of the Matabele (Ndebele) people of present-day Zimbabwe, contract with business associates of English businessman Cecil Rhodes,* mining company, 1888.

Let it be known that Charles Rudd of Kimberley (South Africa), Rochfort Maguire of London, and Francis Thompson of Kimberley have made an agreement to pay me and my heirs the monthly sum of 100 pounds sterling (British currency) and the delivery of 1,000 rifles made in England. Further, they have promised to deliver a steamboat with guns suitable for the defense of my territories on the Zambezi River.

In exchange for these presents, I hereby grant to the above-mentioned individuals and their heirs the complete and exclusive right to collect, sell, and enjoy the profits of all of the metals and minerals contained in my kingdoms. And since I have been much pestered lately by various persons and companies seeking these rights, I further grant to them the right to take all necessary measures to exclude all their competitors seeking mining rights and privileges from my kingdoms.

---

### Teacher's HIPP Analysis:

Now that you have read the document, here is how I analyze it using all four HIPP elements. In later modules, you will learn each one in depth. For now, just see how the framework works as a whole.

**H — Historical Situation:**

"This agreement was made during the Scramble for Africa, when European powers and private companies were using treaties and concession agreements to claim African resources and territory. By 1888, Cecil Rhodes's British South Africa Company was aggressively seeking mining rights across southern Africa. **This matters because** the concession format — offering rifles and money in exchange for exclusive mining rights — was a standard tool of imperial expansion, designed to create a legal veneer for resource extraction."

Notice: I start by identifying what was happening in the world at that time (Scramble for Africa, Rhodes's company seeking mining rights). Then I explain why that context matters to understanding the document — it shows this was part of a broader pattern of imperial expansion.

**I — Intended Audience:**

"The intended audience was the British government and European investors, who needed a signed document from an African ruler to legitimize their claims under international law. **This matters because** the agreement was written in English and structured in European legal terms that Lobengula may not have fully understood — the document was designed to be legible and enforceable in London, not in the Ndebele capital."

Notice: I identify who the document was written for (British government, investors), then explain why knowing the audience changes how we interpret the document — it was written in terms that made sense to Europeans, not necessarily to Lobengula.

**P — Purpose:**

"The purpose of this document was to secure exclusive mining rights for Rhodes's associates while appearing to offer a fair exchange. The rifles, monthly payment, and steamboat were meant to make the agreement look like a negotiation between equals. **This matters because** the 'exchange' was wildly unequal — the mining rights were worth millions, while the compensation was negligible — and the purpose was to create a legal basis for resource extraction, not genuine partnership."

Notice: I identify what the authors were trying to accomplish (secure mining rights, create appearance of fairness), then explain why understanding that purpose helps us see what the document is really doing — creating legal cover for exploitation.

**POV — Point of View:**

"This document represents the interests of European imperialists seeking to extract resources from southern Africa. As agents of Cecil Rhodes, the European signatories viewed African territory as a resource to be acquired through whatever means necessary. **This matters because** Lobengula later repudiated the agreement, claiming he had been deceived about its terms — which suggests the document reflects the perspective and goals of the European parties, not a genuine consensus."

Notice: I identify whose perspective shapes the document (European imperialists working for Rhodes), then explain how that perspective biases what the document says and how we should read it — it is one-sided, not a mutual agreement.

---

**Key Takeaway:** Each element starts with *identification* and ends with *explanation* ("this matters because..."). That two-step pattern is what earns points. You will practice it extensively starting in Module 2.

## WORKED EXAMPLE 2: Full HIPP Analysis — A Different Kind of Document

Let's see how HIPP works on a completely different type of document — an essay by a woman writer published in a conservative newspaper. The framework is the same.

Read the document first, then see my analysis.

---

**DOCUMENT 1 (2023 Set 2 DBQ):**

*Source: Aisha al-Taymuriyya, Egyptian woman poet and novelist, essay titled "Family Reform Comes Only Through the Education of Girls," article published in Al-Adab, a conservative Egyptian newspaper, 1889*

"It is the duty of the male establishment to concern itself with the education of girls and the refinement of families. Take the example of a man in the following situation — his educated wife loves him, comforts him, and expertly manages his household. Despite this, he works to hide her abilities from the rest of society, and fears that by announcing her worth it will be said that she is an educated woman, which will disrupt his peaceful life.

How astounding that our civilization is obsessed with decorating young women in jewelry, and exaggerating their beauty. But this practice casts these girls into a pit of evil; they have earned nothing from these trinkets but vanity. If male authorities strove for the improvement and progress of these girls' behavior through their education, these women would then find themselves adorned with necklaces strung with pearls of wisdom."

---

### Teacher's HIPP Analysis:

Now that you have read the document, here is my analysis:

**H — Historical Situation:**

"This essay was published in 1889, during a period of Egyptian modernization under British influence, when reformers debated how to strengthen Egyptian society. Education reform — particularly whether women should be educated — was a major point of contention. **This matters because** the call for girls' education was part of a broader modernization movement, and the author was entering a live political debate about the future of Egyptian society."

**I — Intended Audience:**

"The intended audience was the readership of Al-Adab, described as a conservative Egyptian newspaper. **This matters because** al-Taymuriyya was making a progressive argument (educate girls) to a conservative audience — she had to frame her argument in terms her readers would accept, which likely meant grounding it in Islamic tradition and family values rather than Western liberal ideas."

**P — Purpose:**

"The purpose was to persuade conservative Egyptians that educating girls was essential for family and social reform. **This matters because** the title itself — 'Family Reform Comes Only Through the Education of Girls' — frames education not as a feminist demand but as a practical necessity for family stability, which was a strategic rhetorical choice designed to appeal to her conservative audience."

**POV — Point of View:**

"As an elite Egyptian woman poet and novelist, al-Taymuriyya had personal experience with the benefits of education and could speak from lived authority on the subject. **This matters because** her status as an educated woman gave her credibility — she was proof that women's education produced accomplished individuals, not social chaos — which strengthened her argument."

---

**Key Takeaway:** This document is very different from the Lobengula concession — different time, place, type, and purpose. But the HIPP framework works the same way. That is its power: it is a universal tool.

Whether you are analyzing a legal agreement, a political essay, a government decree, a personal letter, or a religious text, you ask the same four questions:
- What was happening in the world? (H)
- Who was this for? (I)
- Why was it created? (P)
- How does the author's identity shape it? (POV)

And you always connect your answer to the document's meaning: "...and that matters because."

## What Makes HIPP Different from Just Reading the Document

Most students think they are analyzing documents when they quote them or paraphrase them. But that is not analysis — that is just reporting what the document says.

HIPP asks you to step back and think about the document as an artifact: Who made this? Why? For whom? What was going on at the time? How does all of that shape what the document says and how we should interpret it?

**Without HIPP (0 points):**
"The author argues that railroads brought economic benefits to India."

**With HIPP (1 point):**
"As an Indian nationalist writing for a British colonial newspaper in 1920, the author strategically emphasizes economic benefits to make his argument for Indian self-governance more palatable to British readers, who needed to see Indians as capable economic partners rather than subjects requiring permanent control. **This matters because** the focus on benefits rather than exploitation is a rhetorical choice shaped by his audience, not necessarily a comprehensive view of railroad impact."

The second version does not just report the claim — it analyzes why the author made that claim, who he was making it for, and how that context changes our interpretation.

That is sourcing. That is HIPP.

**Module 3: Purpose (P)**
You will learn how to figure out why the author created the document — what they were trying to accomplish, who they were trying to persuade, or what problem they were trying to solve. Purpose is about the author's goals.

**Module 4: Intended Audience (I)**
You will learn how to identify who the document was written for and explain how knowing the audience changes the way we interpret the document. Authors write differently for different audiences — the same person addressing the king writes differently than when addressing peasants.

**Module 5: Point of View (POV)**
You will learn how to analyze how the author's identity, role, or position shapes what they wrote. This is the hardest HIPP element because it requires you to think about bias, perspective, and how someone's social location influences their worldview.

## KEY TERMS

**HIPP Framework:** A method for analyzing primary source documents by examining four elements: Historical Situation, Intended Audience, Purpose, and Point of View.

**Historical Situation (H):** The historical context in which a document was created — what was happening in the world at that time and place.

**Intended Audience (I):** The person or group the document was written for — the intended recipient or reader.

**Purpose (P):** The author's reason for creating the document — what they were trying to accomplish or achieve.

**Point of View (POV):** How the author's identity, role, or social position shapes what they wrote and how they wrote it.

**Sourcing Point:** One point on the DBQ rubric earned by doing HIPP analysis on at least 2 documents, explaining how or why an element is relevant to your argument.

**Complexity Point:** One point on the DBQ rubric that can be earned (among other methods) by doing HIPP analysis on 4 or more documents.

**Source Line:** The italicized text above a document that tells you the author, date, type of document, and sometimes the context or audience. Always read this first.

**Primary Source:** An original document from the time period being studied, created by someone who was there. All DBQ documents are primary sources.
