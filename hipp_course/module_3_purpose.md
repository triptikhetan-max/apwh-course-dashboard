## ARTICLE CONTENT

### Quick Reminder: What Heimler Said About Purpose

In Module 1, you watched Heimler explain the HAPPY framework. Remember his "P" for Purpose: it is not about what the document says — it is about what the author was trying to DO with the document.

A petition is trying to persuade. A propaganda poster is trying to mobilize. A personal diary is trying to process experience. The "why did they write this?" question is Purpose.

Now let's learn how to analyze it in a way that earns points.

---

### What Is Purpose?

Purpose is the most misunderstood HIPP element. Students constantly confuse it with summary. Here is the difference:

- **Summary** tells you what the document says. ("The author argues that women should be educated.")
- **Purpose** tells you why the document exists. ("The author created this essay in order to persuade conservative readers that educating girls would strengthen families and society.")

Those are two completely different things. Summary is the WHAT. Purpose is the WHY.

Think about it this way: every document was created for a reason. Nobody writes a petition, paints a propaganda poster, or publishes a newspaper article by accident. They are trying to accomplish something — to persuade, to warn, to justify, to expose, to celebrate, to request, to mobilize. Your job is to figure out what that something is.

**The Key Question**

Every time you encounter a document, ask yourself: *"What was the author trying to DO by creating this?"*

Not "What does the author say?" Not "What does the document describe?" But: **What action or outcome was the author trying to produce?**

Examples:
- A petition to the British government is trying to **change colonial policy**
- A propaganda poster for public display is trying to **build support for the regime**
- A newspaper editorial is trying to **shape public opinion**
- A memoir published decades later is trying to **preserve a personal narrative** or **justify past actions**

See the pattern? Purpose is always about the **intended effect** of the document on the world.

---

### Why This Matters on the Exam (The Point Math)

Purpose is one of the four HIPP elements that earns the sourcing point. The DBQ rubric requires HIPP analysis on at least 2 documents for the sourcing point (1 point) and on 4+ documents for one pathway to complexity (1 point).

Just like with Historical Situation, you need to do two things:

1. **Identify** the author's purpose (Step 1)
2. **Explain** why that purpose matters to how we read the document (Step 2)

Identification alone is worth zero points. You must connect the purpose to the document's content, tone, or argument.

The rubric language is the same: **"Explains how or why"** the Purpose is relevant. Not "identifies" — **explains**.

This two-step pattern is your key to earning the point.

---

### The Golden Rule: "The author created this in order to..."

Just like Historical Situation had "...and that matters because," Purpose has its own golden phrase:

**"The author created this in order to..."**

This phrase forces you to name an action — to persuade, to justify, to expose, to mobilize, to warn, to celebrate. It prevents you from slipping into summary mode.

**Bad example (0 points):**
> "The author says that factory conditions were terrible and workers were exploited."

This is summary. It tells us what the document says. It does not tell us why the author wrote it. Zero points.

**Good example (earns the point):**
> "The author created this account in order to expose exploitative labor conditions and inflame public opinion against the factory system. This matters because by describing labor conditions as 'slavery,' the author was not just reporting facts — they were using emotionally charged language to mobilize readers to demand reform, which shapes the document's tone and word choices."

See the difference? The good example names an action (expose, inflame, mobilize) and explains why that purpose shapes the document (explains the emotional language and tone).

---

### **The "To Inform" Trap — AVOID THIS**

Here is the single most common mistake students make with Purpose: they write **"to inform."**

Example:
> "The purpose was to inform the reader about conditions in the factory."

This is almost always wrong — or at least not analytical enough to earn a point. Very few documents are created purely "to inform." Even a government report has a purpose beyond information: it is trying to justify a policy, request funding, document a crisis for political reasons, or establish a bureaucratic record.

**"To inform" is the Purpose equivalent of "this was written in China" for Historical Situation** — so vague it means nothing.

Replace it with a specific action verb:
- **to persuade** (change someone's mind)
- **to justify** (defend an action or decision)
- **to expose** (reveal something hidden or suppressed)
- **to warn** (alert to danger)
- **to celebrate** (commemorate or praise)
- **to request** (ask for something)
- **to mobilize** (inspire action)
- **to document** (create a record for specific reasons)
- **to legitimize** (establish authority or rightness)

When you catch yourself writing "to inform," stop. Ask: What specific action was the author trying to produce? What did they want their audience to DO, THINK, or FEEL?

---

### Sentence Frames: Your Training Wheels

Here are sentence frames you can use while learning. Pick whichever feels most natural. The non-negotiable parts are: (1) naming a specific action the author was trying to accomplish, and (2) explaining why that purpose shapes the document.

**Frame 1 (Full structure):**
> "The author created this in order to [action verb: persuade / justify / expose / warn / celebrate / request / mobilize], and that matters because [connection to document's tone, content, word choices, or argument]."

**Example:**
> "The author created this petition in order to persuade the British government to change colonial policy by requesting better infrastructure for Indian merchants. This matters because the petition uses deferential language ('humbly request') rather than demanding rights, which reveals the author's strategic purpose: to secure concessions from within the colonial system rather than challenge British authority directly."

---

**Frame 2 (Because-therefore):**
> "The purpose of this document was to [specific action] because [what the author wanted to happen as a result], which explains why the author [specific choice in the document: emotional language / selective evidence / particular framing]."

**Example:**
> "The purpose of this document was to expose labor exploitation because the author wanted to shock readers into demanding reform, which explains why the author compared factory conditions to 'slavery' — an emotionally charged word designed to provoke moral outrage rather than present a neutral description."

---

**Frame 3 (Document-as-tool):**
> "This document was a tool for [achieving X goal], and knowing that purpose matters because [it explains why the document presents information in a particular way]."

**Example:**
> "This propaganda poster was a tool for mobilizing public support for World War I by depicting the enemy as barbaric, and knowing that purpose matters because it explains why the poster uses exaggerated imagery and emotional appeals rather than rational arguments — the goal was not to inform but to inflame."

---

**How to use these frames:**

1. Start by filling in the blanks with information from the source line and document
2. Always name a specific action verb (avoid "inform")
3. Always complete the explanation clause (why the purpose matters)
4. As you practice, you will internalize the pattern and stop needing the formula

The frames are not the goal — they are the scaffold.

## WORKED EXAMPLE 1: Teacher Models the Thinking (ME)

Let's walk through this together. I am going to show you exactly how I think when analyzing Purpose. This is Document 4 from the 2025 Set 1 DBQ.

First, read the document. Then I will walk you through my thinking process.

---

**DOCUMENT 4 (2025 Set 1 DBQ):**

*Source: Peninsular and Oriental Steam Navigation Company (P&O), a British shipping company that provided passenger and freight service from Great Britain to many parts of the world, an advertising brochure for travelers on P&O ships to Egypt, 1908*

"As we leave the ship at Alexandria and board the train to Cairo, we may observe simple farm and rural scenes but also, here and there, the huge new cotton mills that remind us that Egypt is progressive and up to date.

Cairo has changed greatly since 1882,¹ and it is inevitable that, in a country so closely in touch with Western civilization, many traditional local shops have been replaced by storefronts with large windows displaying products of European industry. In the west end of Cairo huge hotels and commercial buildings have replaced the former delightful residences of the elite. In the hotels, the vibrant social life of modern Cairo is in full swing, making the city attractive to many."

1: Beginning in 1882, Great Britain effectively controlled Egypt without formally colonizing the country.

---

### Teacher's Thinking Process:

Now that you have read the document, here is how I think through the Purpose analysis step by step.

**Step 1 — Read the source line and identify what KIND of document this is.**

I see: a British shipping company (P&O). An advertising brochure. 1908. The document is about Egypt.

This is an advertisement. Advertisements have one fundamental purpose: to sell something. But what specifically, and why does that matter?

**Step 2 — Ask: What was the author trying to DO by creating this?**

P&O is a shipping company. They created this brochure to sell travel tickets to Egypt. But look deeper: the brochure describes the "modernization" of Cairo since 1882 (when Britain took control) — cotton mills, European shops, hotels.

So the purpose is not just "sell tickets." It is "sell tickets BY presenting British-controlled Egypt as a modern, safe, appealing destination for Western tourists."

Notice: I am thinking about WHY they chose to highlight "modernization" and European amenities. That tells me something about their purpose.

**Step 3 — Write it using the frame, making sure to explain WHY the purpose matters.**

> "The purpose of this brochure was to sell travel tickets to Egypt by presenting British-controlled Cairo as a modern, civilized destination with European shops, hotels, and cotton mills. This matters because P&O had a direct commercial interest in portraying Egypt positively — they profited from tourist traffic — which means the brochure is NOT an objective description of Egyptian conditions. It selectively highlights 'modernization' that would appeal to Western travelers while ignoring aspects of Egyptian society that might discourage tourism. Knowing this purpose helps us understand why the document emphasizes European-style amenities rather than the experiences of ordinary Egyptians under British control."

**What I did:**
1. Named a specific commercial purpose (sell tickets by framing Egypt as modern and safe)
2. Explained why that purpose matters (the company profits from tourism, so the description is selective and biased toward positive aspects)
3. Connected the purpose to specific choices in the document (emphasis on European amenities, not Egyptian perspectives)

**What I did NOT do:**
- I did not say "the purpose was to inform people about Egypt" (the "to inform" trap)
- I did not summarize what the brochure says
- I did not confuse Purpose with Point of View

## WORKED EXAMPLE 2: Good vs. Bad (The Spectrum)

Now let's look at Document 7 from the 2024 Set 1 DBQ (Soviet newspaper propaganda article). I will show you three attempts at Purpose analysis, ranked from worst to best.

First, read the document. Then see the three attempts.

---

**DOCUMENT 7 (2024 Set 1 DBQ):**

*Source: Richard Kosolapov, Soviet journalist, former editor in chief of the Communist Party's journal Komunist, article in Pravda, the largest Soviet newspaper, 1983*

"In our society today, there have been some discussions about moving toward greater acceptance of 'private ownership' and 'private enterprise.' These discussions are clearly unfounded, and they can be dangerous. We have all seen how easy it is for uncommunist-like behaviors and ideas to spring up spontaneously among us when we let our collectivist spirit grow weak or die. Party bureaucrats work hard to foster a healthy social and educational climate. All sorts of bribe takers, thieves, grifters, and parasites stand ready to steal the fruits of others' labor. Their 'entrepreneurship' is nothing but a desire to pocket the loose kopek (penny) of the honest Soviet citizens. Such people are especially dangerous because they, like moths, eat holes in the fabric of socialist economic and social relations and try to draw others into their selfishly individualistic, anti-collectivist worldview.

These advocates of more private ownership, materialism, and consumerism are enemies of the socialist state, and the struggle against them must be uncompromising. Maintaining full central control over both production and consumption is of utmost importance for strengthening the organic wholeness of our system. Socialist society is a working society, not a consumer society."

---

### Three Attempts at Purpose Analysis:

**ATTEMPT 1 (Bad — 0 points):**
> "The purpose was to inform Soviet citizens about capitalism."

**Why it fails:** This falls directly into the "to inform" trap. A state-controlled newspaper in the Soviet Union is not trying to "inform" — it is trying to persuade, promote, and control public opinion. "To inform" is almost never the right answer for propaganda. Zero points.

---

**ATTEMPT 2 (Better, but still 0 points):**
> "The purpose was propaganda."

**Why it still fails:** "Propaganda" is a label, not an analysis. You have named the category but not explained what the propaganda was trying to DO or why that matters. This is like saying "the purpose was to have a purpose." You need to explain what specific effect the author was trying to produce. Zero points.

---

**ATTEMPT 3 (Earns the point):**
> "The purpose of this article was to reinforce Soviet citizens' loyalty to communism by contrasting the alleged prosperity of socialist societies with the supposed failures of capitalist ones. This matters because the state-controlled newspaper was not reporting objectively — it was selecting and framing information to promote a specific ideological worldview. Knowing this purpose helps explain why the article presents a one-sided comparison that demonizes capitalism while ignoring any shortcomings of the Soviet system."

**Why it earns the point:**
- Names a specific action (reinforce loyalty through ideological contrast)
- Explains why that purpose shapes the document (selective framing, one-sided comparison)
- Connects purpose to how we should read the document (not as objective reporting but as ideological persuasion)

**Key takeaway:** The difference between 0 points and 1 point is specificity and explanation. Name the action. Explain why it matters.

## Common Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

These are the patterns AP Chief Readers see every year. Knowing what NOT to do is just as important as knowing what to do.

**Mistake 1: Saying "to inform."**

Example:
> "The purpose was to inform the reader about conditions in the factory." (0 points)

What's missing? Specific action. Documents are created to DO something, not just to pass along information. Replace "to inform" with a specific action verb: to persuade, to justify, to expose, to warn, to celebrate, to request, to mobilize.

Fix it:
> "The purpose was to expose exploitative factory conditions and mobilize public opinion against the factory system, because the author used emotionally charged language ('slavery') designed to provoke outrage rather than present a neutral report." (1 point)

---

**Mistake 2: Summarizing instead of analyzing purpose.**

Example:
> "The author argues that women should be educated and that families need reform." (0 points — this is summary)

What's missing? Any mention of what the author was trying to ACCOMPLISH by making that argument. Why did they write it? What outcome did they want?

Fix it:
> "The purpose was to persuade conservative Egyptians that educating girls was essential for family stability, because by framing education as necessary for 'family reform' rather than women's rights, the author tailored her argument to values her conservative audience would accept." (1 point — this is Purpose analysis)

---

**Mistake 3: Confusing Purpose with Point of View.**

Purpose = what the author was trying to DO (the action or outcome they wanted)
POV = how the author's identity shaped what they wrote (bias, perspective, position)

They are related but distinct.

Example of confusion:
> "The purpose was shaped by the author's identity as a colonial governor." (0 points — this is actually POV)

What to do instead: Keep them separate.
- Purpose: "The purpose was to request more troops to suppress resistance."
- POV: "As a colonial governor, the author's perspective was shaped by belief in European superiority."

---

**Mistake 4: Vague action verbs.**

Example:
> "The purpose was to express feelings about the topic." (0 points)

What's missing? Specificity. Express what feelings? To what end? "Express" and "share" are almost as bad as "inform." They do not name a concrete action or intended effect.

Fix it: Use verbs that imply a specific intended effect — persuade, justify, expose, warn, request, mobilize, commemorate, legitimize, document (for a specific reason).

---

**Mistake 5: Identification without explanation.**

Example:
> "The purpose was to persuade." (0 points — identification only)

What's missing? The "and that matters because" clause. You named the purpose but did not explain why it shapes the document.

Fix it:
> "The purpose was to persuade the British government to change colonial policy, which matters because the petition's deferential tone ('humbly request') reveals the author's strategic calculation: securing concessions required appealing to British interests rather than challenging their authority." (1 point)

## What's Next: Intended Audience

You now have two HIPP elements down:
- **Historical Situation (H):** What was happening in the world?
- **Purpose (P):** What was the author trying to accomplish?

Next up: **Intended Audience (I)** — who was this written for, and how does knowing the audience change the way we interpret the document?

Then you will learn **Point of View (POV)**, the hardest element. After that, you will practice combining all four elements on new documents.

---

## KEY TERMS

**Purpose (P):** The author's reason for creating the document — what they were trying to accomplish, achieve, or make happen. NOT what the document says (that is summary), but why the document exists.

**Action Verb:** A specific verb that names what the author was trying to do: persuade, justify, expose, warn, celebrate, request, mobilize, legitimize, document. These verbs identify the intended effect of the document.

**"To Inform" Trap:** The most common Purpose error — saying a document's purpose was "to inform" when nearly all documents have a more specific purpose beyond neutral information-sharing. Replace "to inform" with a specific action verb.

**Summary vs. Purpose:** Summary tells you what the document says. Purpose tells you why the document was created. They are not the same. The exam rewards Purpose analysis, not summary.

**Intended Effect:** The change or outcome the author wanted to produce by creating the document — what they wanted their audience to think, feel, or do.
