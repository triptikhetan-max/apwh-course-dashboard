## 1. The Academic Foundation

### Historical Thinking Is "Unnatural"

The most important insight for anyone designing document analysis instruction comes from Sam Wineburg's foundational research at Stanford: **historical thinking is an unnatural act**. It goes against the grain of how humans ordinarily process information.

Wineburg's landmark 1991 study compared how professional historians and high-achieving high school students read the same set of primary documents about the Battle of Lexington Green. The findings were stark:

- **Historians engaged in sourcing 99% of the time** — examining who wrote a document, when, why, and for whom *before* reading the content itself.
- **High-achieving students sourced less than one-third of the time** — they jumped straight to content, treating documents like textbook passages to be absorbed rather than interrogated.

This gap is not about intelligence. Wineburg's research showed that the kind of textured interrogation that comes automatically to historians is a highly specialized, domain-specific skill that must be deliberately taught. Even a history-major student teacher was less able to pull back from the text and examine its construction than a former physics major who had been trained as a historian.

### Why "Unnatural"?

Three cognitive tendencies work against historical thinking:

1. **Presentism**: Students naturally interpret the past through present-day values and assumptions. They see concepts like prejudice, tolerance, and fairness as timeless truths rather than historically situated ideas. This makes POV analysis especially difficult — students cannot understand why Lincoln's views on race were progressive for his era because they measure him by 2024 standards.

2. **The textbook effect**: Textbooks eliminate what Wineburg calls "metadiscourse" — the places where an author reveals their positionality. Written in omniscient third person, textbooks train students to see history as a single settled narrative rather than contested interpretation. Students then bring this same passive reading approach to primary sources.

3. **Confirmation bias**: Students shape new information to fit what they already believe. Wineburg observed students making evidence conform to pre-existing narratives rather than letting evidence challenge their assumptions.

### The Implication for Teaching

The gap between comprehension and analysis is not a continuum students naturally progress along. It requires explicit instruction, modeling, and deliberate practice. Students will not "pick up" sourcing skills by simply being exposed to documents. The skill must be directly taught, modeled, and scaffolded.

## 2. College Board Official Guidance

### Skill 2: Sourcing and Situation in the Course and Exam Description (CED)

The AP World History CED defines six historical thinking skills. Skill 2 — Sourcing and Situation — is the skill most directly tested through document analysis:

**What the CED says Skill 2 requires:**
- Analyzing primary and secondary sources in their historical context
- Considering the author, historical context, author's point of view, and intended audience
- Understanding how and why a source was produced

The CED frames sourcing not as a mechanical checklist exercise but as a fundamental historical thinking practice — the ability to evaluate evidence by understanding its origins and context.

### How Skill 2 Is Tested Across the Exam

| Section | How Sourcing Appears |
|---|---|
| **Multiple Choice** | Sets of 3-4 questions based on stimulus material; questions may ask about author's perspective, purpose, or historical context |
| **SAQ Q1** | Required; 1-2 secondary sources; focus on historical developments 1200-2001 |
| **SAQ Q2** | Required; 1 primary source; focus on historical developments 1200-2001 |
| **DBQ** | 7 documents; students must demonstrate sourcing analysis for at least 2 documents to earn the sourcing point |

### The Key CED Principle

The CED treats sourcing as inseparable from argument-making. Documents are not neutral containers of information — they are artifacts produced by specific people in specific circumstances for specific reasons. Understanding those circumstances is essential to using documents as evidence.

## 3. The DBQ Rubric: What Sourcing Actually Requires

### The 7-Point DBQ Rubric (Post-2023 Revision)

| Category | Points | Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Thesis/Claim | 1 | Historically defensible argument |
| Contextualization | 1 | Broader historical background |
| Evidence from Documents | 2 | Use of documents to support argument (need 4 docs for full credit) |
| Evidence Beyond Documents | 1 | One piece of outside knowledge |
| **Sourcing (HIPP)** | **1** | **Explain how/why HIPP of at least 2 documents is relevant to argument** |
| Complexity | 1 | Nuance, multiple perspectives, or connections |

### Critical Rubric Language: "Explain How or Why"

The single most important phrase in the sourcing requirement is **"explain how or why."** The rubric does not say "identify" or "name" — it requires explanation of relevance.

To earn the sourcing point, a student must:
1. **Identify** a HIPP element (Historical situation, Intended audience, Purpose, or Point of view) for a document
2. **Explain** how or why that element is relevant to their argument

This two-step requirement is where most students fail. They identify without explaining.

### The Complexity Connection

The 2023 rubric revision created a powerful connection between sourcing and complexity. Students can earn the complexity point (Row 6) by:
- Using all 7 documents effectively, OR
- Providing HIPP analysis on 4+ documents

This means sourcing is no longer just worth 1 point — strong sourcing can also unlock the complexity point, making it potentially worth 2 points on the exam.

### What Changed in 2023

The revised rubric:
- Reduced required documents from 6 to 4 for full evidence credit
- Reduced sourcing requirement from 3 documents to 2
- Added explicit pathways to earn complexity through sourcing (4+ HIPP analyses)
- Provided clearer definitions of what constitutes acceptable sourcing

## 4. Wineburg's Historical Thinking Framework

### The Four Heuristics

Based on Wineburg's empirical research observing historians at work, four historical reading strategies define disciplinary thinking:

#### 1. Sourcing (The Sourcing Heuristic)
- **What it is**: Examining the attribution of a document *before* reading its content
- **What historians ask**: Who wrote this? When? What were their credentials? What motivated them? Who was the intended audience?
- **Why it matters**: Wineburg calls this not merely a "strategy" but a "weltanschauung" — an entire way of apprehending the world. It represents the foundational distinction between reading for comprehension and reading historically.
- **The key insight**: Historians source *before* they read for content. Students do the reverse — they read for content first (if they source at all).

#### 2. Contextualization
- **What it is**: Anchoring texts in their specific time and place
- **What historians do**: Situate the document within the political, social, economic, and cultural conditions of its era
- **Student difficulty**: Students tend to see the past through modern lenses (presentism) rather than reconstructing the worldview of the time period

#### 3. Corroboration
- **What it is**: Comparing information across multiple documents
- **What historians do**: Check claims in one source against claims in other sources to identify agreements, contradictions, and gaps
- **Student difficulty**: Students tend to treat each document in isolation rather than reading documents against each other

#### 4. Close Reading
- **What it is**: Deeper analysis of language, word choice, and rhetoric
- **What historians do**: Examine how language is used to persuade, what is included vs. omitted, and what assumptions underlie the text
- **Student difficulty**: Students read for surface meaning and main idea rather than interrogating language choices

### Wineburg's Core Argument About Teaching

Wineburg argues that students need to be taught to "think like historians" not because they will become professional historians, but precisely because most will not. The goals of school history are not vocational — they are to prepare students to:
- **Tolerate complexity** rather than seeking simple answers
- **Adapt to new situations** rather than relying on fixed schemas
- **Resist the first answer** rather than accepting information at face value

This framing matters for curriculum design: HIPP is not just an exam skill — it is a transferable critical thinking disposition.

## 5. SHEG / Reading Like a Historian

### Background

The Stanford History Education Group (SHEG), founded by Sam Wineburg in 2002, developed the **Reading Like a Historian (RLH)** curriculum. In 2024, SHEG spun out of Stanford to become an independent nonprofit called the **Digital Inquiry Group (DIG)**. The curriculum is available free at sheg.stanford.edu and inquirygroup.org.

### The Research Evidence

Abby Reisman's landmark 2012 study (published in *Cognition and Instruction*) tested RLH in a six-month quasi-experimental intervention with 236 11th-grade students across five San Francisco urban high schools. The results showed that **RLH students outperformed control students on all four measured dimensions**:

1. **Historical thinking ability** — students better interrogated sources
2. **Transfer to contemporary issues** — historical thinking strategies generalized
3. **Factual knowledge mastery** — inquiry-based learning improved fact retention
4. **General reading comprehension** — disciplinary reading improved general reading

This is the only extended curriculum intervention study in disciplinary historical reading in urban schools, and its findings have been highly influential.

### The RLH Lesson Design

Every RLH lesson shares a common structure:

1. **Central Historical Question** — Each lesson revolves around a debatable question (not a factual one)
2. **Background Knowledge Activation** — Students review relevant context before encountering documents
3. **Document Analysis** — Students read modified primary sources using sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, and close reading
4. **Small Group Discussion** — Students analyze documents collaboratively
5. **Whole-Class Discussion** — Students offer competing interpretations and reconcile responses
6. **Evidence-Based Conclusion** — Students write responses using evidence from documents

### Scaffolding for Struggling Readers

RLH was designed specifically for diverse learners, including English language learners and struggling readers. Key scaffolding strategies:

- **Modified documents**: Primary sources are adapted for reading level while preserving historical authenticity
- **Targeted guiding questions**: Questions direct attention to specific sourcing elements
- **Graphic organizers**: Visual tools help students track sourcing analysis across documents
- **Explicit strategy instruction**: Teachers model the thinking process (think-alouds) before students practice independently
- **Formative assessment**: Ongoing checks for understanding during document analysis

### Why RLH Works: The Design Principles

RLH lessons are intentionally designed so that the documents **create interpretive tension**. The sources don't all agree — they offer competing accounts, which forces students to evaluate evidence rather than simply absorb it. This is fundamentally different from textbook instruction, where there is one "right answer."

The culminating whole-class discussion is not optional decoration — it is the pedagogical core. Students must publicly defend their interpretations using evidence, which requires them to articulate their sourcing analysis verbally.

## 6. The DBQ Project Method

### Philosophy

The DBQ Project (dbqproject.com) is the most widely used DBQ teaching resource in the United States. Its core philosophy treats document-based questions as **a method of instruction, not just an assessment format**. The goal is to help students read smart, think straight, and write clearly.

### The Six-Step Method

| Step | Activity | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1. The Hook | Engaging activity to build interest | Creates buy-in and activates prior knowledge |
| 2. Background Essay | Short reading on context | Builds the knowledge base needed for document analysis |
| 3. Clarifying the Question | Define key terms, pre-bucket | Ensures students understand what they're being asked |
| 4. Close Analysis | Read and analyze each document | The core sourcing/analysis work |
| 5. Bucketing | Group documents by theme/argument | Teaches organizational thinking for essay structure |
| 6. Writing | From thrash-out to essay | Converts analysis into argumentative writing |

### The Enhanced Version vs. Clean Version System

This is the DBQ Project's signature scaffolding innovation. Every Mini-Q lesson exists in two forms:

- **Enhanced Version (EV)**: Highly scaffolded. Document analysis questions appear directly below each document. Students are guided with specific questions that direct their attention to key sourcing elements. The Highly Scaffolded Writing Form walks students from facts to inferences to arguments in full sentences.

- **Clean Version**: Same documents, no scaffolding. Students must generate their own analysis without guided questions. This version simulates exam conditions.

The pedagogical logic: Start students on the Enhanced Version to build skills and confidence, then gradually transition to the Clean Version as they develop independence. The progression is:

**Maximal scaffolding** (guided questions help students find essentials) --> **Minimal scaffolding** (template differentiates facts from inferences) --> **No scaffolding** (independent analysis)

### Four Document Analysis Sheets

The DBQ Project provides four different document analysis sheets at varying scaffolding levels:

1. **Highly Scaffolded Writing Form**: Full sentence responses; guides students through fact --> inference --> argument progression
2. **Shrunken Long Form**: Condensed version; practices conciseness while maintaining analytical structure
3. **Less Scaffolded Templates**: Differentiate between facts and inferences with minimal guidance
4. **Minimal/No Scaffolding Forms**: Open-ended analysis matching exam conditions

### The Bucketing Strategy

"Bucketing" is the DBQ Project's term for grouping documents into analytical categories. This concept directly maps to the essay structure students need for the DBQ.

Bucketing scaffolding levels:
1. **Level 1**: Teacher provides bucket labels (most scaffolded)
2. **Level 2**: Class creates bucket labels together through discussion (requires prediction)
3. **Level 3**: Students discover and label buckets independently (least scaffolded)

The "chickenfoot" graphic organizer supports bucketing — students create a visual map connecting their thesis (the body) to their supporting categories (the legs).

### Marzano Research Connection

The DBQ Project explicitly grounds its methods in Robert Marzano's evidence-based instructional strategies, particularly:
- Identifying similarities and differences (bucketing)
- Nonlinguistic representations (graphic organizers, chickenfoot)
- Cooperative learning (small group document analysis)
- Generating and testing hypotheses (pre-bucketing predictions)

## 7. Framework Comparison

### Overview of Major Document Analysis Frameworks

| Framework | Stands For | Origin | Elements | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **HIPP** | Historical situation, Intended audience, Purpose, Point of view | AP History (College Board aligned) | 4 | AP DBQ sourcing point |
| **HIPPO** | HIPP + Outside information | AP History (teacher variant) | 5 | Connecting sourcing to outside evidence |
| **HAPPY** | Historical context, Audience, Purpose, Point of view, whY it matters | AP History (teacher variant) | 5 | Adding significance/relevance step |
| **SOAPSTone** | Speaker, Occasion, Audience, Purpose, Subject, Tone | AP English / Rhetoric | 6 | Rhetorical analysis, AP English |
| **ACE** | Answer, Cite evidence, Explain | AP History SAQs | 3 | Short Answer Questions (not DBQ) |
| **TEA** | Topic sentence, Evidence, Analysis | AP History SAQs (alternative) | 3 | Short Answer Questions (not DBQ) |

### HIPP: The Standard for AP History DBQs

**Strengths:**
- Maps directly onto the AP DBQ rubric categories
- Simple (only 4 elements to remember)
- Each element directly matches what exam readers look for
- College Board explicitly uses these categories in scoring guidelines

**Limitations:**
- Does not include a "so what?" step — students can identify elements without explaining relevance
- Does not include subject/content analysis (assumes students already comprehend the document)
- Does not explicitly prompt for tone or emotional register

### HAPPY: HIPP + Relevance

HAPPY adds a critical fifth element: **Y = "Why does it matter?"** (significance/relevance). This directly addresses the most common student error — identifying HIPP elements without explaining their relevance to the argument.

**Why HAPPY may be superior for instruction**: The "Y" forces students to complete the analytical loop. Instead of stopping at "the audience was Parliament," HAPPY requires "...and this matters because the author is trying to persuade lawmakers to change policy, which shows the document is advocating reform rather than describing reality."

### HIPPO: HIPP + Outside Information

HIPPO adds **O = Organization/Outside Information**, prompting students to connect document analysis to their broader historical knowledge and to their essay structure. This is useful because it bridges the sourcing point and the evidence-beyond-documents point on the rubric.

### SOAPSTone: The Rhetorical Alternative

SOAPSTone was originally designed for AP English and has been adapted for AP History. It includes 6 elements, making it more comprehensive but also more complex.

**Key differences from HIPP:**
- Includes "Subject" (content summary) and "Tone" (emotional register), which HIPP does not
- "Speaker" overlaps with but is not identical to "Point of View"
- "Occasion" overlaps with but is not identical to "Historical Situation"
- Does not explicitly include "Purpose" as prominently as HIPP does

**Why SOAPSTone is less preferred for AP History**: It was designed for rhetorical analysis of literary texts, not historical source analysis. The categories don't map directly onto the AP History rubric, and the inclusion of "Subject" and "Tone" can distract students from the sourcing analysis that actually earns rubric points.

### ACE and TEA: SAQ-Specific Frameworks

These are writing frameworks for Short Answer Questions, not document analysis frameworks. ACE (Answer, Cite, Explain) and TEA (Topic sentence, Evidence, Analysis) are useful for structuring SAQ responses but should not be confused with document sourcing tools.

**Note on ACE**: Some AP teachers prefer TEA over ACE because "Cite" in ACE implies quoting from text, whereas AP History SAQs (especially no-stimulus ones) require students to deploy their own historical knowledge rather than quoting documents.

### Recommendation for Struggling Students

**HAPPY is likely the most effective framework for struggling students** because:
1. It includes all four HIPP elements the rubric requires
2. The "Y" (Why?) step explicitly teaches the explain-relevance skill that students most commonly miss
3. It is only one element more than HIPP, so it does not add significant cognitive load
4. The "Why?" prompt directly addresses the rubric language ("explain how or why")

For more advanced students ready for complexity, HIPPO adds the organizational/outside information step that can help earn both the sourcing point and the complexity point.

## 8. Cognitive Science: Why Students Struggle

### Bloom's Taxonomy and the Comprehension-Analysis Gap

The core challenge of HIPP instruction maps directly onto Bloom's Taxonomy:

| Level | Bloom's Category | Document Analysis Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Remember | Recall who wrote the document, when |
| 2 | Understand | Summarize what the document says |
| 3 | Apply | Use document content as evidence for an argument |
| **4** | **Analyze** | **Break down the document's sourcing — examine bias, purpose, POV** |
| 5 | Evaluate | Judge the document's reliability, compare across sources |
| 6 | Create | Construct an original argument using document analysis |

Most students naturally operate at Levels 1-3. The DBQ rubric demands Levels 4-6. This gap is not a matter of effort — it represents a qualitative shift in cognitive processing that requires explicit instruction.

### Why POV and Purpose Are So Hard to Distinguish

Students consistently confuse Point of View and Purpose. The cognitive reason:

- **Purpose** is about the author's **goal** — what they want to accomplish (persuade, inform, justify, critique, etc.)
- **Point of View** is about the author's **identity and position** — how their background, role, and circumstances shape what they see and how they express it

The confusion arises because these are deeply interrelated: an author's identity shapes their goals, and their goals reflect their identity. For students who are still developing analytical abstraction, separating these overlapping concepts is genuinely difficult.

**Teaching distinction:**
- For **Purpose**, ask: "What does the author want to *happen* as a result of this document?"
- For **POV**, ask: "How does the author's *identity* (role, class, gender, nationality, experience) shape what they say and how they say it?"

### The Metacognitive Dimension

Source analysis requires metacognition — the ability to think about one's own thinking. Students must simultaneously:
1. Comprehend the document's content (cognitive task)
2. Monitor their own comprehension (metacognitive task)
3. Step outside the content to analyze the document's construction (metacognitive task)
4. Connect that analysis to an argument (higher-order cognitive task)

Research shows that many students struggle to engage meaningfully in metacognitive processes. They can perform the cognitive task (understanding content) but cannot simultaneously perform the metacognitive task (analyzing how and why the content was constructed as it was).

This is why explicit strategy instruction works better than implicit practice: students need to be taught *what* to think about, not just *how* to think.

### Historical Empathy and Perspective Taking

A related cognitive challenge is historical empathy — the ability to understand how people in the past thought and felt within their own context rather than judging them by modern standards.

Research distinguishes two dimensions:
- **Cognitive historical empathy** (perspective taking): Understanding the reasoning and worldview of historical actors
- **Affective historical empathy** (care): Feeling emotional connection to historical experiences

For document analysis, cognitive empathy is essential. Students must be able to ask: "Given this person's circumstances, beliefs, and available information, why would they write this?" This requires suppressing presentism — a significant cognitive effort.

Effective strategies for building historical empathy:
- Role-playing exercises where students adopt historical perspectives
- Examining multiple documents from different viewpoints on the same event
- Explicit discussion of how context shapes perspective
- Local history projects that create personal connections to historical processes

## 9. Common Student Misconceptions (from Chief Reader Reports)

### What AP Exam Readers Consistently Report

The following errors appear repeatedly in Chief Reader Reports from 2023, 2024, and 2025 for AP World History and AP US History:

#### Error 1: Identifying Without Explaining Relevance
**The most common error.** Students name a HIPP element but do not explain how or why it matters.

- **Wrong**: "The intended audience of this document is the king."
- **Right**: "Because the audience is the king, the author emphasizes military threats and requests resources — this shows the document is a petition for support rather than an objective description of conditions."

#### Error 2: Summarizing Instead of Analyzing
Students describe what the document says rather than analyzing why or how it was constructed.

- **Wrong**: "In this document, the author describes the harsh conditions in the factory."
- **Right**: "The author, a factory inspector appointed by Parliament, emphasizes harsh conditions because his professional role required him to identify violations — his purpose was to build a case for regulatory intervention."

#### Error 3: Failing to Connect Sourcing to the Argument
Students may correctly identify a HIPP element but fail to link it to their thesis.

The rubric requires that sourcing be "relevant to an argument that addresses the prompt." Sourcing that floats disconnected from the essay's argument does not earn the point.

#### Error 4: No New Information Beyond the Document
Some students attempt sourcing by restating information already present in the document or its attribution line. Successful sourcing must bring in additional contextual knowledge.

- **Wrong**: "This document was written in 1848" (just restating the attribution)
- **Right**: "Written in 1848, during the year of European revolutions, this document reflects the widespread revolutionary fervor that made reform arguments politically viable."

#### Error 5: Using Too Few Documents
Many students use only 4 documents (the minimum for evidence credit) and source only 2 (the minimum for the sourcing point). Chief Readers explicitly warn against this strategy:

> Students who used all seven documents and attempted sourcing of four or more documents had a much better chance of earning rubric points, including the complexity point.

#### Error 6: Over-Reliance on Quotations
Students quote extensively from documents rather than paraphrasing and analyzing. The Chief Reader for AP US History noted this as a persistent pattern across exam years.

#### Error 7: Content Knowledge Errors in Sourcing
When students attempt historical situation analysis, they sometimes bring in incorrect historical knowledge. For example, the 2024 AP World Chief Reader noted students mistakenly attributing Mongol practices to "gunpowder" by confusing the Mongol Empire with later gunpowder empires like the Ottomans.

### Chief Reader Recommendations for Teachers

1. **Do not teach strategies that "game" the rubric.** Teachers should avoid endorsing minimum-effort approaches.
2. **Make primary source work a year-long priority.** Students need regular, sustained practice with primary sources throughout the course, not just before the exam.
3. **Provide rubric-targeted feedback.** Written assignments should be annotated to show students exactly where sourcing skills are demonstrated or missing.
4. **Use a tiered teaching approach.** Start with individual skills (sourcing, evidence, thesis) before combining them into full DBQ responses.
5. **Practice sourcing frequently.** Students should practice sourcing documents in short exercises before attempting it within full essays.

## 10. Effective Scaffolding Strategies

### The Stair-Step Approach

Experienced AP teachers recommend building DBQ skills incrementally rather than assigning full essays from the start:

1. **Phase 1 (Early in course)**: Focus on reading and comprehending primary sources. Start with images before texts — visual sources are less intimidating and help students realize that anything contemporaneous to an era can be a primary source.

2. **Phase 2**: Add thesis writing and document evidence (using documents to support claims).

3. **Phase 3**: Add contextualization (broader historical background for the prompt).

4. **Phase 4**: Add sourcing (HIPP analysis). By this point, students are comfortable with the documents and can layer analytical skills on top of comprehension.

5. **Phase 5**: Add complexity and full timed writing.

### The Think-Aloud Method

Both SHEG and the DBQ Project emphasize **teacher modeling through think-alouds**. The teacher reads a document aloud while verbalizing their sourcing thought process:

"I notice this was written by a British factory owner in 1832. Before I even read the content, I'm thinking: what is a factory owner's interest in this debate? He probably wants to defend the factory system. His perspective is shaped by his economic stake. So when I read his claims that conditions are good, I need to consider that his PURPOSE might be to deflect criticism rather than to describe reality accurately..."

This explicit modeling of the invisible cognitive process is critical because students cannot observe analytical thinking — it happens silently inside experts' heads.

### Graduated Reading Levels

Start students with shorter, simpler primary source excerpts early in the year and gradually increase complexity:
- Begin with 2-3 sentence excerpts with clear attribution
- Progress to paragraph-length documents
- Build to full-length primary sources matching exam difficulty

The DBQ Project's Enhanced Version / Clean Version system embodies this principle at the lesson level.

### The "Sentence Starters" Strategy

For students who struggle to articulate sourcing analysis, provide sentence frames:

- **Historical Situation**: "Because this document was created during [historical context], the author's [claim/description] reflects [connection to broader trends]..."
- **Intended Audience**: "Since the intended audience is [audience], the author [adjusts/emphasizes/omits] [specific content] because..."
- **Purpose**: "The author created this document in order to [purpose], which is evident in [specific choice] and suggests [connection to argument]..."
- **Point of View**: "As a [identity/role], the author's perspective is shaped by [factor], which explains why they [specific content choice]..."

### Collaborative Analysis

Both SHEG and the DBQ Project emphasize small-group document analysis before individual practice. The pedagogical logic:

1. Students discuss and debate interpretations, which externalizes the analytical process
2. Stronger students model analytical thinking for peers
3. Discussion reveals misconceptions that can be addressed in real time
4. Multiple perspectives on a single document demonstrate that analysis is interpretive, not factual

## 11. Implications for Curriculum Design

### Key Principles from the Research

1. **Sourcing must be explicitly taught, not assumed.** Wineburg's research proves that even bright students do not naturally develop sourcing skills through exposure alone. Direct instruction in the four sourcing heuristics is essential.

2. **Scaffolding should be gradual and multi-leveled.** The DBQ Project's Enhanced/Clean Version system and SHEG's modified documents provide models for differentiating instruction. Start with heavy scaffolding (guided questions, graphic organizers, think-alouds) and systematically remove supports.

3. **The "explain relevance" step needs special attention.** The single most common exam error — identifying without explaining — suggests that instruction must explicitly teach students to complete the analytical loop: Identify --> Explain How/Why --> Connect to Argument.

4. **HAPPY may be more effective than HIPP for instruction** because the "Y" (Why?) step directly addresses the relevance gap. Use HAPPY for teaching; students can drop the Y on the exam once the habit is internalized.

5. **Year-long integration beats exam-prep cramming.** Chief Readers consistently recommend embedding source analysis throughout the entire course, not concentrating it in a pre-exam review unit.

6. **Formative feedback must be rubric-specific.** Rather than holistic essay grades, annotate exactly where students earned or missed sourcing points, with specific guidance on how to improve.

7. **Multiple documents create interpretive tension.** Following SHEG's design principle, present documents that disagree with each other. When sources conflict, students are forced to analyze rather than simply absorb.

8. **Historical empathy enables better sourcing.** Students who can imagine the worldview of a historical author produce better POV and Purpose analysis. Role-playing, perspective-taking exercises, and explicit anti-presentism instruction all support sourcing skill development.

9. **The progression should be: Comprehend --> Contextualize --> Source --> Corroborate --> Argue.** These are not parallel skills but a developmental sequence. Students must comprehend a document before they can source it, and they must source individual documents before they can corroborate across documents.

10. **Sourcing is transferable.** Wineburg frames historical thinking as preparation for citizenship, not just exam performance. Students who learn to evaluate sources historically are better equipped to evaluate information in general — a critical skill in an era of information overload.
