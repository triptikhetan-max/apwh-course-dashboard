## 3. Fiveable: HIPP Analysis Submissions & Feedback (Written Resource)

- **URL:** https://fiveable.me/ap-world/exam-skills/hipp-analysis-submissions-and-feedback/blog/7utzWtS4wvIm66qzBhG3
- **Format:** Blog post with student work samples and teacher feedback

### Content Structure
Three student submissions analyzing Early Modern Period documents (c. 1450-1750):
1. **Document set:** Martin Luther's reformation critiques, Ottoman-Safavid conflict letter, Louis XIV portrait
2. **Submission 1:** Full HIPP treatment of all three documents (thorough but over-analysis for exam conditions)
3. **Submission 2:** Focus on Historical Context and POV, less developed on other categories
4. **Submission 3:** Targeted single-category approach per document (exam-appropriate brevity)

### HIPP Element Definitions Used
- **H (Historical Context):** "What was going on at this time period?"
- **I (Intended Audience):** "Who/whom is the document meant for?"
- **P (POV):** "Who/what is the document representing?"
- **P (Purpose):** "Why is the document created?"

### Teacher Feedback Themes
- Key insight: Students should analyze only ONE HIPP category per document on the actual exam
- Submission 1 praised for depth but flagged as over-analysis for timed conditions
- Submission 3 praised for exam-appropriate brevity with focused single-category analysis
- Recurring advice: Connect sourcing directly to argument, not standalone analysis

### Usefulness for Teaching
- **Good for:** Showing students the spectrum from over-analysis to exam-appropriate analysis
- **Good for:** Exemplifying what "good enough" looks like vs. what thoroughness looks like
- **Shows good vs. bad analysis:** Yes, implicitly through the three submissions at different depth levels
- **Limitation:** Written resource, not video; no element-by-element breakdown

## 4. Fiveable: HIPP/HAPPY Practice & Feedback (Written Resource)

- **URL:** https://fiveable.me/ap-world/exam-skills/document-analysis-hipp-happy-practice/blog/Y6nW2oJMH7iXgK0dF7aA
- **Format:** Blog post with student work samples and teacher feedback

### Content Structure
Five student sample responses analyzing four documents from c. 1750-1900:
1. **John Locke** (Enlightenment natural rights)
2. **Mary Wollstonecraft** (women's rights advocacy)
3. **French Revolutionary painting** (Third Estate/Napoleon era)
4. **Friedrich Engels** (Industrial Revolution working conditions)

### Feedback Patterns by Element
| Element | Feedback Quality | Key Teaching Point |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Historical Context | Strong praise for accurate periodization | Connect documents to specific contemporary events |
| Purpose | Repeated correction | Purpose requires "an objective or desired action" -- not merely informing/describing |
| Point of View | Good | Identify author perspective and connect to argument |
| Intended Audience | Less explicitly critiqued | Identifying specific target groups earns approval |

### Key Teaching Takeaway
- Purpose is the element students most commonly get wrong
- The most frequent error: stating purpose as "to inform" or "to show" (too vague, could apply to any document)
- Correct approach: identify what the author wants to CAUSE or CHANGE

## Summary Assessment: Can These Be Used as Per-Element Teaching Segments?

### Best Candidates for Per-Element HIPP Teaching

| Rank | Resource | Per-Element? | Worked Examples? | Good vs. Bad? | Timestampable? |
|------|----------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | **Marco Learning (v5AJxsSiEJA)** | Yes (defines each separately) | Yes (WWII poster) | Partially | Needs viewing to confirm |

| 4 | **Fiveable: Intro to Sourcing** | Likely (covers HIPP) | Unknown | Unknown | Cannot access video |
| 5 | **Heimler's History** | No (holistic DBQ) | Yes | No | Not for HIPP specifically |

### Key Findings

1. **No single video found that teaches each HIPP element with substantial depth (5+ min per element) using multiple worked examples and showing good vs. bad analysis.** This is a gap in available resources.

2. **Marco Learning's video (v5AJxsSiEJA) is the strongest candidate** -- it covers each element with a through-line example. However, it needs to be watched to confirm whether segments are long enough and clearly delineated for per-element use.

3. **Fiveable's written resources are excellent for student-facing examples** showing what good vs. bad HIPP analysis looks like. The HIPP/HAPPY Practice page in particular has the strongest teacher feedback distinguishing quality levels for each element.

4. **The Fiveable videos are inaccessible for analysis** -- they are embedded behind Fiveable's platform and do not appear to be on YouTube directly. This limits their utility for timestamping and per-element module creation.

5. **Purpose is the element most poorly understood by students** across all resources. Every feedback source flags "to inform" or "to show" as the most common error. Any per-element module on Purpose needs to address this directly.

6. **A custom video may be needed** to achieve the per-element depth desired. Existing resources either cover HIPP as one segment within a broader DBQ strategy video, or provide written practice without video instruction.

### Recommended Next Steps

1. **Watch Marco Learning video (v5AJxsSiEJA)** to timestamp segments and assess per-element depth
2. **Consider creating custom per-element video modules** that use the Fiveable student work samples as worked examples of good vs. bad analysis
3. **For Purpose specifically:** Build a module around the common "to inform" error with multiple corrected examples
4. **Combine resources:** Use Marco Learning video for element definitions + Fiveable written samples for practice + CMHS writing stems for scaffolding
