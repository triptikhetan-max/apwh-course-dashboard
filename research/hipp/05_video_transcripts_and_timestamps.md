## 11. Heimler's HAPP/HIPP Framework (Consolidated from All Sources)

### What Heimler Teaches About Sourcing

From Heimler's worksheets, videos, study guides, and related teacher resources, here is his consolidated HAPP framework:

#### The HAPP Acronym (Heimler's version)

| Letter | Element | What It Means | Heimler's Sentence Stem |
|--------|---------|---------------|------------------------|
| **H** | Historical Context | Events or developments that influenced the creation of this document | "The events in this document are situated in the larger context of..." |
| **A** | Audience | Who the author intended to reach with this document | "The audience of this document was X and that suggests..." |
| **P** | Purpose | Why the author created this document | "The purpose of this document was to X, which is significant because..." |
| **P** | Point of View | The author's perspective, biases, or position | "The author's point of view is X, which suggests..." |

#### Heimler's Key Teaching Points About Sourcing

1. **HAPP is a simplified version of ACAPS** (Author, Context, Audience, Purpose, Significance)
2. **You only need ONE HAPP element per document** to earn sourcing credit
3. **You need to source at least 2 documents** (minimum for the rubric) but 3+ is safer
4. **Sourcing is NOT just naming the source** -- you must explain WHY the sourcing element matters for your interpretation
5. **The "and that suggests" / "and that's important because" clause is essential** -- this is what turns naming a sourcing element into actual analysis

#### Heimler's DBQ Planning Worksheet Structure

From the widely-used Heimler DBQ worksheet (available on multiple school sites):

| Column | Content |
|--------|---------|
| Document # | Doc 1-7 |
| Main Idea | Brief summary of what the document says |
| Outside Evidence (1x) | One piece of evidence not in the documents |
| HAPP/HIPP (3x) | Three sourcing attempts across documents |

**Thesis Formula:** "Although X, because A & B, therefore Y"
- X = counter-argument
- A & B = specific historical evidence (categories)
- Y = your argument

**Contextualization Section:** "Name two or three vocab words associated with the theme of the prompt. Name them, explain what they mean, and connect them to your argument."

## 12. Teaching Approach Analysis & Module Mapping

### Comparative Analysis: How Each Source Teaches Sourcing

| Dimension | Heimler (Shorts) | Heimler (Long-form) | Fiveable | Khan Academy |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|----------|--------------|
| **Time spent on sourcing** | 52-58 sec | 2-5 min within larger video | Full video dedicated | Part of broader series |
| **Framework name** | HAPP | HAPP (sometimes HIPP) | HIPP | "Historical thinking" / "Source identification" |
| **Worked examples** | 0-1 | 1-2 hypothetical | Likely real docs | Real primary sources |
| **Sentence stems** | Minimal | YES -- explicit stems | Likely | No -- teaches thinking process |
| **Good vs bad examples** | No | Rarely | Unknown | Unknown |
| **Scaffolding** | Low (just the tip) | HIGH (step-by-step formula) | Medium (framework + demo) | Medium (concept + practice) |
| **Tone** | Casual, funny | Casual, funny, relatable | Academic but friendly | Academic, methodical |
| **Student engagement** | Very high (millions of views) | Very high (300K-650K views) | Moderate | Moderate |

### Key Findings for Our Module Design

1. **Heimler's most popular approach is the FORMULA** -- students want step-by-step, not theory
2. **Sourcing is consistently taught as an ADD-ON**, never as the primary skill -- our module can differentiate by making it central
3. **Sentence stems are the most practical tool** -- Heimler's stems ("The audience of this document was X and that suggests...") are directly usable
4. **The "and that suggests/because" clause is the key skill** -- identifying HAPP elements is easy; explaining WHY they matter is where students struggle
5. **No video source teaches HIPP with multiple worked examples** -- this is our biggest opportunity
6. **Students need to see the DIFFERENCE between naming a source element and analyzing it** -- good vs. bad examples are missing from all sources
7. **The original "How to Source (HAPP) Documents for a DBQ" video is now private** -- Heimler has moved his most targeted sourcing content behind the paywall, leaving only Shorts and brief segments in longer videos

### Recommended Module Structure Based on Video Analysis

Based on how these videos teach (and don't teach) sourcing:

| Module Section | What We Should Do | What Heimler Does | Our Advantage |
|----------------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Hook** | Quick "sourcing is confusing" acknowledgment + reframe | Same approach in his Shorts | Match his energy but go deeper |
| **Framework Intro** | Introduce HIPP with clear definitions + visual | Brief mention, relies on worksheet | More structured, visual framework |
| **H -- Historical Context** | 2-3 worked examples with real AP docs, model the thinking | One sentence stem | Multiple examples, think-aloud |
| **I -- Intended Audience** | 2-3 worked examples, show how audience changes meaning | One sentence stem | Show WHY audience matters for interpretation |
| **P -- Purpose** | 2-3 worked examples, distinguish purpose from main idea | One sentence stem | Common mistake: confusing purpose with topic |
| **P -- Point of View** | 2-3 worked examples, show bias/perspective impact | One sentence stem | Hardest element -- needs most scaffolding |
| **Good vs Bad** | Side-by-side examples of weak vs strong sourcing | Not done | Unique differentiator |
| **Practice** | Interactive practice with feedback | Homework/worksheet | Immediate feedback loop |
| **Integration** | Show how sourcing fits into full DBQ essay | His main focus | Bridge from skill to application |

### Sentence Stems to Adopt/Adapt from Heimler

**For Historical Context:**
- "The events in this document are situated in the larger context of [specific historical development], which is important because [connection to document's meaning]."
- "This document was created during a time when [specific context], which helps explain why [interpretation]."

**For Audience:**
- "The audience of this document was [specific group], and that suggests [what the audience tells us about the document's purpose or reliability]."
- "Because this was written for [audience], the author likely [adjusted their message/emphasized certain points]."

**For Purpose:**
- "The purpose of this document was to [specific purpose], which is significant because [what purpose reveals about the author's intent or the document's reliability]."
- "The author wrote this in order to [purpose], which means [interpretation]."

**For Point of View:**
- "The author's point of view as a [identity/role] suggests that [how their position shapes what they say]."
- "As a [identity], the author would have [particular perspective], which explains why [they emphasize/omit certain things]."
