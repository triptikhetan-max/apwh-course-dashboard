## 1. Exam Structure & Scoring Overview

### FRQ Section Breakdown

| Component | Count | Time | % of Score | Points |
|-----------|-------|------|------------|--------|
| SAQ (Short Answer) | 3 questions (Q1-Q2 required, choose Q3 or Q4) | 40 min | 20% | 3 pts each (9 total) |
| DBQ (Document-Based) | 1 question, 7 documents | 60 min (15 min reading) | 25% | 7 pts |
| LEQ (Long Essay) | Choose 1 of 3 options | 40 min | 15% | 6 pts |

### SAQ Structure
- **Q1**: Required, secondary source stimulus, covers Units 3-8
- **Q2**: Required, primary source stimulus, covers Units 3-8
- **Q3**: Choice option, NO stimulus, covers c. 1200-1750 (Units 1-3)
- **Q4**: Choice option, NO stimulus, covers c. 1750-2001 (Units 6-9)

### LEQ Structure
- **Q2**: Covers c. 1200-1750
- **Q3**: Covers c. 1450-1900
- **Q4**: Covers c. 1750-2001
- All three test the SAME reasoning skill (comparison, causation, or CCOT)

### DBQ Time Period
- Always covers c. 1450-2001

## 2. Released FRQ Catalog by Year

> **Note on available years**: College Board publicly releases only 3 years of FRQs at a time. Starting 2023, they release two sets per year ("Set 1" and "Set 2"). Pre-2020 exams used a different course framework (all of world history, not just "Modern" from 1200 CE). The 2017-2019 exams are still useful for practice where the content overlaps with the Modern course. There was no 2020 released exam due to COVID format changes.

### Key Insight
China appears more frequently than any other single non-European civilization. The 2024 DBQ combining China AND Russia/Soviet Union is a landmark question — it's the only released DBQ that explicitly asks students to address communist societies as the primary focus. Both civilizations are tested more as examples within broader thematic questions than as standalone deep-dive subjects.

## 5. College Board Prompt Style Patterns

### Prompt Types Observed

**1. "Evaluate the extent to which..." (MOST COMMON)**
- Used in virtually ALL DBQs and many LEQs
- Requires students to take a position on degree/extent
- Examples: "Evaluate the extent to which the Portuguese transformed maritime trade..." / "Evaluate the extent to which economic factors led to..."

**2. "Develop an argument that evaluates..." (LEQ standard phrasing)**
- Standard LEQ format
- Always paired with a historical reasoning skill

**3. Stimulus-based (SAQ only)**
- SAQ 1: Always a secondary source (historian's argument)
- SAQ 2: Always a primary source (image, document, map)

### Prompt Style Patterns

| Pattern | Frequency | Description |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Single-civilization deep dive** | MODERATE | 2023 S1 DBQ (Qing China), 2021 DBQ (Mexico) |
| **Comparative / multi-civilization** | HIGH | 2024 S1 DBQ (Soviet AND/OR China), 2022 DBQ (Africa AND/OR Asia) |
| **Thematic across regions** | HIGH | 2018 DBQ (railroads in Afro-Eurasia), 2025 S2 DBQ (women globally) |
| **Causation ("extent to which X caused Y")** | VERY HIGH | Most DBQs use this framework |
| **Continuity and Change** | MODERATE | Several LEQs, some SAQs |
| **Comparison** | MODERATE | LEQs and SAQs |

### Key Style Observations

1. **College Board STRONGLY favors "evaluate the extent" framing** — this forces students to qualify their arguments rather than make absolute claims.

2. **DBQs tend to be thematic rather than civilization-specific** — even the "Qing collapse" DBQ requires contextualization within broader imperialism themes. The "and/or" construction (Africa and/or Asia) is common.

3. **The complexity point rewards cross-regional and cross-temporal connections** — students who compare the Qing to the Ottoman decline, or connect Mexican Revolution to Russian Revolution, score higher.

4. **SAQs are the only place where straightforward factual recall is tested** — no thesis required, just identify/explain/describe.

5. **Women/gender has emerged as a major testing theme since 2021** — appearing in SAQs, DBQs, and LEQs across multiple years.

6. **The "and/or" construction gives students flexibility** — "Soviet AND/OR Chinese" means either focus is acceptable.

## 8. 2024 Released DBQ: Chinese Communism — Deep Dive

### Prompt
"Evaluate the extent to which communist rule transformed Soviet and/or Chinese societies in the period circa 1930-1990."

### Key Details
- **7 documents** offering various perspectives on communist rule in Soviet Union and/or China
- **Time period**: 1930-1990 (spanning Stalin to late Cold War)
- **"And/or" construction**: Students could focus on either country or both
- **CED alignment**: Unit 8, Topics 8.3, 8.4

### Scoring Guidelines Expectations

**Thesis**: Must take a position on the EXTENT of transformation — not just "communism transformed society" but HOW MUCH and in what ways

**Contextualization examples**:
- Russian Revolution of 1917 and Bolshevik takeover
- Chinese Civil War and Mao's rise to power
- Marxist-Leninist ideology
- Pre-existing social structures (Russian feudalism, Chinese Confucian hierarchy)

**Outside evidence examples** (from scoring guidelines):
- Stalin's purges and show trials
- Gulags in Siberia
- China's Cultural Revolution
- "Struggle sessions"
- Trial of the Gang of Four
- Five-Year Plans (both countries)
- Collectivization of agriculture
- Great Leap Forward
- Women's roles under communism
- Education reforms
- Religious persecution

**Complexity approaches**:
- Compare Soviet and Chinese approaches to communism
- Discuss how transformation was uneven (urban vs. rural, men vs. women)
- Acknowledge both intended and unintended consequences
- Connect to broader Cold War dynamics

### Why This DBQ Matters for Course Building
This is the most direct test of Unit 8 content in any released DBQ. It requires deep knowledge of both Soviet and Chinese communist history — topics that students often find challenging. This should be a centerpiece FRQ for Unit 8 practice.

## 9. Scoring Guidelines Summary

### DBQ Rubric (7 points)

| Point | Category | What College Board Expects |
|-------|----------|---------------------------|
| 1 | **Thesis** | Historically defensible claim that establishes a line of reasoning; located in intro or conclusion |
| 1 | **Contextualization** | Describe broader historical context BEFORE the argument; not just restating the prompt |
| 1-2 | **Evidence (Documents)** | Use 3 docs (1 pt) or 6+ docs (2 pts) to SUPPORT the argument — not just list/quote |
| 1 | **Evidence (Beyond Docs)** | At least 1 specific historical fact not in the documents |
| 1 | **Sourcing** | For 3+ docs, explain HOW/WHY the author's POV, purpose, situation, or audience matters |
| 1 | **Complexity** | Demonstrate nuanced understanding — qualify argument, compare across regions/periods, explain multiple variables |

### LEQ Rubric (6 points)

| Point | Category | What College Board Expects |
|-------|----------|---------------------------|
| 1 | **Thesis** | Historically defensible claim with line of reasoning |
| 1 | **Contextualization** | Broader context relevant to the prompt |
| 1-2 | **Evidence** | Provide specific evidence (1 pt) that SUPPORTS the argument (2 pts) |
| 1 | **Analysis & Reasoning** | Use historical reasoning skill (comparison, causation, CCOT) to frame argument |
| 1 | **Complexity** | Nuanced understanding |

### SAQ Rubric (3 points — 1 per part)
- Each part (a, b, c) scored independently
- Must be specific and historically accurate
- No thesis required — just answer, cite, explain

### Critical Insight for Course Building
The **complexity point** is the hardest to earn on both DBQ and LEQ. College Board data consistently shows only 5-15% of students earn it. Teaching students to make cross-regional comparisons, qualify arguments, and explain multiple variables is essential. Released FRQs with scoring commentary are the best tool for this.

### Prompt Style Recommendations for Practice
1. Always use "evaluate the extent to which" framing for DBQ/LEQ practice
2. Include "and/or" options when possible to teach flexibility
3. Emphasize the complexity point in every FRQ practice session
4. Use stimulus-based SAQ practice with both primary and secondary sources
5. Train students on sourcing (POV, purpose, historical situation, audience)
